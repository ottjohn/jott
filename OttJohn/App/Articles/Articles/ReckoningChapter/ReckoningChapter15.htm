Chapter 15

 

 

Tobias lay in bed staring at the alarm clock, the display burning the time into his eyes, branding them with its garish yellow color. The rest of the room was almost pitch dark, and if he looked away, he could see the clock's afterimage each time he blinked. He closed his eyes, tried to will himself to sleep. The time 2:31 hung in the darkness behind his closed lids, darting left and right as he moved his eyes, finally floating away and dissipating after several minutes. He let out a deep, heavy sigh, then spent the next few minutes attempting to identify the first forty prime numbers. It was an exercise he'd used many times in the past when he couldn't get to sleep. Typically, under normal circumstances, he'd be out by the time he got to 111. The math involved in ensuring the number was prime took longer as the numbers got larger, so by the time he'd reached the hundreds his mind would be exhausted. Not this time. He gave up at 137. He wasn't going to get any sleep because something was nagging him. A persistent thought kept breaking into his stream of consciousness like an air raid siren splinters a peaceful silence.

And it wasn't really a thought. It was more like a gut feeling, a powerful intuitive sense he had about the Hassenback case. He'd had gut feelings before. Lots of times. When he had them during the day, he could easily do something about them. He could wrap them into his workday and act on them. When he had them at night, however, he invariably lost sleep. Tonight's gut feeling told him if he put off the work required to find Eduardo even a minute longer, he'd be too late. Eduardo would be dead, and his suspect would be gone. Sometimes he acted on the feelings; sometimes he didn't. When he did, he found them to be correct about fifty percent of the time.

"What's wrong, sweetie?" Carol rolled toward her husband, placing her left elbow beneath her for support. "You haven't slept a wink all night."

"I've slept some." Tobias protested, looking again at the clock, facing away from Carol.

"Right. You've been laying over there sighing like a little kid who wants a toy but can't have it."

"Sorry." Tobias rolled over, stroked Carol's hair with his left hand.

"It's okay. Unlike you, I don't have to work tomorrow if I don't want to. I can just stay in bed and sleep until noon after you've gone." She smiled.

Tobias flopped over on his back, stared up at the ceiling into the darkness. His eyes adjusted, leveraged the meager light coming from the alarm clock, which allowed him to just make out the ceiling fan above him.

"Yeah, I know. But I ruined our evening working, and now it's ruined my sleep and yours. I guess I still need to learn when to put my problems away."

"You thinking about what you were working on earlier?"

"Yeah." The word came out throaty, like it had been dug out from the very pit of his stomach.

"I know how you get, Toby Bear." She teased, labeling him with the moniker she created for him in college. "You know you're not going to be able to sleep all night. Just go do what you have to do. I don't mind. You'll just owe me one."

She rubbed his chest. He propped up onto his elbows, leaned over and gave his wife a tender kiss on the lips. He knew he was the fortunate one in their relationship. He figured she knew it too.

"I'll take you out for a steak dinner later this week, okay?" He said.

"That would be great, honey, but you know I don't eat steak."

"Oh yeah. I keep getting your tastes mixed up with my girlfriend's. How about fish?"

"That's better. Now go do your work and let me sleep."

Tobias gave his wife another kiss, then got out of bed and headed for the kitchen.

 

Tobias walked into his kitchen at 3:10 in the morning. He made some coffee, then rummaged through the refrigerator looking for leftovers that might quiet his rumbling stomach. Finding nothing, he contented himself with a couple of peanut butter and jelly sandwiches. He resumed his place at the kitchen table he'd occupied only hours before, picked up his cell phone, and made the first of three calls.

The first was to the night team in the Computer Forensics Lab at LAPD. He didn't call the LAPD lab because he had doubts about his department's ability to work computer forensics. He called because he knew that, save for a couple of patrols units and a night clerk, his department was empty. After three rings and a request to speak to the duty officer, a Lieutenant Dursk picked up the phone.

"Dursk. Help you?" The voice was gravelly, sounded like it belonged to a guy in his mid-fifties, a chain smoker.

"Good morning, Lieutenant Dursk. I'm Detective Tobias with the Pasadena PD. Was wondering if you could help me with something."

"Depends on what it is," Dursk replied, flatly.

"I'm on a fresh case right now. Has to do with a dead sex offender, name's Hassenback. He was in possession of a URL for a website that pedals child pornography. There may be a local connection with regard to the site, as in local sex trade. There's a time issue here, and if we could get some particulars ..."

"Local?" The gravelly voice sounded incredulous.

"Yeah, local."

"Give me the particulars. I'll make sure we get right on it."

Tobias rattled off the IP addresses for the website and its associated ISP. He knew he didn't need to, but he gave Dursk the URL for the ISP as well and let the him know the contact address was located somewhere in the Czech Repulic.

"Sounds like you've been doing a little digging," said Dursk.

"Yeah, a little. Figured if you guys could help me, I could do a little grunt work for you."

"Czech Republic, huh?" Dursk let it hang in the air, weighing on the line like a massive turkey vulture, bending it almost to the ground.

"Yeah, I know. Probably not a snowball's chance in hell of getting any information out of them at all. I'm sure it's worth a couple of phone calls and maybe some well placed threats, though." Tobias tried to sound hopeful.

"Anything's possible." Replied Dursk, tacitly confirming the probabilities were very near zero.

Tobias sighed, then said, "At any rate, I really appreciate you looking into this with such short notice."

"Not a problem, Detective. When it comes to this kind of shit, you can wake me up in the middle of the day, and I'll work on it. There's no place, you know what I mean?"

Tobias liked the sound coming from the man's voice, took it to mean he was going to give it an honest effort, no matter how badly the odds were stacked against him.

"You got that right. With any luck, I'll have more for you later in the morning."

"Yeah, what's that?" Dursk's inflection implied he was hoping for something that might not lead to a dead end.

"I may have a local ISP for you. Need it to find an email address of a potential witness, another sex offender. The dead guy exchanged emails with him. If I can get you the ISP, you think you can get me the address?"

"Now that's something we can more than likely do. I'd almost guarantee it."

"Great. How long you going to be at the office?"

"Barring a site visit, I should be here until around ten in the morning."

"Excellent. I'll try to get you the name of that ISP before then."

Tobias hung up the phone. The second call he made was to the answering service for the Pasadena Superior Court. He simply asked the woman on the other end of the line to make an appointment for him with the first-available magistrate in the morning. She put him on hold, got back on the line about three minutes later, and told him he could see a magistrate by the name of Robert Micheals at 7:30. She then told him Michaels' office was on the first floor, room 128, and that she had blocked out a half hour of his time. Tobias thanked her, hung up the phone, hoping he'd be able to make the meeting without wasting the guy's time.

The third call was to Sanchez. He felt bad about asking, but he needed the help. Turning a fairly well-kept, organized house was one thing. Turning one that had already been ransacked and then subject to a police investigation was altogether different. He figured by himself he might be there all morning. If he had Sanchez's help, he might actually be able to make his appointment with the magistrate. Tobias dialed. After five rings, Sanchez answered.

"Yeah, Sanchez." His voice came across slurred, the words delivered from a tongue deadened by a deep sleep.

"Hey, kid. Tobias."

There was a rustling noise on the other end of the line, then the sharp exhale, which created a sound not unlike that made by a person thrown into a pool of ice-cold water. Tobias felt a little wave of guilt flush over him, knew what was coming next, attempted to offer reassurance, but Sanchez beat him to the line, panic in his voice.

"Tobias? Is everything okay?"

"Yeah, everything's fine. Relax."

"What happened? Is Carol all right?"

"Yeah, I ..."

Sanchez clipped Tobias's response again, the early hour tying him up in panic mode.

"Ok. Just tell me there wasn't an accident, a heart attack, an illness, or a death. Just tell me that."

Tobias could hear Sanchez breath out deeply, get himself under control. He allowed himself a slight, silent chuckle. He didn't find any gratification in waking anyone from a sound sleep at 3:30 in the morning. Generally, when calls come in at that time, they're nothing but bad news. He should've predicted the reaction, phrased his greeting differently. Hindsight is twenty-twenty, right? While he did feel guilty about the call, he was genuinely touched by the concern. He'd never let on about it, at least not explicitly, but he thought of Sanchez as the son he never had.

"No, nothing like that, kid." Tobias let his chuckle get a little louder, carry across the line, let Sanchez know everything was definitely OK.

Sanchez blew out an audible sigh of relief, said, "Then what the hell is going on, boss? It's 3:30 in the freaking morning! You looking to go play pool and troll for the ladies or something? 'Cause if you are, we might be able to catch some hotties just leaving the clubs now."

"Sounds tempting, but I need your help."

"With what?" Sanchez asked, a quizzical tone in his voice.

"With the Hassenback house. You know how it was turned by our perp?"

"Yeah."

"Well, we need to turn it again."

Tobias briefed Sanchez about the details he'd hammered out the night before. Was it really just last night? After five minute's conversation, they agreed to meet at the Hassenback residence at 4:30 in the morning, allowing for a 30 minute commute time and a coffee run.

Tobias cradled the phone, ending his last call of the morning. He quickly returned to the bedroom and kissed Carol on the forehead and said goodbye. She turned away, mumbled something about cats tracking through pancake batter. He stood there for a minute, watching her sleep. He had a good wife, better than he deserved. He could say the same thing about Sanchez. Better partner than he deserved. Both of them had done so much for him over the time he had known them, never asking for anything in return. He thought briefly about inviting Sanchez to the dinner he promised Carol. Perhaps that would make up for the careless early morning call. Then he thought about what his wife was wearing when he'd arrived home the night before, thought about her come-hither look. He quickly reconsidered, squashed the idea. He'd take the kid out for a burger.

The first order of business when conducting an interrogation is to make the subject uncomfortable. Not so much as to inhibit him from answering questions, but just enough to make him understand he's not in control, just enough to make him want to be forthright and motivated to get the questioning over with as quickly as possible. Clark sat on the edge of Eduardo's ugly orange couch, balancing his computer on his lap, scrolling through a document template to get to the first of his pre-interview questions. He peered over the screen and looked at Eduardo, who was sitting cross-legged on the floor in front of him, his long thin face now haggard and worn, his eyes showing fear and a little panic.

He felt good about his position, felt he'd established an appropriate level of dominance. While he was certainly on Eduardo's home turf,  he felt the earlier take-down and Eduardo's current position on the floor had done more than enough to make the man uncomfortable, perhaps too much so. In spite of his perceived level of control, though, he felt an unaccustomed need to hurry, to move quickly through the interrogation and get out. He didn't know if the feeling came from a lack of planning, or if it came from an intuitive sense that the police were closing in on him. Whatever the origin, he knew it didn't matter. He'd follow protocol and continue with this interview as he had all of his previous ones. His first few questions wouldn't be asked to collect pertinent information, though; rather, they'd be carefully crafted and posed to help him set the tone for the rest of the interview.

This leads to the second order of business when conducting an interrogation: maximizing the subject's psychological comfort under the distress brought about by the first rule. The best way to do that is to make the subject believe he shares some common point of interest with the interrogator. It lubricates the subject's tongue, gets him to talk. And once he starts rattling off information, even information of no consequence, he'll find it difficult to stop under any circumstance. And that's where Clark needed to be. He took another quick look around the room, trying to find something worthy of conversation. He'd occupied some fairly nasty dumps over the past couple of years, but Eduardo's place literally merited a mention in the dictionary next to the word squalid. Almost every fixture in the place was either second-hand or employed in a non-traditional way to serve some traditional purpose: milk crates were fashioned to serve as coffee and end tables, the light fixture next to the couch had no shade and was probably older than both of them, an old blue sheet was fashioned into a barely passable curtain for the living room window. The couch on which he was sitting was by far the best example he'd ever seen of a hand-me-down that should've been left for dead years ago. Then he glanced over at Eduardo's computer. It was sitting precariously on a rickety old desk, gleaming like a diamond in the rough.

"Nice computer," he said, playing the delivery as if he were disinterested in the task at hand, instead wanting to talk about something else. "Where'd you get it?"

Eduardo looked confused by the question, but he answered anyway.

"Best Buy. They had a sale." He tried to force a smile, couldn't pull it off.

"I really like the monitor. Thing's gigantic. What's the resolution?"

Eduardo perked up just a little. "Yeah, that's the best monitor they had. Cost a lot too, about a grand. But it was worth it. Has a resolution of 1600 by 900."

Clark let out a low whistle. "No shit? I need to get one of those. How's the video quality on it?"

"Bad ass." He paused for a moment, as if searching for the right words. "The computer will play regular and blue ray. Blue ray kicks ass on that screen. I spend a lot of time on that thing. Might as well have the best, right?" He let out a nervous chuckle.

"Definitely. So how fast is it?"

Eduardo grinned with a little pride, as if he were talking about a successful member of his family. "Two processors. 2.8 gigahertz each. One terabyte hard drive. Six gigs of RAM. It's a sweet machine." He practically cooed.

Clark returned his attention to the computer, shaking his head in disbelief. Not at the power of the machine, but at how quickly he'd gotten Eduardo to a mental comfort zone. But then again, how surprising was it, really? Most pedophiles are going to spend a lot of money on computer equipment. It's how they get to their stash; it's how they store their stash; it's the center of their universe. He typed an annotation into his laptop:

Answering matter-of-fact questions: pantomimes with hands very little. Sits almost   perfectly still. Eyes focused and forward. Seems more relaxed. Worry lines on face all but gone.

 

With respect to their initial, short conversation, Eduardo's body posturing had given Clark a point-of-reference, or a baseline, for what he should expect when asking him straight-forward questions, ones that required little or no thinking to answer. Most of the questions he'd ask Eduardo would fall into that category. But that was only half of the equation, only half of the information Clark required to determine whether Eduardo was being forthright with his answers. To better focus the blurred chasm between truth and fiction, Clark had to also ask Eduardo questions that required him to think creatively, so he could better understand the man's outward pantomimes when fabricating. Clark raised a fist to his mouth, stifled an affected yawn,  then gave Eduardo a sleepy smile.

"Sounds like you're pretty good with computers."

"I'm pretty good, I guess." A slight hint of wariness returned to the man's eyes. Clark sensed the tension creep, raised his right hand in front of him, palm out, and said,

"Oh, don't worry. We're not starting yet. I just have an unrelated question for you." He paused. "I'm thinking about going back to school next year. Trying to get away from this shit and get into a more nine-to-five routine, you know what I mean?"

"Yeah, I guess so." Eduardo looked a little less tense, apparently happy the conversation wasn't turning serious yet.

"What kind of computer would you recommend? You know, for going back to school?"

"Why don't you just use that thing?" Eduardo motioned to the laptop perched on Clark's knees.

Clark caught himself, shut down the look of surprise before it ever reached his face. Why would he need advice about purchasing a computer for school when he had a perfectly good computer sitting on his lap? It took him a split second to give a reason: "This thing?" He pointed to his laptop with his right index finger. "This is the department's equipment. Using it for anything other than police business is strictly forbidden."

"Oh, okay." Eduardo furrowed his brows, rubbed his chin with his left hand, then said, "What's your preference, desktop or laptop?"

"You know, I've thought about that, and I've kind of gotten used to the convenience of a laptop, so I'd like to stay with one of those."

"Gonna use it for anything else?"

"Nope. Just school."

"Let's see ..." Eduardo tapped the side of his legs with his hands, looked up to the ceiling, and closed his eyes. He stayed in that position for about a minute, then looked at Clark and said, "I think you'd like the Toshiba. You could get something pretty nice for about 700 bucks. It won't be blazing fast, but I bet you could get a 17 inch screen, a fairly large hard drive, and a decent amount of RAM. Oh, and all of them come with DVD players these days, so if you want to watch movies or educational videos, it's handled."

Clark leaned back as if to take in what he'd just heard. "Ok. Let me just make a note of that." He moved as if he were going to type in a note, stopped, looked at Eduardo, and said, "Gonna have to remember to delete this as soon as I'm out of here, though. The guys at the station who manage these things are like the Gestapo." Clark gave Eduardo a knowing smile, then typed in his observation:

Creative thinking: pantomimes with hands much more. Closes eyes when working through a problem. Looks up. Furrows his eyebrows.

 

Clark finished typing the note, noticed Eduardo continued to look relaxed. He wasn't. He still felt a need to hurry. He knew from experience, though, that forcing issues lead to mistakes, and a mistake in this case could lead to disaster. He took a deep, silent breath and let it out, then forced himself to banish the urgent thoughts for a while longer, pushed them into the back of his mind. He'd deal with them later. Right now, he needed to get on with the meat of the interrogation.

"Okay. I guess we need to get to work. That way I can get out of here, and you can get back to what you were doing. Ready?"

Eduardo looked nervous again, but much more comfortable than when he'd first taken the position on the floor in front of him.

"Do I have a choice?"

"No."

"Then I guess I'm as ready as I'm ever gonna be."

 

Clark briefly returned his attention to his computer, considered the core questions he asked during interrogations. They were thorough and tailored to his specific requirements. During each interview, he asked most of them and several others the document did not contain. The supplementary questions didn't betray a deficiency in his level of preparedness, however. Those questions were simply unique to each particular case and by their very nature could not be anticipated. Those he appended to the document as he asked them. He thought of one very important question he'd be adding to Eduardo's document today, perhaps the most important question he'd ever asked in his life: where is the facility that held my daughter? He was impatient for the answer, but he knew he had to ask his normal litany. Just in case something went wrong. Just in case he needed to find another target. He desperately hoped that wouldn't be the case. He looked down at Eduardo, fired off his first question.

"Where have you lived over the past seven years?"

Eduardo shifted his gaze from the floor, looked in a direction just to the right of Clark. "L.A., mostly. But I've stayed in San Antonio, Dallas, and Memphis too."

Clark made an annotation in his computer. Eduardo fidgeted.

"Can I ask you a question?"

Clark looked up. "Sure."

"I guess I don't understand. Why you need to know where I've been? How does that help?"

"Gives us a sampling of offender locations. From that we can look at associated population densities, crime rates against children, and relative populations of the offenders themselves. That, together with arrest rates, periodicity of offenses, and target age groups of the victims gives us a decent picture of which offenders operate alone and which tend to gravitate toward organizations that peddle children in the sex trade."

 Regarding the method under which Clark operated, most of what he told Eduardo was true, just on a very small scale. In fact, in spite of the enormous amount of work he put into his profiling system, he had mostly stumbled onto an attribute formula regarding sex offenders that allowed him to distinguish their two classifications with a great deal of success. He'd tried reading various academic articles concerning the psycho-social profiles of pedophiles. At first, they'd helped, had given him a starting point, but after a time they'd lost their value. And appeal. He didn't like to read them. He found most of them bloated, arrogant, and pedantic to a point that studying them almost gave him dry heaves. Most of his real successes had come from simply plunging into the lives of his targets and interrogating them in a way the social-psychology community simply couldn't. The rest was borne out of a natural skill for finding patterns in data and dumb luck.

Eduardo appeared to consider what Clark had said, then simply nodded his head. "Oh."

"Next question: how do you typically make contact with your victims?"

"Why do you need to know that?" Eduardo learned back, crossed his arms over his chest in a classic defensive posture.

"I just told you. We're looking for patterns in behavior, trying to differentiate profiles between lone hunters and those who work in packs, remember?" Clark was surprised by his impatience, immediately regretted his condescending tone. Eduardo didn't appear too perturbed, simply nodded his head and answered the question.

"Last time I got busted, it was for inappropriate um ... activities with a girl. She was eight. A neighbor's daughter. I befriended the family and worked my way in. That's the way I work. It's sick, I know. But I can't help it. I've done it several times, got busted twice."

Clark annotated the response. Lone hunter. Not exactly what he'd expected. He hadn't looked up any information regarding the man's criminal past, but he'd suspected he would have been busted for some kind of association with trafficking, given his probable association with the facility. He again wondered if Eduardo hadn't lied about his use of it. Perhaps he was just waiting for someone to go in with. Perhaps Hassenback was that person. Maybe not. Perhaps he was over-reacting: profiles were never perfect, and so far it appeared Eduardo clearly marched to the beat of a different drum. Further, the context of his emails had told Clark Eduardo had to have established some kind of relationship with the operation: The place rocks dude. If he hadn't actually been there, why would he indicate otherwise? Clark decided not to burn any more energy on doubts. It was unproductive. Instead, he pushed on.

"Next question: What do you consider your best online resources?"

"I only got three." He looked down at the carpet, blanched. "You sure I'm not gonna get busted for this?"

"Nothing you say to me will put you back in jail."

"Okay. Like I said, I got three I use. One's littlelolita.com, another's youngstuff.com, and the last one has no name. It's just an IP address. I can never remember what it is, so I got it written down."

Clark felt a sudden rush of satisfaction and hope, followed closely by another wave of urgency to end the interrogation quickly. He knew he had more information he needed to gather, much more. But he made a quick decision to focus on information about the site that didn't have a domain name, see where it would lead him. He figured he'd include some questions about the other two sites as well, in the unlikely event they were all related somehow. Clark allowed himself a brief, inward smile. Maybe he could get the information he needed and get the hell out of Eduardo's apartment sooner rather than later.

"Where do you keep the IP address?"

"At my desk. You want me to get it for you?"

"No. Just stay where you are. Tell me where it is. I'll get it."

"But my computer ..."

"Nothing you say and nothing I see is going to lead to your arrest. We need information. Consider it a trade for looking the other way."

"Go to the computer. It's taped under the keyboard."

Clark got up from the couch, placing his laptop on the cushion next to him so the screen faced away from Eduardo. He then walked around its ugly orange edifice, dodging dirty laundry scattered between its backside and the seating area in front of the half wall. He moved to the edge of the desk that supported the computer. He looked at the keyboard. He lifted it. He closed his eyes. He wasn't a religious man, but he found himself asking God to let it be the right IP address, let it be the one piece of evidence that connects Eduardo to the organization that murdered his little girl. He opened his eyes, held the keyboard with a nervous, sweaty grip, then turned it over. A wave of excitement and satisfaction flushed over him. Finally. It was the same IP address he'd gleaned from the phony marketing literature. He held the keyboard in front of him, burned a minute to regain his composure. He then put the keyboard down, returned to his position on the couch, retrieved his laptop, and re-engaged Eduardo.

"You sure these are the only sites you depend on?"

"Yes."

"Ok. Next question. Other than the images, what is it about these sites you find of value?"

Clark anticipated Eduardo would have to think a little about his response and was ready to disregard any outward expression that might indicate creative thinking. To his surprise, however, Eduardo answered immediately.

"Storage capability for my stuff, file sharing, and membership services." He said it like he was reciting the contents of a shopping list. Clark made the requisite annotation in his computer, then followed up with a clarifying interrogative.

"Can you tell me more about the membership services? What do they offer?"

"They have secure, encrypted email and chat and storage. There ain't nothing that's not encrypted. Hell, they practically guarantee you won't get busted ... if you store everything with them. And for sixty bucks a month? It's a pretty good deal."

"Have you used any of the communication services?"

"Yes."

"Can you elaborate?"

"Huh?"

"Can you tell me how these services are used?"

"To trade and share stories. A big draw for some of the members is to submit and rate them. Um ... " Eduardo thought for a moment, then said, "the chat services are used but not a lot. Most of the members are just looking for pics or stories."

"Any message boards?"

"Yeah. But they're never used, really." He thought for a second, as if he was considering something important, and then offered, "Most of what's there requires a code to open them and another to read them. And that costs money."

Two levels of security. And probably an encryption system as well. Pretty safe way to manage communications in an industry that strayed way outside the boundaries of the law. He wondered how the big guns in computer forensics handled that. If what Eduardo was telling him was accurate, he wouldn't have any chance at getting to the items on the boards. Not with the tools at his disposal, anyway. But he'd soon have Eduardo's hard drive, and that might give him an edge, just in case he needed one.

"Next question: have you ever received or sent any of these encrypted messages?"

"Yeah."

"Which web site?"

"The one with the IP address."

"Do all of the sites you visit offer this service?"

"No. Just the one."

Clark felt close, now more confident than ever Eduardo had been to the facility. He wanted to ask him about its location, but he needed another name first. Just in case.

"I'm curious. How'd you become a member of a site with no domain name? It's not like you're going to accidently type in its IP address, right?"

"You have to be invited by a current member. Then you have to actually go the dude's house to ..." Eduardo stopped suddenly, seemed to know where this was going.

"Who invited you?"

"I forget." Eduardo glanced to his left, his fidgeting picking up.

"I think you do remember. I need the name."

Eduardo tapped his right knee twice, closed his eyes, then said, "Frank. Frank Sorensen."

Fabrication. Clark wondered if it really mattered, quickly dismissed the idea. Never take anything for granted. He then spoke very softly, very deliberately.

"I know you're lying, Eduardo. I need the name."

Eduardo shuffled on the floor, rocked forward a little. His eyes were wider now, his breathing a little more audible. It was obvious to Clark the man was moving away from his mental comfort zone.

"Give me the name or all bets are off."

"What's that mean?"

"It means I'll drag your ass downtown and charge your for possession of child pornography. I don't need to tell you that's a felony."

"But I thought you said ..."

"In exchange for information. You're withholding it. You're not keeping up your end of the bargain. Give me the name."

Eduardo seemed highly conflicted. He sat perfectly still, the only movement being little beads of sweat that had formed on his brow, which were beginning to trickle down his face.

"Okay, okay. His name's Randy Matthews."

"When's the last time you communicated with him?"

"Last week."

"Email?"

"Yes ... " Eduardo paused, a look of panic written on his face. "please can you do me a favor?"

"Depends on what it is."

"Randy. He's connected. He finds out that came from me, I'm as good as dead."

"He'll never know."

As Clark typed the name into his computer, he found himself making a critical decision: in the most practical sense, he had all of information he required to find his next target. Only one question remained. The most important he'd ever ask.

"Last question, Eduardo."

"Okay." Eduardo relaxed a bit, seemed to be bolstered by the fact the interrogation was almost over.

"Where's the house?"

"Randy's house?"

"No. The house the organization runs. The house behind the IP address. The house that keeps all the children."

Eduardo's eyes popped wide, his mouth fell open, and his breathing suddenly went ragged.

It was 3:30 on Sunday morning when Gerkowitcz finally walked into the main house on his property. He'd spent the better part of the evening in the dining room of the guest house crunching numbers, trying to determine if he could afford his Chief of Security's recommended enhancements. He had to admit, the man had some pretty far out ideas, real secret agent stuff. At first he'd scoffed at the augmentations, complaining they were going to be way too expensive and unnecessary. Why the hell would he need a perimeter of god damned solar-fed Minoltas surrounding his property in the middle nowhere, anyway? To his credit, though, the chief had kept his cool, explained the nature of the business and the attention it could attract from adversarial elements required such safeguards, discretion being the better part of valor and all that horseshit. He was convincing, enough so to make him reconsider, and then give the go ahead with a plan for implementation. The numbers were good enough, anyway, at least good enough to equip the place with sufficient measures to keep the nefarious elements out.

He walked through the foyer and looked around. It had been more than a few years since he had the old place rehabilitated, and he still found himself impressed with the changes that had taken place. When he purchased the property, he felt certain he'd have no choice but to tear the house down, his gut reaction telling him it was nothing more than a moldy, rotten, decaying structure that couldn't be salvaged. He fretted more than a little over that perception, wondering if he could both demolish and replace the house while keeping his precious find a secret from the small army of workers required for such an undertaking. But the general contractor he brought out to inspect the house told him that besides moderate exterior work and some serious interior rehab, it was basically as sound as the day it was built. So he hired the guy and told him to replace everything: the walls, the ceilings, the floors, the windows, the bathrooms, and the kitchen. When everything was said and done, he'd not been disappointed. What had once been nothing more than a highly functional abandoned building, now looked clean and modern, furnished and decorated to bathe its inhabitants and visitors in a rugged yet elegant Southwestern style.

As he passed through the kitchen, he paused briefly to pour himself a cup of coffee. He didn't much like the taste of it, really, but like everyone else, he found it highly effective at keeping his mental gears grinding. And he'd require a lot more of it in the coming hours. He'd already been up for almost twenty hours, and he figured it would be another twelve or so before he'd allow himself some rest. He'd been mostly satisfied with the security enhancements, but there were still some clarifications he needed, and there was still the matter of inventory replenishment. He took a sip of his coffee, cursed under his breath at scalding his tongue on the hot liquid, and then turned and made his way into the basement, flipping a switch on the wall to light his way.

Compared to the rest of the house, the basement remained relatively unchanged. He'd decided against major improvements there, unwilling to risk the chance of someone not under his control gaining knowledge about what was buried beneath it. Besides some trivial cleaning, the only major difference was the floor: instead of dirt, it was now covered with pea gravel, which made it easier to conceal his find. Its uniform compressive nature also dramatically reduced the odds of an outsider inadvertently discovering the opening by noticing inconsistencies in ground color and compression, as he originally had. He took another sip of his coffee, studied the perfectly circular hole in the gravel. A hatch that could have come from a submarine angled out of the ground above it, a built-in ladder was visible through its opening.

He lowered himself down the steel ladder, which took him fully twenty feet below the floor of the basement. The landing was dimly lit, about ten feet in diameter. He fumbled around at the bottom of the ladder for a moment, located the bank of wall switches, and flipped one that illuminated a long line of incandescent bulbs. The lights were strung along the ceiling of a corridor that lead out of the landing. The corridor led  to the main area of a bomb shelter, built by a crazy old man almost thirty years before. He figured the guy had to have been crazy, spending so much money on a facility that looked as though it had never been used. He let out a soft chuckle as he took in the enormous cylindrical shaft: old man's loss, his gain.

He quickly walked down the long corridor, which eased him approximately ten feet below the floor of the landing. It opened into an large cavern, about 50 feet long, 75 feet wide, and 20 feet high. When he'd first discovered the shelter, he found its main area populated with four things: a greenhouse, which was centered in the middle of ten holes bored into the concrete floor; an outdated, full-service kitchen with a seating area that could comfortably accommodate ten people; an entertainment area, which was furnished with old couches, lamps, end tables, and an color television; and a fitness area, which sported a treadmill, two stationary bikes, workout benches, and racks of free weights.

Equally spaced along the walls to his left and right were doors. There were ten altogether. Behind eight them he'd found small but comfortable bedrooms. Each had a twin bed, a dresser, a desk and chair, and closet space. The remaining two doors opened to large full baths, each with four sinks, a large tub, and a shower area with multiple shower heads. The far wall had only one door. Behind it was another cavern, about half the size of the main one. It contained supplies. Lots of them: hundreds of cans of chili, beans, fruit, vegetables, tuna, chicken, and powdered milk. There was also about 4000 gallons of water. This surprised him as the water to the showers and the kitchen worked. He figured the old man had probably decided to plan for a worst-case scenario, something that might either taint or knock out his water supply, some mega-disaster he never lived to see.

As he walked through the shelter now, he literally breathed in its magnificent transformation. What had started out a depressing concrete gray intertwined with the damp reality of an underground structure now dripped with a welcome freshness. If he were given a hundred years to think about it, he didn't think he could come to terms with why the old man hadn't done something to make it more livable. It had been functional, and that was about it. If the Armageddon he'd been planning for hadn't have gotten them, certainly the oppressive monotony of the shelter would have. After the contractor completed rehabbing the main building, Gerkowitcz had immediately set out to remedy the deficiency. He could not, after all, run his operation with such repugnant surroundings. So he hired a bunch of day laborers and set about carpeting the floors and hanging drywall to a height of ten feet all around the main living area: for the floor, he'd chosen a light cherry Berber, for the walls a desert sandstone. Together, the colors combined to give the whole place a homey, comfortable feeling and a brightness that came close to making up for the lack of natural sunlight.

He rounded out the living area by enhancing or replacing almost everything: he painted the greenhouse the same color as the walls, planted dwarf citrus trees in each of the ten holes surrounding it, and wrapped the structure with a ten foot wide, cream-colored tile patio. The lighting was mostly replaced with ground-mounted light fixtures, giving the area around the greenhouse the look of an upscale neighborhood. He completely gutted the old kitchen, replacing it with cabinetry and appliances that were much more efficient and much more functional. He purchased leather couches for the living room, surrounding them with sturdy yet elegant coffee and end tables. In its far left corner, he set up an entertainment center, the focal point of which was a 60 inch plasma television, enhanced by theater-quality speakers, a DVD player, and a high-end stereo system. In the far right corner, he set up two new ellipticals, a stationary bike, and health-club rated nautilus equipment.

As they were going to be the centerpiece of his new operation, he'd paid particular attention to the bedrooms. The twin beds were replaced with jumbo queens dressed up with frilly comforters and matching throw pillows. On the interior walls, he mounted 40 inch plasma televisions, supplementing each with ceiling-mounted speakers and a DVD player. For the outer walls, he purchased identical antique dressers and matching roll-top desks. Each dresser came with decorative vanities, each desk with a high-backed wooden chair. The remaining space on the walls was broken tastefully by framed prints of landscapes from all over the world, the walls themselves painted with appropriate colors and designs to complement the prints. He'd been aiming for the worldly look of an old-time bed and breakfast, something that would make his guests feel both at home and mentally stimulated. Looking into one of the unoccupied bedrooms as he walked to the back of the living area, he smiled to himself, figuring he'd succeeded.

While he loved the new living space, adored it even, it wasn't his favorite place to spend what little free time he had during his visits. That distinction was solidly held by one of the bathrooms, both of which had started as barely more than military latrines, wrapped up in the same dingy dullness of the living space, only more depressing. And much darker. In each, he'd tiled the floors, covered the concrete walls to ten feet with peach-colored ceramic squares, and removed all of the commodes, showers, and tubs. He'd kept the bathroom on the left wall tied to customary duties, installing four toilets, four glass-enclosed showers, and a pair of cream-colored, ceramic, three-basin sinks. The bathroom on the right wall was his pride and joy: it had only one toilet and one glass-enclosed shower. The rest of the space was filled with a steam room, a jacuzzi that could hold up to eight people, and a full-service bar. He had a weakness for hot water, alcohol, and company, and he estimated he spent a good ten percent of his time in that one bathroom. Sometimes with company; sometimes alone, thinking.

He walked to the rear of the living space and entered the storage area. Most of the staples that had been kept in the middle of the room had been dumped into surrounding open pit mines and replaced with food of better quality and taste. He decided to keep the water, figured it might come in handy if he ever had to wait anything out. Besides, it was all vacuum-sealed, so he figured there was no risk of it being contaminated in any way. In the left rear corner of the room sat a brand new walk-in freezer that held what was left of two of his cows, a variety of fish, and hundreds of pounds of frozen chicken breasts. Prominently displayed in the right rear corner of the room was a wine rack, holding 500 bottles of various brands he especially liked. The only other embellishment to the space was a small board room he'd built into its forward right hand corner, just adjacent to his favorite bathroom. Its windowless walls were built twenty feet high to push flush against the concrete ceiling and heavily insulated to render them sound proof. He paused at the room's entrance, poked in a five-digit pass code, walked in, and closed its heavy oaken door behind him.
