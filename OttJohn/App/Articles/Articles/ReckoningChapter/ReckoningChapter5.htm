Chapter 5

 

 

Carri Parker angled her green Prius into a spot in front of the Rusty Pelican, a bar and grill at which she'd agreed to meet a girlfriend for drinks and appetizers. She exited her car, walked across the sidewalk and toward the front of the establishment, which presented tall, smoky glass windows just opaque enough to allow pedestrians a foggy glimpse into its cavernous interior. She'd never been to the bar before, had never been into the bar-hopping scene, but as she entered through one of its big, glass doors, she immediately felt a sense of kinship with the place.

The establishment had a comfortable-looking dining area to the right and a full-service lounge to the left. And it was all done up a nautical theme: rustic-looking, with lots of wood, nets, and whaling artifacts placed tastefully throughout to pay homage to a profession that had seen its heyday more than a century before. Cutting the interior space into two equal parts from rear to front was an enormous bar, impressively shaped like a 19th century whaler, and carefully engineered to appear as if it were erupting from the oaken floor beneath it. The ship had three large masts, each erected from translucent shelving packed full of rum, vodka, and tequila bottles. The shelves were constructed to wrap around floor-mounted spotlights, which cast powerful beams up and through the glassy masts to give them the glowing look of St. Elmo's fire. The bar was anchored in an upscale neighborhood of Pasadena, a community close enough to L.A. to afford its residents the conveniences of big city life, but far enough away to escape the ubiquity of the crime and excess that infected the greater metropolitan area. Carri walked across the hardwood floor and took a seat at one of the round tables on the port side of the ship.

Besides the staff, the bar was peppered here and there with patrons, a testament to the financial difficulties that had again descended onto the state of California. Typically, during economic downturns, tributary communities around the Basin remained fairly insulated from the cyclic financial distresses that hit L.A. This time, the financial mess had rippled violently from the city center, spreading its concentric rings of financial hardship outward until they lapped at the shore of the normally bustling bar. It was still early, though, just a little after 7 PM, and the crowds would surely gather over the next two hours. A tiny waitress in wench's attire approached with a smile, thankful for something to do. Carri looked over the wine list, ordered a glass of Pinot Noir, and waited for her friend Lisa to arrive.

She'd met Lisa while doing a story for the LA Times about the quality of healthcare for seniors in L.A. hospitals. A friend of a friend had mentioned Lisa as a young but very capable nurse who might be interested in helping with the story. She'd been told that while Lisa worked with pre-adolescent children, she had formerly specialized in senior care. Upon meeting, she'd found Lisa to be a fiery advocate for seniors, pointing out a multitude of hardships that confronted them every day in California's monolithic healthcare system. Along the way, Lisa had taken great pains to help Carri understand the senior-relevant medical landscape and the sometimes-complicated information she received from local experts. By the time she'd completed her article, she'd found much more than a competent source in Lisa, she'd found a life-long friend. After weeks of collecting data and collaborating with her new friend, she finally had her article. It had merited front page placement and was received very well by both local and national readers, even garnering her some regional awards. Emboldened by success, Carri had pitched her editor a similar story on children. He liked the idea, so here she sat, sipping her wine, waiting to ask her friend if she would once again guide her through California's persistently over-complicated healthcare system.

As ten minutes of waiting slipped to twenty, Carri ordered another glass of pinot, then glanced toward the aft part of the bar where a television was hanging from the ceiling. As she watched the latest news on CNN, she caught herself looking occasionally at a man sitting by himself at the stern of the ship. He was large, probably over six feet, definitely over 200 pounds. He had light brown, cropped hair, which she thought might place him in the military. He had a generally rough-hewn but handsome face that looked as though he'd not shaved in the last day or so. He leaned over the bar, large hands cradling a beer. He looked directly forward, seemingly at nothing. His eyes appeared focused, steely, and intense. She could imagine his fixed stare boring holes through the rear of the ship, drilling right through the forecastle and out the front of the bar. Whoever he was, he looked as though he had a lot on his mind. She tried to look away, but found it difficult, the same way people find it hard to avert their attention from an accident. She considered herself a student of society and believed she had a knack for first impressions, and if her first impression about the man at the end of the bar was correct, he wasn't just a traffic accident, he was a train wreck.

Carri's natural instinct for finding a story was suddenly supplanted by shame for staring at the man. She tried again to look away, succeeded for a time, but her gaze gravitated back toward him. She was curious about what kind of thoughts, if any, were ruminating behind those eyes. He raised the bottle to his mouth, its neck pinched between his fore- and middle fingers. The movement distressed the sleeves on his shirt, which were momentarily punched skyward by a bicep muscle as he downed the rest of his beer. He set the empty on the bar, then glanced in her general direction. They'd shared eye contact for just a split second, but that was enough to send an ice-cold shiver up and down her spine. Whatever he was thinking, it was too hot for her. She looked away.

"What the hell are you looking at?" A voice asked from behind her.

Carri stiffened but didn't turn. She didn't need to. The voice belonged to her ex-husband, a first-rate ass whom she'd divorced two years ago. Since she'd left him, he'd disrupted her life constantly, which had caused her to contemplate everyday how she would drive to work, where she could go to lunch, how she would get home, where she could meet with friends, and even where she could go shopping. She'd done everything she could to keep him away, even calling the police and going through the court system to file a restraining order. As a last resort, she'd even moved and kept her location a secret from everyone except her closest friends. Her career, it seemed, had been work and avoiding Chuck, the maelstrom in her life that had suddenly appeared on the shores of this sparsely populated island in the middle of Pasadena. Obviously, Chuck wanted Carri, but what Carri wanted was irrelevant. She remained motionless just long enough to build up her resolve, then slowly turned to face him.

"Why are you here?" She asked, flustered. Carri was genuinely scared of the man. And she had reason to be. He'd put her in the hospital more than once, and he'd scared the living hell out of her more times than she cared to remember. When they'd first met, she knew he'd be a little difficult; but at the time, he was mostly sweet, even romantic. His gentle side, however, was balanced with a nasty disposition, which was exacerbated by an addiction to alcohol. She had thought she could change him, calm the dark beasts that occasionally made him crazy. But she had no idea what she was getting into, no idea of the possessive, jealous, and abusive monster he could be. As time passed, his addiction worsened, and with the alcohol came the abuse, both mental and physical. And now he was here, and she had no idea how this was going to end. From the look on his face, probably in the emergency room.

"Don't ask me any fucking questions! I asked you what the fuck you're looking at!" He grabbed her arm, his ruddy face flushed red with a happy rage.

She unsuccessfully tried to free herself from his grip.. At six foot and a little over 200 pounds, Chuck was just too much for her petite frame. She looked him in the eyes, praying he wouldn't get any more violent. She could smell the alcohol on his breath, escaping every pore of his body in a repugnant, bitter stink that made her want to throw up.

"I'm not looking at anyone, Chuck! Please let go of me. You're hurting me!" Carri was on the verge of tears, her voice cracked, elevated to just under a shout. The staff and the patrons had now taken notice, each of them lingering at that uncertain point between acknowledgement and action.

"Fuck you!" He replied. Then more softly, "Look, we need to talk. Let's go to the car. I promise I won't hurt you. Let's just get out of here, okay baby?" Chuck tightened his beefy grip around Carri's wrist, began pulling her off her seat.

"No! Stop it! You're hurting me! Stop, Chuck!" This time, she did shout. Feeling trapped and in imminent danger, she began fighting back by kicking, pulling, and scratching. Whatever the outcome, she knew she had to resist Chuck with every ounce of strength she had. She knew if he took her now, forced her out of here, the only thing waiting for her would be a savage beating and another week in the hospital, at best.

By now, the entire bar was transfixed with the drama. The bartender, a young kid, probably a college student, tentatively approached.

"Hey! Sorry, but the two of you'll hafta leave. If you don't, I'll be forced to call the cops." He then made a nervous show of reaching for and picking up the receiver.

Instead of scaring some sense into him, the threat from the bartender only enraged Chuck further.

"Look what you did, bitch!" He screamed as he pulled Carri violently from where she was sitting.

Carri made one last attempt to pull herself free, but Chuck was too strong. She had tried to use the floor as leverage, but all she'd managed to do was hurt her wrist more and slip and fall against her chair. Chuck looked down at her menacingly, his sandy blonde hair now dripping a little with sweat, his eyes crazy with rage.

"Get up, and get your ass into the car. Now!" He spoke it almost in a whisper, a carefully-controlled voice that told her if she didn't comply, she'd be beaten. It was that eye-of-the-storm voice he had just before he flipped out. Carri wanted nothing to do with it, decided she'd make a stand. She'd fight.

"No!" She sobbed, defiantly. She remained on the floor, crying. The bartender began punching digits on the phone. Then an unfamiliar voice interjected.

"Listen, Chuck, I don't think the girl wants to go home with you, so why don't you just take this opportunity to get out of here before someone gets hurt?"

Carri looked up. Behind Chuck stood the man with the cold, steel blue eyes. The man who'd been sitting at the end of the bar.

Clark had watched the drama unfold along with the other patrons in the bar. He didn't want to get involved, didn't want to risk putting himself on anyone's radar unless he really had to. But no one else reacted. They were all immobilized, looking like shocked bystanders, uncertain how to quench the domestic violence burning in front of them. They all simply remained seated, watching. Even the bartender seemed to have trouble making his fingers work the keypad of the bar phone. So Clark watched with them, hoping someone would step up. Then the guy named Chuck pulled the woman from her chair, and that's when he'd decided he'd seen enough.

He stepped off his stool, walked around behind the guy, and offered up his warning. But the man didn't turn around, didn't acknowledge him. Maybe he'd gotten the name wrong, or maybe the guy was just so focused on beating the woman, he hadn't heard. Just as he was about to repeat his warning, the man turned to face him.

Chuck's face was flushed red, his eyes wide like saucers. He took a step forward, then pushed his face into Clark's space, just close enough so their noses were no more than three inches apart. Then he threw his arms out to the sides, cocked his head to the left a little, and said, "Who the fuck are you?"

Clark could smell the alcohol. And that was good and bad news: the bad news was Chuck was going to fight someone. The alcohol made that unavoidable. The good news was Chuck was going to be easy to take down. The alcohol made that unavoidable too. Clark recaptured his personal space by taking a step back.

"Just a guy who isn't going to let a jackass like you continue to hurt the lady."

"Jackass?" Chuck paused, looked at Clark as if he were sizing him up. "If you know what's good for you big guy, you'll back the fuck off. Me and my girl are having a little disagreement. But it's over now. We're going home. You get in my way, you're gonna regret it."

As he listened to Chuck's slurred threat, Clark noticed the woman had successfully wrung herself free. But she remained on the floor, looking terrified as she massaged her left wrist with her right hand. Then Chuck turned back toward her.

"You fucking bitch!" He seethed. "Get your ass up and let's get the fuck out of here." He bent closer, said in a whisper, "Get up. I swear to God I'll kill you if you don't do what I say!"

As Chuck started moving toward the woman, Clark grabbed his right elbow and spun him around.

"Look, you're not listening to me, sport. The woman doesn't want anything to do with you. Don't make a scene. Just go."

"And what if I don't, asshole?" Chuck took a step forward.

Clark waited as the woman seized the opportunity to move away to safety behind the bar, then he shifted his stance slightly so his body angled away from Chuck's. First rule of fighting: never let your opponent get an easy shot at your package.

"Well, pal," Clark said coolly, "your choice. You can leave now on your feet, or you can stick around and wind up having a couple of EMT's carry you out."

"How's that, big man? You gonna fuck me up?"

Chuck moved a little closer, reclaimed the personal space Clark had freed up by taking the step back. The man's hairline was beginning to drip with sweat, his words had gone up in pitch, leveled off somewhere between a command and a scream. Clark kept his eyes on Chuck's,  then took a half step back to regain the real estate.

"That's the general idea."

"Right!" Chuck screamed as he launched a lumbering haymaker with his left hand, targeting Clark's right temple.

Clark pulled back, causing Chuck's fist to miss wildly. The momentum behind the swing caused the man to stumble to the left slightly, so Clark shifted to the left along with him, just far enough to grab his collar and pull him in for a vicious head butt to the nose. He could hear the crunching disintegration of cartilage as the crown of his forehead slammed into the man's face. Chuck's head careened backward from the impact, his legs buckled, and a crimson geyser followed him downward as he crumpled to the floor.

Clark moved around Chuck so the man's nose was facing away from him. Then he knelt and pressed a knee between Chuck's neck and jaw, applying just enough pressure to cut a little circulation and a little air. As a small pool of blood spread on the floor beneath Chuck's face, Clark bent over so his mouth was just over the man's left ear.

"Listen, Chuck, I'm gonna give you a really important piece of advice right now, and if you're smart, you'll listen."

Chuck moaned lightly, managed to gurgle out, "You broke my fucking nose!"

Clark pressed a little harder with his knee, pushing Chuck's face into the oaken floor. "You have to concentrate, Chuck, okay? I need you to pay attention to this. You gonna listen?"

"Yeah." Chuck gasped, taking a ragged breath.

"The woman you're harassing, what's her name?"

"Carri." He retched as he wheezed the name.

Given the angle of Chuck's head, Clark figured blood was starting to pool at the back of his nose and beginning to flow down his esophagus. He'd have to let him up soon, or he'd choke.

"Listen, Chuck, Carri doesn't like you. Quite frankly, neither do I. You're an asshole. You listening?" Clark slapped the side of Chuck's face a couple of times until he got a reaction.

"Yeah." Chuck gasped, coughing up a little blood.

"Okay, Chuck, Carri's my new friend, and I'm very protective. That means you have to be careful …"

"Fuck you!" The words were coughed out explosively, rather than spoken. Little trails of blood streamed in front of Chuck, painting the picture of a  crimson crown on the floor in front of him.

"Chuck." Clark admonished. "You're not listening." Clark pressed his knee down a little harder, until Chuck's face turned beet red.

"Listen carefully. I'm very protective of my friends. They're like family to me. If I hear you even so much as give Carri a questionable look, I'll hunt you down. And here's the important part, okay?"

Chuck nodded.

"When I find you, Chuck, you won't be walking away after I'm done with you. Is that clear?"

Chuck managed to grunt out an acknowledgement, the lack of any verbal concession signifying he was having difficulty breathing.

"Okay, sport. I guess you can go. Do me a favor and just get out. Don't look back."

Clark pushed himself off the floor, then walked around Chuck to place his body in a defensive position in front of Carri, who was still behind the bar.

Chuck stumbled to his feet, tripped to the nearest table, grabbed some napkins to stanch the blood pouring from his nose. He then turned toward Clark, looked as if he were going to say something, but instead turned and headed for the door.

The bar was silent as Clark watched Chuck leave. He didn't know if the bartender had finally gotten around to calling the police, but he figured he'd play it safe and assume the kid did. He waited a minute longer to ensure Chuck wouldn't return, then turned to Carri and nodded as he headed for the exit.

 

Carri just stood there, so stunned she hadn't noticed Lisa had entered the bar, was standing right next to her, just as terrified.

"Oh my God, Carri! Are you okay? That was Chuck, wasn't it? Who the hell was the other guy?" Lisa asked the questions frantically, her words shaken with stress.

Carri didn't answer, just walked slowly toward the door, taking her tentative first steps to run after the man who'd saved her from a trip the emergency room. She wasn't sure that going after him was a good idea. But she wasn't sure about a lot of things lately.

"Where're you going?" Lisa placed a hand on Carri's forearm, causing her to slow but not stop entirely.

"I need to thank him." She motioned toward the door as she continued to walk.

"Are you sure that's such a good idea? Maybe he's just a drunk who was looking for a fight. Maybe he's no better than Chuck himself."

"All the same, Lisa, he may have just saved my life. The least I can give him is a thank you." She stopped, then looked at her friend.

"Hey, I think I want to do this alone. It's going to be awkward enough. Can you wait for me at the bar and order me another drink? I'll be back in a minute."

"Okay, honey. You sure you're okay?"

"Yeah," she lied. How could she be okay?

As Lisa headed back to the bar, Carri walked quickly to the door, hoping she wasn't too late.

Clark walked toward his truck like a guy in a bit of a rush, but not so much of one as to attract attention. What the hell was he thinking? Stupidity, the essential ingredient for disaster. And he just whipped up one of the dumbest meals he'd cooked up for himself in a long time. A fight in a bar days after he'd killed a guy just across town wasn't asking for trouble; it was begging for it. He retrieved his keys from his pocket and unlocked his truck, an old 1996 F150. He was about to open the door of his pickup when he heard the clack-clack of heals closing in behind him.

"Wait!" Female voice. The woman from the bar. Carri.

Clark backed away from his truck, then turned toward the sidewalk and crossed in front of a tattoo parlor, moving as if he didn't hear her. He slowed up his pace a little, studied a sign on the parlor door advertising its operating hours. He saw no point in evasion, he'd have to come around to his truck later anyway, and that would cost him precious time. He didn't want to talk to the woman, either, but perhaps if he ignored her, she'd get the idea and get the hell home before Check decided to return. No sirens yet, maybe he could still make it.

 

"Wait!" Carri exclaimed again. "Please stop!" She knew he was ignoring her, but she'd noticed he'd slowed. She continued until she was about ten feet from him, then remembered what Lisa had said. She had no idea who this guy was, but she didn't believe he was there hoping for a fight. It didn't make sense. He looked like a guy with enough troubles already, not like one who needed more. Still, she couldn't be too careful. She already had one violent stalker; she didn't need another.

As the man turned to face her, she was again taken by his cold, steel blue eyes. She tried to look down, away from them, but she was frozen. Again she began to wonder what thoughts ruminated behind them, what scars had chilled them, froze them to their core. There was deep anger in them. And hurt. Instinctively, she stepped back.

The man shrugged, turned, and started walking away. Not the reaction she was looking for. Not the interaction she had in mind. Not a good day at all. She cast her eyes to the dirty sidewalk, studied the discarded cigarette butts cast about by careless smokers, then burst into tears. It was all just a little too much.

She'd spent the last few years hiding and running from Chuck, a man who clearly deserved every ounce of scorn she could throw his way. And now for some ridiculous reason, she felt slighted by this man who had just stepped in and helped her out in a big way. She felt her face flush, her hands begin to tremble. A feeling of vertigo took hold of her. She leaned on the cement wall of the tattoo parlor behind her, became vaguely aware of her tears. She covered her eyes with her hands and tried as best she could to choke back the sobs, then slowly started sinking to the grimy sidewalk. To hell with her clothes. To hell with what people thought. To hell with men. To hell with it all. Just as she was about to settle onto the concrete, she was suddenly propped up by a pair of very strong hands. She panicked, thinking Chuck had stuck around, returning to finish what he'd begun. But the panic was short-lived, replaced almost instantly by a maniacal anger, rooted in years of pent up resentment for having to live the way she did. She used every bit of energy to free her elbows, then screamed.

"Get the fuck away, Chuck!"

She waited for the beating. But it didn't come. She looked up, found herself staring into those steel blue eyes again. And they were no longer cold.

 

Clark knew he had to get away, but he didn't feel right about leaving Carri on the sidewalk, alone, and on the verge of a nervous breakdown. Maybe he had a little time. He still hadn't heard any sirens. He wondered if the bartender had even called the police, began to doubt that he had.

"Are you going to be okay?" He asked.

Carri retreated a step, then looked up. "Sorry. I thought you were Chuck." She paused, shifting big blue eyes nervously to the left and right. "Yeah. I think so. Just tired of the hassle; tired of running and hiding."

"Does he know where you live?"

"No, I don't think he does. I've been careful."

"You here alone?"

"No. I have a friend inside. I was supposed to meet her about an article I'm writing for the paper."

"Why isn't this guy in Jail, Carri?" She perked up a little when he said her name. Clark didn't understand why, but it was a good sign. Perhaps she'd recover quickly enough, and he could get back to work.

"He did go to jail after his last assault. Since then, he's been a lot more careful, mostly just following me around, sending me nasty emails, damaging my property, that sort of thing. He's gotten really good at it too. Most of what he does I can't prove, so the cops don't seem too interested." She paused. "Listen, I'm really sorry you had to get involved. Thank you very much just the same. I know you're busy, so I'll let you go. Thanks for listening, ..." Carri paused again, waiting for the man in front of her to fill in the blank.

"Joe. It was my pleasure."

Carri smiled weakly, then turned and headed back to the Pelican. As he watched her walk away, Clark figured he could do one more thing for her, something to keep her safe and get them both on their way.

"Carri," he called after her, taking a couple of steps her way as he did.

She turned to face him. For the first time he became aware of the mascara that coursed down her cheeks, leaving dark rivulets, smeared here and there where she'd wiped at her tears. Even through the mess, he thought she looked beautiful.

"This time you're calling me back?" She managed a crooked smile.

"You can't go back in there."

"What do you mean?"

He motioned around his eyes and down his cheek, not wanting to actually tell her she was a mess. She got the message.

"Oh. Thank you," she said.

"He might come back. You really need to get out of here. I suggest you have your meeting with your friend someplace else."

"I have no plans to stay, but thank you." She spoke softly as she rubbed her face with  her hands.

Clark noticed her composure was back. Whatever else was true about this woman, she had demonstrated she was very strong, resilient. As she wiped at her face with her hands, their eyes locked for a moment. His mind was immediately overwhelmed with a familiar pang of loneliness and longing and the resolute hope that a woman so beautiful could find her way past her troubles and into the arms of a warm and loving man. Just as quickly as they'd come, though, the thoughts disappeared, moved to an unproductive area of his brain where extraneous ideas are deposited to die. The present sidebar to his mission was a mistake, and he had to ensure it wouldn't happen again. From now on, if he needed a beer, he'd drink it in his room. But he needed to see this one through.

"Look, I really don't feel good about leaving you here now. So if you'll let me, I'd like to run a little interference for you on your way to wherever you're going."

"And how do you plan on doing that?" She asked, trying to force a smile that wasn't there.

"Just give me a description of Chuck's car. We can caravan until you get about a mile from where you're going. When you're there, tap your brakes three times. I'll pull off to the side of the road and stick around the area for about a half hour just to make sure he didn't follow."

"Why the change in heart? You seemed to be in such a big hurry earlier."

Clark could feel her appraising him with her eyes. It made him feel nervous and warm all at once. It was the right look at the wrong time. It was a look he longed for but couldn't acknowledge.

"Just want to make sure you make it out of here in one piece. Promise. It goes no further than that."

Carri nodded, turned toward the bar, then stopped.

"Joe?"

"Yeah." Clark cringed at the sound of the name.

"I'd really like the opportunity to thank you for this somehow. I'd like to buy you lunch or coffee if you'll let me."

"Sure." He wasn't good at lying. It didn't appear to him, however, that she'd noticed.

"Thanks," she said.

Carri took a moment to gather herself, smiled at Clark, then returned to the bar to retrieve her friend.
