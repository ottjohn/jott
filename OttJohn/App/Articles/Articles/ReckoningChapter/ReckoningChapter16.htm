Chapter 16

 

 

Tobias rolled up to the Hassenback residence a little after 4:30 in the morning. He stepped out of his car into the warm, comfortable air that generally hangs around the LA area in the early Fall. He took a deep, tired breath and listened. The din of the constant stream of metro traffic sounded far off, supplanted by a chorus of crickets, accompanied by the rhythmic beat of a few early-morning sprinklers, an annoyed California Blue Jay taking a rough stab at lead vocals. He closed his eyes and focused on the peacefulness, breathed in a total of two-minutes' worth of nature's symphony before the angry gurgle of Sanchez's Mustang broke the natural silence. He turned toward the sound and waited on the driveway as Sanchez parked his car.

"So, how was the drive?" He asked as Sanchez approached.

"Not bad. I could get used to driving around this time of night ... morning." Sanchez walked up to Tobias, holding a cup of coffee in his right hand, keys jangling in his left.

"You have fun with that. I try not to get up before the sun does. Speaking of daylight, I have an appointment with a magistrate in Pasadena in three hours, so we need to hurry. How 'bout you take the back part of the house, and I'll take the front. We'll meet somewhere in the middle."

"Sounds good to me, boss." His reply sounded upbeat, but his face painted a different picture. It was done up in more than a little concern, something Tobias had seen many times before.

"What's on your mind?"

"It's nothing."

"Must be something, so you might as well tell me. And don't lie to me; I can tell, you know." Tobias gave Sanchez a mock look of irritation, the same he oftentimes gave his daughter when she needed to talk about something but felt she couldn't. It always worked with her, and he'd found it worked just as well on Sanchez.

"Okay. It's just that we need to have some kind of code word for early morning calls. You scared the shit out of me, Tobias. I thought for sure something bad had happened."

Tobias saw that Sanchez was smiling good-naturedly, but he could tell the kid meant what he said. He was right, too. Late night calls are no fun. He took the keys to the Hassenback residence out of his pocket, searched for the right one, and pushed it into the lock. As he entered the house, he looked back at Sanchez and said,

"Sounds good to me. How about 'Yo!'?"

Sanchez scratched his chin, said, "Hey, that's pretty good. You been taking Ebonics classes again?"

"Gotta figure out a way to understand you, don't I?"

"I know what you mean. I keep waiting for the community college to offer a class in Colloquial Cracker so I can understand you. Bridge the gap, you know?"

"It is the official language around these parts."

"When are you retiring again?"

"Soon as I run you off the force. Then my job's done."

Tobias and Sanchez stepped into the house, the short, good-natured exchange providing the necessary grease to get their mental wheels turning. As Sanchez headed to the rear of the house, Tobias stayed in front and headed to the kitchen. As he opened and closed drawers looking for Hassenback's designated bill repository, he yelled to the other side of the house.

"Hey, you have a chance to contact Rathman?"

"Yeah, as a matter of fact, I did. Why is it everyone I know who knows you too seems to be working on the weekends?"

"Because I'm a magnet for hard work and productivity. What'd he say?"

"Not much. They did run the query, but they got too many results, like more than 100 hits."

Tobias whistled, then said, "Wow. That's a lot of big guys with victimized children."

"Yeah, I guess predators don't keep those demographics in mind when they troll for little kids, do they?"

"Guess not. So what's the deal, then? How long until they get that list narrowed down?"

"He said he doesn't have an ETA. Same old crap from him. I kind of got the vibe he wasn't too interested in sharing. Must not have taken that class in grade school. I did, however, ask him for the local list he came up with. He told me he'd be more than happy to get to me tomorrow ... or this morning, I mean."

"Good. We've got nothing on our guy. Might as well have a look. He said nothing about running through it with FBI resources?"

"I asked him about that. He told me he was crunching the list only as a professional courtesy. Beyond that, he said the murder's local and everything else we have is nothing more than speculation. Told me until there's stronger evidence regarding kidnapping and a sex ring, the FBI couldn't officially get involved."

Tobias thought about that for a moment while he opened and closed the last of the drawers in the kitchen.

"Doesn't sound right, kid. We have a dead body, former sex offender, with solid evidence for a local sex ring. This sounds right up their alley. You'd thinking they'd be chomping at the bit to take this one over."

"Not according to him." Sanchez's voice quieted as he rounded the corner from the living room to the kitchen. He walked up to Tobias, leaned against the counter, said,

"I told him about the IP address you found in the phony marketing file from Hassenback's thumb drive, told him the site behind it re-iterated almost word-for-word the information in the file. I even went through why we believed the site is a front for an organized sex ring that caters to pedophiles. He didn't sound convinced, said it was probably just mock-up work for some small bank in the valley. I asked him if he thought it might be a good idea to follow up on it anyway, you know, given the nature of Hassenback and this Eduardo guy. He told me if the FBI followed-up on every weak lead they received, they wouldn't have the man-power to do what they were actually paid to do."

"And what's that?"

Sanchez chuckled, said, "I was kind of wondering that myself, but I didn't ask, and he didn't say. You find anything?"

"Not a damn thing. What about you?"

Sanchez shook his head, said nothing.

"Well, shit. Magistrate's not going to be too happy with me wasting his time. You remember anyone bagging mail for evidence?"

"I don't think so. I don't remember."

"Murder case, it would seem the only thing they would bag is personal correspondence, leave the bills behind," Tobias said, mostly to himself.

"Did you check the trash?"

"Yeah. There was some mail, but nothing from a phone company or an ISP." Tobias looked at his watch, the dials on its face telling him it was 5:15 in the morning. He mulled calling the department but figured he'd be going straight to work anyway. No sense in asking a clerk to do something he could do himself in five minutes. He doubted he'd find what he was looking for in evidence anyway. He looked at Sanchez, said,

"Maybe he uses wall plates to hide his bills too."

Sanchez smiled, looked a little haggard, the early hour beginning to catch up with him, run him down like a slow-moving Mack truck.

"Doubt it. What's next? Whatever it is, I'm gonna need more coffee before we do it."

"We can go back to the station and check evidence, but you know as well as I do what the outcome's going to be."

"Yep. Better to be thorough, though. Who knows, maybe one of the techs bagged it."

"Yeah, let's hope so because that's all we got now, short of looking up and calling every ISP in the greater LA area."

Sanchez frowned. "That would suck."

"You're telling me. Let's get out of here."

Tobias and Sanchez walked out of the house and into the morning air. It was still quiet, peaceful even. Tobias closed the door behind him and locked it, then closed his eyes and gave a listen for the angry Blue Jay he'd heard screaming earlier. He loved birds, but he didn't particularly care for the Blue Jay. He found its obnoxious character and incessant angry, squawk irritating, its genetic disposition for thievery repugnant. Tobias stopped in his tracks. The word hung in the foreground of his mind, shining with the cool light of a cheap neon sign: thievery. It kept its position long enough for him to understand, then faded into oblivion. He shook his head, cursed under his breath.

"You okay?" Asked Sanchez.

"I apologize, kid. I should've never gotten you up for this."

"S'ok. How could you have known?"

"I should have. I don't think the bills were ever here. At least not when the techs arrived. Our perp took them. Thing is, if he's thorough enough to take the equipment, he's certainly thorough enough to take the bills. Our theory's that he's after information, right? That ISP bill is the kind of information he's after. He might see it as a get-out-of-jail card. But it's not free. It's risky. Information he'd use as a last resort. If he happens to have trouble finding what he's looking for with respect to the hardware he took, he could simply call the ISP, pass himself off as Hassenback, and try to obtain information that way."

Sanchez looked down at the ground, smiling, then returned his focus to Tobias and said, "How the hell do you come up with this stuff?"

"A little birdy told me."

Sanchez laughed, said, "Yeah? Well next time could you have your conversation with the bird before you call me?"

"I'll make sure I do that. Let's go get some fast food and some coffee, I'll buy. Then I need to get to the station. No sense in you coming. Go back to bed. All I'm going to be doing is affirming the bill isn't there. After that I'm going to be busy eating crow."

Sanchez loitered for a moment, turned around and took one last look at the house. He shook his head, and started after Tobias, who was almost to his car. Then something stopped him cold.

"Hey, Tobias."

"Yeah."

"What'd that little birdy say about checking the mailbox?" He hitched his thumb over his shoulder at the mailbox hanging right next to the door. It was overflowing.

 

They found the bill. It was sent from a company called The Orange Network, a local ISP operating in Orange City, California. The search of the mail had burned another 15 minutes, and  the eastern sky was barely beginning to glow with a light-pink hue that told Tobias he had about two hours to burn before his early morning meeting with the magistrate. Too tired to think about pushing paperwork after the quick drive-through breakfast he promised Sanchez, Tobias instead elected to treat his understudy to a full breakfast and coffee at a local sit-down establishment a block north of the station. After they ate, Tobias told Sanchez to go home, nap for an hour or so, catch up with Rathman, get the local suspect list, and meet him back at the station at around noon.

He arrived at the Pasadena Superior Court at seven in the morning, favoring an early arrival over a an ill-advised gamble on LA traffic. He spent his half-hour wait looking over Hassenback's ISP bill, reviewing notes he'd entered about the case in his PDA, and entering new notes into the device regarding the more salient points he'd include in his affidavit. When the appointment time arrived, the secretary, a handsome, older woman with taste for Johnson-era attire, lead Tobias into the office of magistrate Robert Michaels.

Michaels sat behind his desk, elbows propped on its surface, chubby fingers steepled in front of his face. He had short, thin, greasy hair, a large, bulbous nose, two close-set bright blue eyes, and dark fleshy lips that held the remnants of a recently-consumed powder donut. As Tobias, entered the room, the corpulent man stood, his shirt almost bursting with exertion. He extended a his right, sweaty paw, then said,

"You must be Detective Tobias. I'm Robert Michaels. Pleased to meet you." His voice was almost falsetto.

"Nice to meet you. Glad I could get on your schedule this morning. I know you must be busy."

"Well, you know just as well as I do, right?" Michaels sat back down, his chair protesting as he did. "What can I do for you?"

"I was hoping you could help me secure a search warrant."

"Got the paperwork?" Michaels leaned back in his chair expectantly, wiped his hands on his tie, adding to its burgeoning population of stains.

Tobias retrieved his PDA, opened the file he'd been working on, and presented it to the magistrate, who looked confused.

"I'm sorry, what's this?"

Tobias blanched slightly, knew this might be a problem. "Sorry, sir. I had no time to type an affidavit. I only found the target of the warrant a couple of hours ago, and the evidence I might find there is extremely time sensitive."

Michaels looked slightly irritated, rocked further back in his chair, clasped his hands atop his ample belly, and said, "I have to be honest with you, detective. I almost never process a warrant unless I get some paperwork with it. Bringing the paperwork shows a level of respect for the court, not to mention a level of preparation on your part. Now, if you like ... " Michaels looked through his calendar on his desk. "If you like, I can have Rebecca out there reschedule you for later today, which will give you time to come back prepared."

Tobias held back a scowl, replaced his PDA in his pocket and stood. He looked down at the big, round man in the chair, who was again busying himself with wiping his stubby fingers on his maligned tie.

"If that's what you think is necessary." Tobias turned, started toward the door. Michaels called after,

"It's not what I think is necessary, detective. It's called protocol."

Tobias stopped and turned. "There are times, sir, when rules need to be bent, if not completely broken. There are some circumstances so compelling, so overwhelming, that they demand immediate attention, even in the face of marginal preparation. I'm sure your rules are important. Forgive me. I know they're important. I have, after all, followed them for over two decades! But in my line of work, sir, we're allowed to make judgment calls, especially when it comes to matter of life and death. That must be the difference between our operational processes. I understand. Your protocol is much too important to see it violated by something as meaningless as the welfare of children or the life of a single man." Tobias turned again toward the door, was reaching for the latch, stopped, and said,

"Please forgive my outburst. It's been a long couple of days. No sleep. If it's still an option, I'll take that appointment for later today." He turned the latch, pushed the door open.

"Stop. Close the door detective."

Tobias stopped, turned around, and closed the door. He then raised a hand in front of him and shook his head.

"Listen, it's been a really long day. I'm sorry about ..."

Michaels interrupted, his falsetto voice giving way to a more demanding pitch, eyebrows furrowing into twin knots above his eyes. "Children. What about children?"

Tobias said nothing, just looked surprised. Michaels motioned him back to the chair, and Tobias sat.

"I'll bend rules, detective, if it'll help a child in danger. Rules be damned in that case. Elaborate."

"Mind if I just give you a verbal argument for the warrant along the way?"

"Well, we're here, you've got my attention, so be my guest."

Tobias retrieved his PDA from his pocket, used it for reference as he took Michaels through a chronological history of the case, starting with Hassenback as a dead body and ending with Eduardo as a potential victim.

"So you're saying there could be information at the ISP that might help you identify this Eduardo fellow, who could be in grave danger."

"That's correct, sir. The ISP handled Hasseback's internet connection. Hassenback electronically corresponded with Eduardo. With any luck, then, there might still be some evidence of those correspondences on the ISP's servers. Problem is, many ISP's trash their logs and any deleted files after 60 days, and I'm not sure about when the two last corresponded. With respect to this case, searching those files in a timely fashion is going to make or break us." Tobias leaned back in his chair, once again feeling uncomfortable about where the conversation might be heading.

"I see. And how is this going to help solve the Hassenback case?"

Tobias held back a look of impatience. He'd never liked having to repeat things, especially simple statements of fact. But in this case he knew better. He knew the magistrate was repeating the questions to gain more clarity with respect to causality, knew it would be much worse if he weren't re-hashing the questions.

"Indirectly. We think the guy who killed Hassenback is also in possession of the phony marketing literature I told you about. Make no mistake, this isn't your regular run-of-the-mill killer. He's very bright. If he does have that file, I'd bet my next three paychecks he's also found the site associated with it. And if he's come to the same conclusion we have regarding the purpose of that site, he's going to be very interested in talking to Eduardo. The last person he visited is dead, and I'm afraid if we wait too long on this, Eduardo's going to wind up dead too."

Michaels stroked his puffy chin with stubby fingers, mulling. "Okay, I think you have cause to search the Orange files to help you prevent a crime from happening. Since you're going to use the fruits of your search to find and protect this Eduardo fellow and leverage his resources to identify Hassenback's killer, I'll grant another warrant on his residence when you find it."

Tobias shifted in his chair, a look of relief washing across his face.

"Now, about the facility behind the website and the kids," Michaels started.

"Yes sir. Again, there's no certainty here. But if we don't act, if we don't put some resources on this, we'll never know for sure. I don't know about you, but I don't think I'd ever sleep peacefully again knowing I didn't at least look into it."

"Cart's before the horse, detective. At this moment, there's not much to write a warrant on with respect to that. Far as I know, everything you need is at the ISP. Once you get me a direction with respect to that part of the case, though, you come see me. I'll have the warrant waiting for you when you get here."

Tobias smiled. The conversation had started tempestuously, but he felt good about where it had gone. Foe to ally in less than ten minutes. Not bad. He figured neither of them had given the other the benefit of the doubt when he first walked in."

"Well, sir, you know we're hoping the Hassenback records will lead us to Eduardo. If Eduardo uses the same ISP, which is a long-shot, I know, we can get an additional warrant to scrutinize his activity. Between finding Eduardo and collecting his transactional history, I'm confident that'll lead us to the establishment behind the website."

Michaels placed both hands on his desk, gripping its edge forcefully. "That won't be necessary. Once you establish they've been communicating, that the content of their communication violates the conditions of their paroles or constitutes criminal activity, you'll be able to drill into Eduardo's activities without an additional warrant." He gave Tobias a wink.

"That's good to know." Tobias looked at the man expectantly, barely containing his impatience to get legal machinery engaged and moving along.

"Okay!" Michaels sat up, seeming to get the hint. He looked at his watch, and then at Tobias. "It's almost eight in the morning now. Tell you what, you give me an hour, and I'll have this ready for you to pick up."

 

Tobias left Michaels to do his work, but decided to stay at the courthouse and wait for the warrant, another morning decision based on California driving and its inexorable disruption of time and space. Some places have weather. LA has traffic. After picking up the paperwork at 8:45 in the morning, Tobias left the offices of the Superior Court of Pasadena, got in his car, and headed in the general direction of the ISP. As he was driving, he phoned Lieutenant Dursk with the LAPD Computer Forensics Team and told him to have crew meet him at the offices of The Orange Network, which was located in the southern part of the City of Orange, on the 1100 block of Taft. A half hour later, Tobias stood face-to-face with the Lieutenant himself.

"Lieutenant Dursk, I presume?" He asked.

"That's me," Dursk replied, the gravelly voice Tobias remembered from the previous night's phone conversation sounding even rougher in person. He was a big man, about a head taller than Tobias. He had short-cropped silver hair, a narrow fish-like face, and a long, thin nose set below two piercing gray eyes. He reached out and grabbed Tobias's hand, flashing a crooked smile as he did. His smile was tightly surrounded by thin, wind-parched lips, teeth yellowed and breath soured from a lifetime of smoking.

Tobias said, "Really appreciate you meeting me here. I know you must be tired."

"I'm not going to lie, Detective, it's a little past my bedtime. But when it comes to this kind of stuff, getting the bad guys is a little more important than my sleep, you know what I mean?" He withdrew his hand, placed his thumbs in the beltline of his over-sized pants, which hung off his frame like curtains. He took a short tug on his cigarette, blew out pale smoke as he studied Tobias.

"You look like you're a little worn out yourself. You slept at all since we spoke?"

"Nope. Like you said, no time." Tobias reached into his shirt pocket, removed a white, folded document he'd stuffed there earlier. "Here's the warrant." Tobias handed the paperwork to Dursk, then said, "Anything I can do to help out? Speed this up a little?"

The man gave him a surprised look, seemed to consider the request for a moment, then said,

"I'm sure you're fully capable, detective, but that won't be necessary." He opened the warrant, glanced at it briefly. "Appears to cover what we talked about last night on the phone ..."

"It has all the information you'll need. The search is centered around any files or transactions directly related to Hassenback's activities on their network. I'm sure that'll net you what you need to find Eduardo. In case Eduardo uses the same ISP, the magistrate wrote the warrant to give you the latitude to follow a reasonable evidentiary chain, which means once you find him, you have the freedom to search records of his activities as well. Sort of an electronic version of Plain View. Eduardo's definitely the missing piece here. We find him, we can put a lot of the unknowns together and perhaps run this to a quick resolution. All depends on us getting to him before our unknown does. He dies, our hopes for solving anything with respect to this mess just might die with him."

"You really think your perp is going to hit this pedo?" Dursk asked, inflecting the interrogative with the same doubt Tobias had heard over the phone the night before.

"Yes, I do. No mistake, he's after a big fish, and he's been chomping on the small ones to get to it. We figure Hassenback was just one in a line, and Eduardo is his next most likely target."

"You'd think there'd be a trail of bodies if he were killing a bunch of pedos though, right? Where's the other db's?"

"Unknown. We figure he's been operating in a different part of the country until now. We've been looking into it, but you know what it's like trying to piece together information from disparate agencies."

Dursk gave a bitter snort of disgust. "Tell me about it. Well, as they say, we're burnning daylight."

Tobias gave Dursk a firm handshake, then turned away. As he opened his car door, the lieutenant called after him.

"Hey, sometimes these things go really slow; sometimes they go really fast. If everyone in here cooperates and the storage gods are smiling down on us, this can be finished in about a half a day. Keep your fingers crossed!"

"Would it help out if I sacrificed a yak in the parking lot here?"

Dursk laughed. "I'd like to see that, but you'd probably get us into trouble with PETA, in spite of your good intentions."

"Yeah, you're probably right. I'll leave it to you then." Tobias got into his car, closed the door, and slumped in the driver's seat. He was tired, hadn't really slept since the night before. He decided to just stay put, catch a quick cat nap. He figured it was going to be a long, busy day. It would turn out to be the longest, busiest day of his life.

Eduardo sat on the floor shaking so badly he looked as though he were vibrating. Sweat was beading on the top of his forehead, the hair closest to his face starting to dampen and mat against his skin. He looked up at Clark, eyes wide open, filled with fear.

"I don't know nothing about that. I swear."

"Then why're you sweating and shaking, Eduardo? You know what that tells me?"

Eduardo hung his head so his eyes were looking directly at the floor.

"What?"

"It tells me you do know something. And I need that information. Make no mistake, you will tell me. We can do this the hard way or the easy way. It's totally up to you."

Eduardo appeared to think about what Clark had said, then gave him an incredulous look. "The hard way? What do you mean? Beat it out of me? That's police brutality, something you can go to the slammer for, man. No way you gonna risk that." Emboldened by his assertion, his expectation of reality, Eduardo stood up defiantly, then patted his pants pockets.

"I need a smoke. And you need to go. I don't need no more of your shit."

Clark said nothing, just got up from the couch and approached the man.

"Good. Get your stuff and get out of here. You want to talk to me again, you can do it through my parole officer ... or better yet, my lawyer!"

Clark feigned chastisement as he assessed Eduardo's posture: ram-rod straight, hands down to his sides. Perfect. The course of the whole interrogation was now going to have to change, and he'd need Eduardo in that position to quiet him down. One of the worst possible ways to quiet a guy is to hit him in the face. Hit a guy in the face, it hurts, and more times than not he'll yell out in both surprise and pain, cause a commotion that can be overheard. Punch a guy in the solar-plexus, though, you knock the wind right out of him, no air to scream. He figured a quick gut shot would not only empty Eduardo's lungs, it'd probably knock him out. That was good too because the rest of the interrogation would have to be conducted with Eduardo restrained and under his complete control. He stepped close to Eduardo and extended his right hand.

"Guess you're right. No hard feelings, huh?"

As Eduardo reached for his hand, Clark stepped forward with his left foot and landed a right-handed blow to the center of the man's stomach. Eduardo doubled over in surprise. All the air in his lungs gone, he struggled for oxygen and consciousness. He took a weak step forward, his legs buckled beneath him, and he fell to the floor.

 

Clark stooped to lift Eduardo off the floor. He was rolling his head from side to side and breathing irregularly. His face was beet red. He was trying to moan, but he couldn't, nothing but silent, choked gasps escaping his mouth. Clark carried him to the kitchen and dumped him into a cheap plastic chair. As Eduardo slumped, eyes struggling to focus, Clark went to his briefcase, opened it, and retrieved the roll of duct tape he'd purchased the night before. He returned to the chair, pulled off four strips, each 18 inches in length, and used them to secure the man's arms and legs. He then pulled off a ten inch strip to cover his mouth, pressing it firm against his skin to seal it shut.

As Eduardo finally drifted out of consciousness, Clark donned his paper surgical over-garments and then searched for a vacuum. Given the condition of Eduardo's floors, he wasn't holding out much hope. To his surprise, however, he found one in the lone closet in the short hallway that lead to Eduardo's bedroom at the back of the apartment. It was an older model Hoover with a bag, which was more than he could ask for. He removed the vacuum from the closet, paused and considered the floor again. It was a disaster, probably held DNA from dozens of people, perhaps even the previous tenant. If he'd shed any DNA at all, it undoubtedly amounted to very little and was more than likely lost in a sea of contaminants that would take the police more time than it was worth to filter through. He thought briefly of simply leaving the floor the way it was, then rejected the idea. No sense in taking unnecessary risks. He unraveled the cord, plugged in the vacuum, and spent the next 15 minutes removing layers grime from Eduardo's floors.

Satisfied he'd sufficiently scrubbed the carpet of most of his biological markers, Clark took the vacuum to the kitchen, unzipped the cover that held its bag, and removed it. He searched for and found a steak knife in a kitchen drawer and used it to slice the bag open lengthwise. He then retrieved his bottle of sodium hydroxide and carefully turned and sprayed every piece debris in the bag, saturating it with the caustic liquid. He placed the bag in Eduardo's oven, set the temperature to 250, and let its contents bake. After a half hour or so, he'd be done with the rest of the interrogation, the debris would be dry, and he could burn it in the kitchen sink.

He returned the solution to his briefcase, trading it for his toolkit. He unzipped the black leather pouch and removed a small Phillips screwdriver, then employed it to remove the hard drive from Eduardo's computer. He then searched the desk, found a secondary storage device and a notebook that appeared to hold the names and email addresses of various people. He took what he found, stuffed it into a heavy plastic garbage bag, and placed it next to the front door of the apartment. He then turned his attention to a general search of the house for any remaining electronic and tactile information, finding nothing he could use. By the time he'd had wrapped up his search, almost an entire hour had passed.

 

He once again turned his attention to Eduardo, gave him a few open-handed taps to the side of the face.

"Eduardo. You in there?"

Eduardo's eyes fluttered. He looked around, then noticed he was secured to a chair. He tried to talk, but nothing but a low-pitched, vibrating mumble moved through the duct tape over his mouth. Clark looked down at him, put his forefinger in front of his lips.

"I'll take the tape off, but you have to be quiet, okay?"

Eduardo nodded his head.

"First, there's something I need to set straight with you Eduardo." Clark took the phony badge from his pocket, shoved it in front of the man's face.

"This is a fake badge, Eduardo. I got it from a toy store. I'm not a cop."

Eduardo simply stared at him, eyes wide.

"I was a father and a husband once. I had a loving family. But that was all taken away from me. My daughter, her name was Jessica, she was abducted and taken to the place I asked you about. She was raped repeatedly. They used her up, Eduardo, and then they dumped my baby girl in the desert to die. You understand what I'm saying?"

Eduardo nodded his head, his breathing beginning to turn ragged.

"Good. Because you're going to tell me where this place is, Eduardo. Here's how it's going to work: if you yell out, I break a rib. If I think you're lying, I break a rib. You ever had a broken rib?"

Eduardo shook his head, beads of sweat forming again on his brow, beginning to trickle down the sides of his face.

"Let me tell you, it doesn't feel good. Every time you shout, sneeze, or cough, it feels like someone is sticking an ice pick in your side. It's not pleasant. Understand?"

Eduardo nodded.

"Good. Now, look at me. Focus, Eduardo!" Clark tapped the side of Eduardo's face again, then grabbed his chin with a gloved hand and angled it up toward him. "I need you to do me a favor. I need you to calm down. You're not going to do either of us any good if you wind up passing out by hyperventilating. This can be really easy. You tell me what I need to know, and I won't break any of your ribs."

Eduardo nodded. His breathing was still labored, but Clark could tell he was working to get it under control. He reached for the duct tape, and gently removed it from his face.

Eduardo took a ragged gasp of air, broken by intermittent sobs. He hung his head, took two deep breaths.

"I'm so sorry about your daughter. Please believe me. But I had nothing to do with it."

"I'm really not sure how to respond to that, Eduardo. And it's really not what I want to hear from you. Where's the place they took her?"

Tears began welling up in Eduardo's eyes, his face molded into an expression of panic.

"They have a van. All I really know is where they pick you up. That's all."

"Where?"

"A place called Baker out on the Mojave Highway. It's a dump, nothing there but fast food, a couple of gas stations, and trash. Like I said, they pick up visitors in vans, they're a sandy yellow color with markings on the sides that say Desert Tours." Eduardo stopped, sucked in his breath, and started sobbing again.

"Keep talking, Eduardo."

"Why? You're gonna kill me anyway, right? Oh fuck, fuck, fuck. I can't believe this!" Eduardo shook in his chair, head down, quietly sobbing.

"I haven't decided what to do with you yet." Clark answered honestly. He really didn't know. The Hassenback kill had been easy, in the heat of passion. Eduardo was different. He'd engaged Eduardo, wasn't really sure if he could kill him now. Could he?

"I went to the place that killed your daughter. You're out for revenge, and I don't blame you. You're planning on killing them, right? So why would you let me live?" His voice was ragged and stuttered, beginning to build in volume and pitch.

"For information. Remember what I told you about crying out, Eduardo. Control yourself. It's not my intention to hurt you, but if I have to, I will. Now keep talking."

Eduardo put his face in his hands, wiped his tears, then took several deep breaths.

"Okay, okay. They do their pick-ups just to the east of a Shell station in town. They have a couple of vans there. I arrived at the same time as two other guys. The pick-up times are specific. That's how they operate. They make it look like a desert tour that leaves at certain times of the day. So I parked my car and walked up to one of the vans, the one with a driver in it. He got out, and we waited for the other guys. When we were all assembled, the driver made all of us show ID, and then he made us pay. After that, he put us in the back of the van. It was windowless, so none of us could see out. Some of the roads were paved, some were dirt. After about 30 minutes of driving, we were there."

"How much of it was dirt road?"

Eduardo took another shaky, deep breath, looked up to the ceiling, mulled the question for a moment, then said, "Less than half. Maybe a third of the time we spent on dirt."

Clark had passed Baker before, knew it was a small town out in the middle of nowhere. He did a quick calculation, figuring the average speed of the van to be no more than 40 miles per hour for the 30 minute trip. Given that, the place could be anywhere within a 1200 square mile radius around the town. Not good enough.

"You're going to have to do better than that. Which came first, dirt or pavement?"

"Pavement." Eduardo coughed, then retched. Clark stepped back for a moment, let the man recover.

"Tell me about the drive on the highway. Only one of two ways you can go out of Baker, east or west. There's a grade heading west, flat land to the east. What do you remember about that? "

Eduardo seemed to focus on the empty space directly in front of him, then answered the question. "I remember. It was flat at first, then a grade."

Clark walked to the couch, grabbed his laptop, launched his browser, and went to Google Maps. He looked at the stretch of highway leading in and out of Baker from west to east. As he suspected, the van had to have been moving east. The west side of Baker was hilly, the eat relatively flat. Most of the trip, too, had to have been on pavement, perhaps between 15 and 20 miles of it. He centered the map at a location 17 miles east of Baker and zoomed. He found what he was looking for. The only place east of town that had access roads into the surrounding desert was right at a place called Halloran Springs Road. There was nothing else for miles. Clark returned his attention to Eduardo. He had one more question.

"When you turned off the highway, did you make a left or a right?"

"Left. I'm sure we took a left. I remember looking out the front window of the van. We were heading toward a hill. There are no hills to the south."

Clark smiled, walked back to his laptop and closed it. He put the computer back in his briefcase, then took the briefcase and set it next to the front door of the apartment, beside the plastic bag he'd placed there earlier. Then he turned and stood in front of the door.

"Thank you Eduardo. You've been a great help." Clark held his ground for a moment. He figured he could just clean up and leave. He could let Eduardo live. He didn't really want to kill the man. He was different from Hassenback. Because of his conversation with him, Eduardo had become human to him, a person. His life for silence. He wondered if it would work, wondered if he should take a risk like that. He continued to stand in front of the door paralyzed with indecision. Simultaneous thoughts regarding what he should do began flooding his mind, churning up conflicting recommendations that screamed within him, culminating in a cacophony of high-pitched white noise. It pierced his head, sending tension from his temples to the back of his skull. He closed his eyes, focused on his breathing. After a moment, the noise subsided, replaced with an urgent warning: he was wasting time and he had to get the hell out. He opened his eyes, looked at Eduardo. He knew what he had to do.

Eduardo struggled in his chair as Clark walked toward him. "Hey! Are you going to leave me like this?"

"I guess I can untie you. No sense you being in that chair anymore." Clark approached to within a foot of where the man was sitting. He looked relaxed, much more so than he'd been all morning.

"Look straight up at the ceiling."

Eduardo seemed to not understand the request, but, happy he was about to be released, complied anyway. The stretch made his Adam's Apple stand out from the rest of his neck like a overgrown knot on a small piece of soft wood. It was a perfect target, and Clark delivered the blow with enough force to crush his larynx and everything behind it. Eduardo had no idea what hit him, and in fact lost consciousness almost immediately.

Clark moved quickly to the oven, took out the vacuum bag, and put it in the sink. He then found one of Eduardo's lighters and  used it to set the contents on fire. As small flames danced and lapped up at him, he removed his paper outerwear, shredded the garment, and slowly added the ripped pieces to the fire. By the time the contents in the sink had burned, Eduardo was dead, asphyxiated by a crushed larynx. He returned to the front of the apartment, put his ear to the door, and listened. Hearing nothing, he picked up his briefcase and the plastic bag, opened the door, and walked into the early morning darkness.

Gerkowitcz took his customary position at the head of an expensive solid oak conference table, which was perfectly centered in relation to the meeting room's four towering walls. Its surface sported a shiny, cappuccino-colored finish that reflected everything around it like a large, dark-hued mirror. He could've simply purchased a couple of card tables for the room, or even one of those cheap pressed-wood jobs that low-class bank he worked for seemed to like so much. But he didn't. As far as he was concerned, he was sitting in a war room; and a war room, in his opinion at least, should be dressed up with appropriate furnishings to remind its occupants of the grave nature of the business that's conducted within its confines. You can't ask a guy to do great things, ask him to work his mind until he bleeds through his eyes, and then park him in front of a card table. So he spent a lot of money outfitting the room, then followed that with a lot of time thinking about its function. That's because function is every bit as important as form, even more important in his opinion. Function lives in the details, and the details with respect to the room implied leveraging available space to optimally facilitate planning. When he put the room together, therefore, he decided each wall would serve a different purpose.

The wall behind him was used for planning asset acquisitions, the most critical and dangerous part of his operation. It sported an eight foot square map of the greater metropolitan area, marked up in several locations with red circles. Tacked into the wall next to the circles were three-by-five cards that contained brief product synopses. Affixed to the cards were smaller pieces of paper, each of which presented a number between one and ten, signifying the product's desirability.

The wall to his right was almost completely covered by white-board. Marking up its surface in black, blue, and red were various notes from previous meetings and ad-hoc lists containing action items for future ones. Scrawled in red on the far right side of the board was the beginning of a command hierarchy he'd started developing during his previous visit. It reflected his first formulations for growing his operation: get the chain of command down, recognize the holes in manpower, then hire accordingly.

Affixed to the wall in front of him was counter space that spanned the width of the room. Created from a single slab of Italian Grigio Carnico limestone, its surface looked like an inky black sky, uniformly broken by sundry-shaped clouds of a rich cocoa color. The top of the counter was sparsely populated with a toaster oven, a microwave, and a gourmet coffee maker. Below the counter were cupboards containing supplies, mostly small snacks and caffeine to help keep energy levels high during the sometimes marathon-long meetings he held. Built into the wall above the counter was a rectangular display case that held various keep-sakes collected from current and former residents of the shelter.

On the wall to his left were several poster boards, each of which contained a summary, task list, and progress status of projects specifically related to ongoing management issues. The poster boards were presented in order of priority, the most important of which were tacked on the wall nearest his position at the table. The poster board closest to him today detailed a to-do list relating to the security enhancements for the property.

Gerkowitcz leaned back in his chair, gave a nod to the two people in the room with him.

"I've been informed we've had some product turn-over."

He looked to his left as he spoke, directly at Dave Manheim, his Chief of Operations. Manheim was a frail-looking man of about fifty with delicate, bird-like features, grey, collar-length hair that sometimes dripped grease, and a long hawkish nose, on top of which sat a heavy pair of wire-frame glasses. Gerkowitcz hadn't really liked the man when he first interviewed him, thought he was a little too effeminate-looking, like a lot of those queers up in San Francisco looked. In spite of his outward appearance, though, he had a great resume, the most interesting highlight of which was his role as a Deputy COO for a Fortune 500 company. In the end, the qualifications had buried the reservations, and he'd hired the man. Gerkowitcz nodded gravely. "Right. We can deal with that later. Let's get some other items off the table first and then plan an asset acquisition after that." He then looked to his right and asked,

"What's the status of our ground security?"

The question was directed at his Chief of Security, Frank Malone, who was sitting directly across from Manheim and presenting an exaggerated study in contrast when compared to the diminutive man. Where Manheim was not much more than a leafless twig, bent out of shape from time and malady, Malone was an enormous mountain, a picture of health packed into a body that would tower over most. Standing at just over six foot five and carrying about 250 pounds of chiseled muscle, Malone looked every bit like a soldier-for-hire. While Gerkowitcz and Manheim were decked out in casual business attire, the only style of dress each had ever known, Malone sported pressed olive greens, jump boots, and a buzz-cut. He wiped a trickle of sweat from his brow with a giant hand, then ran it down the length of his bulldog face, stopping momentarily to massage the stubble on his chin.

Given the proposed plan, the spread of the devices will be no more than 25 feet all the way around the most important parts of the property. That maximizes our coverage, minimizes our risk, and necessitates the extra expenditure."
