Chapter 6

 

 

Tobias leaned back in his patio chair, soaking up sun and breathing in the scent of barbecued steak wafting over the yard. As he took a sip of wine, he thought about the walk-through he and Sanchez had conducted at the Hassenback residence. Something was nagging at him. It was there, right at the periphery of recognition, just out of sight. Something he missed. And just like trying to focus on dim objects blanched by overpowering light, he knew if he concentrated too long on what was bothering him, nothing would come. So he'd glance at it sideways, hoping whatever he missed would emerge from obscurity and reveal itself in due course. He took another sip of his wine and wondered how it was possible for anyone in his right mind to be a vegetarian with that kind of smell floating about. He smiled to himself. It had occurred to him on more than one occasion that perfume-makers were probably missing out on a fortune in sales by avoiding the obvious: steak-scented perfume. He could picture the ad: Ladies, you want to bring out the carnivore in your man? Send him into a feeding frenzy with T-bone After Dark. He shook his head and laughed.

"Papa?"

Sarah toddled to her grandfather and looked up. She was a beautiful little girl: blonde hair, a perpetually messy face, large green eyes, and a joyous laugh that made everyone around her smile. And she idolized her papa. She looked at him with awe and wonder. He was, after all, one of those guys who wore a badge and got to chase bad guys with his shoot gun. Just like on television. She crouched a little and got into a playful stance meant to communicate to her grandfather she was ready to play the catch-me-if-you-can game. Of course, he always did. He was fast for an old guy.

"Two …. Go!" She squealed, and ran away from Tobias, looking back all the way to ensure he was following. Tobias placed his wine on the patio and bolted from his chair. In less than five steps he grabbed her and scooped her high into the air. She shrieked with delight.

"Papa! You always catch me too fast!" She admonished.

"Well, maybe you need faster shoes, pumpkin!" He replied, tickling Sarah as she kicked and squealed in his arms. As he was about to put her down for a best of two out of three, a muffled buzz interrupted their play, announcing an incoming call. He really didn't want to answer, but, being lead detective, he guessed he probably didn't have much of a choice. Something about open murder cases the brass up top didn't like. He put Sarah down.

"Just a moment, sweetie. Anne?" Anne walked from a small crowd that included her mother, her husband, a couple of their neighbors, and the family dog. Sarah fussed a bit, not wanting to part from her grandpa, but Anne offered her a cookie, which quickly refocused her attention.

"Tobias." He said, somehow angry about receiving a call he should have expected.

"Yo, Tobias! How's the picnic with the family?" Sanchez sounded happy, and Tobias was pretty sure why: the kid knew family gatherings decompressed him, made the following work week much less anal than usual.

"Great, Sanchez. Wish you could be here. Whatcha got?"

 "Nothing at all El Capitan!" Sanchez replied joyfully. "We got zero. Whoever took out Mr. Hassenback did a really good job covering his ass!"

"Yeah, I guess we expected that."

"It's good news, though, right boss?"

"I guess. We still have some open roads to go down on this one, though, kid."

"Can't we just chalk this one up to natural causes? We can say he just slipped and twisted his neck."

"Sanchez, have you been drinking?"

"Naw, but if you got some burgers left, I'll consider drinking some of that nasty old red wine with you."

"Yeah, come on over. We got plenty. We can talk a little shop about this case, too. Something's been bothering me."

"Something's always bothering you!"

"This is a little different. I think we may have overlooked something. How close are you?"

"Just happen to be pulling into your driveway as we speak. See you in less than one minute. Out."

Tobias slapped his phone shut, put it in his pocket, then found his daughter and reclaimed his granddaughter. With Sarah in his arms, he walked through the sliding glass door at the rear of his two-story Victorian house, through the kitchen, and made his way to the front of the house just as the doorbell rang. He pulled the door open and greeted his partner.

Sanchez saw Tobias had Sarah so he made a show of flashing his badge and saying, "Officer Sanchez reporting for duty, sir!"

Sarah looked at Sanchez and giggled. "Uncle Juan, you're silly!" She raised her arms up and was unceremoniously transferred from grandfather to adopted uncle.

As Sanchez carried Sarah into the house, Tobias closed the door and glanced down at the bank of light switches on the wall next to the coat closet.

"Light switches," he said.

"Pardon?" Sanchez replied.

"Light switches."

"What about them, boss? You okay?"

"You ever have that thing happen to you where you're in the kitchen trying to find some sugar for your coffee and you just can't?"

"Yeah, when it's right there in front of you but you can't see it."

"Yeah. Light switches. I'm thinking about light switches right now. Something about them is bothering me. It's right there for me, but I just can't reach it."

Tobias looked again at the bank of switches. The fog cleared a little and the obvious implication burst through him with sweeping clarity.

"You're going to have to grab something on the road, son. We have to go now."

"Okay." Sanchez replied, as if he were half expecting for the evening to be derailed.

It was a reaction Tobias had come to expect from the kid. He'd always incorporated a little Zen into his detective work, letting the scenes pose the questions themselves instead of forcing issues. He found it an efficient way to process them. But he also found the answers sometimes came to him at less-than-convenient times. Like now. But that didn't seem to bother Sanchez.

"Are you going to tell me what got your attention?" Asked Sanchez.

"Tell you in the car. Maybe we can get there and back before anybody notices we're gone."

He'd ensured Carri got home safely, then returned to his weekly and immediately put on a pot of coffee. Some pot. It had enough capacity for two cups. Certainly not enough to push him into the early hours of the morning, and from the amount of data he had in front of him, he fully expected it to be a long night. He'd given some thought to purchasing a decent coffee maker from one of those full service retail stores, but he figured the trouble of setting it up wherever he went and keeping it in his truck when he was without a place to stay made the convenience more of a pain than anything else. As he waited for the noisy little machine to belch out its brew, he sat down at the table and considered the work in front of him.

It might seem counterintuitive, but he'd learned from experience long ago that jumping straight into an image search was unproductive. Most pedophiles had gigabytes of images and videos stored on their drives, and gigabytes more deleted, sent to their grave in an electronic circle of life: stars would fall out of favor, failing to elicit the sexual high they once had, to be replaced by newer, more appealing images. But the deleted files weren't really gone. Far from it. In his former life, Clark had demonstrated that to his students every year in his Introduction to Computer Forensics class.

The lifecycle of an electronic file isn't complicated: it starts and ends on the hard drive, placed there by its read-write heads under the direction of the operating system. The file is typically broken into many parts and scattered over the surface of the hard drive, embedded into areas called sectors. The operating system knows how to retrieve the disjointed file because its parts are entered in something called the File Allocation Table (FAT), which keeps the name of the file and the address of the sectors in which it is contained.

When a file is deleted, it isn't destroyed. It's simply renamed in the FAT, it's former name prefixed by a special character that tells the operating system the file is no longer needed and may be written over. The contents of the file, though, remain on the hard drive, and there it will stay until a process such as defragmentation writes over it completely.

Defragmenting optimizes a hard drive by moving files around on its platters so they occupy contiguous sectors. In other words, instead of being scattered all over the hard drive, the logical pieces that make up the file are placed side-by-side in neat little data chains. This makes access to the files quicker, as the read write head doesn't have to move all over the hard drive just to retrieve them. The rewriting of the files during a defrag, especially if the hard drive is already quite full, writes over sectors marked for deletion, and this effectively removes most traces of any potentially unwanted garbage. If the hard drive is not so full, then a defrag won't do much to destroy the scattered pieces of the deleted files.

Every time he gave the lecture, Clark's students would offer up their opinion that formatting a hard drive would remove all traces of all files. But this is not the case, either. Most of the time, formatting simply resets the File Allocation Table, leaving the sectors behind for potential forensic inspection. He'd always end his lectures on file durability by admonishing that there were really only two ways to absolutely remove them from a hard drive: one, fill it with random garbage until every sector on the drive is used in its entirety; or, two, simply destroy the drive. If the goal is to destroy all of the data on a hard drive, demolishing it is the best option.

A comprehensive image search, then, not only implicates files recognized by the FAT, it implicates an exploration of the graveyard of previously-deleted files as well. Which means interrogating every single sector of a hard drive with a piece of software that will put them back together, much like dozens of paper documents, ripped to shreds, might be taped together to get as close to their original state as possible. And a search like that using his equipment could take days. But there are other ways to start file searches without having to interrogate them directly. Clark had found searching text-based material on storage media was a much more efficient way to begin his forensic work. It was faster, gave him more meaningful information, and typically pointed him to specific images that had relevance with respect to his investigation. He connected the imaged drive to his laptop, then entered his first-pass keyword search list: meet, attractive, young, conference, location, contact, organizer, goal, plan, safe, legitimate, investment, opportunity, review, asset, mature, plan, market, years, old, entertain, return, fresh, in-house, guarantee, private, host, and any occurrences of the numbers zero through 255.

Clark clicked the search icon, got up from the flimsy two dollar chair the weekly had somehow declared furniture, went to a rickety dresser that doubled as a kitchen table, and poured himself his first cup of coffee. As his laptop crunched and churned, his thoughts unexpectedly turned to Carri. He'd regretted his encounter with Chuck, but in the end, he was glad he'd been there to help out. If he hadn't been, he was certain Carri would have wound up in the hospital, or worse. Chuck was clearly a jealous ex who wanted nothing more than to subjugate or hurt her.

He'd spotted Chuck about ten minutes after they'd begun their caravan toward Carri's home. The girls decided to take Lisa's car to help deflect Chuck's attention in case he followed, adding reasonable confusion regarding who was in the vehicle and where they were heading. As soon as Clark had spotted him, he'd slowed and allowed the girls to pass. He then lagged behind them, placing himself midway between their vehicle and Chuck's in a one-mile long caravan. Fortunately, Carri appeared to live somewhere off Foothill Blvd, which narrowed to two lanes as it approached Crescenta Highlands. Clark had been hoping for a lane reduction, and as soon as it happened, he stepped on his brakes, honked once, and flashed his high-beams to message the girls to keep going. As he slowed to 35 miles per hour, Chuck raced forward, preparing to pass. Clark swerved left, denied passage. Chuck slowed, regrouped, tried to pass again, but Clark positioned his vehicle to prevent Chuck from going around him.

The only thing that had made Clark a little nervous about this new interaction with Chuck was the possibility catching the attention of a police cruiser, which was the last thing he needed. As in the bar, Clark had no reason to fear the man following him. Chuck certainly had the faster vehicle: he drove a newer-model Ford Mustang, pimped out in low-profile tires, chrome wheels, tinted windows, and a bright yellow paint job. The overall presentation of the car exuded oozed compensation for some personal deficiency on the part of the driver. Clark, on the other hand, drove a 1996 F150. His ride was certainly not as fast as Chuck's, but in the event of an accidental or intentional collision, he liked his chances. Clark gave a quick tug on his seatbelt to ensure he was properly belted in, glanced in his rear view mirror, then slowed down a little more. Chuck slowed to match his speed, then, after another mile, finally stopped, turned around, and headed off in the other direction. Clark finally allowed himself to focus on what was ahead of him and saw the reason for Chuck's change of heart: the girls were no longer in sight, more than likely lost in a maze of neighborhood streets or perhaps even garaged. Chuck knew his chances for finding them were almost zero, so he had abandoned the chase. Clark continued on Foothill Avenue until it hooked up with the 210, then headed back to his weekly, arriving there a little after nine in the evening.

Clark finished his cup of coffee, poured himself another, and returned to his computer. It had not completed searching the files and emails on the imaged drive, but it already had a list of over three dozen hits. Clark scrolled through them, selected one that looked promising, and began reading.
