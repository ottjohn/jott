Chapter 8

 

 

Look at the badge of a Los Angeles police officer, and you'll see the iconic edifice many associate with that department's headquarters. But it's not. That building is actually City Hall, and it has the singular distinction of being the tallest base-isolated structure in the world. Base isolation is the science of separating a building from its foundation, inserting a carefully-engineered layer between to dampen surface movement, which theoretically protects the building above from tremors that would otherwise destroy it.

The actual LAPD headquarters building, unfortunately, was not built with such a careful consideration to the science of plate tectonics and their seemingly whimsical and catastrophic movements. Constructed in 1955, the Parker Center building, located at 100 North Los Angeles Street, was erected at a cost of six million dollars, fully two million under budget. When built, the eight-story, 400,000 square foot building dedicated an entire floor to what was at the time one of the largest crime labs in the world. While functional, it certainly was not an illustration of architectural grace and style, which is probably the reason it had never really made it in Hollywood. After 50 years of service, time and safety concerns crept up on the old building, so the Golden State put together Proposition Q, a public facilities safety bond measure. Through that bond, enough funds were accrued to construct a new building. At 10 stories, 500,000 square feet, and a cost of 437 million dollars, the new headquarters is nothing less than spectacular.

Instead of the stuffiness imposed by small offices and tight hallways, the new building offers an open floor plan and natural lighting, accomplished by a mostly glass exterior that insinuates itself into the space and light of the atmosphere around it. The exterior of the building presents an edifice that looks more like an art museum or a large university library than a structure serving as police headquarters. It is at once a study in oppositions of materials and shapes: concrete, steel, and glass provide the substance for an expression dominated by rectangles, triangles, and asymmetric segmentation. The public entrance offers the most dramatic view of the building: the right side is dressed up in concrete slabs, a thin ribbon of glass centered vertically near its right-most edge. The left side of the building succumbs stunningly and dramatically to a glass exterior that appears to rush into the building from the right and bounce back out on the left, protruding from the roofline by a good 50 feet, just far enough to present an image of City Hall in its glimmering reflective surface.

Tobias and Sanchez walked into the public entrance of the new LAPD headquarters, took an elevator to the tenth floor, and walked into one of the building's many conference rooms. In the center of the room was an oblong conference table sporting a pressed wood surface. At the center of the table was a speaker phone, wired up to six microphones distributed evenly around its circumference. At the far right of the table was a small console that  controlled the room's multimedia equipment. There were six chairs around the table, each composed of what looked like dark gray netted canvas, which gave them the appearance of tiny, high-tech hammocks. There were two walls made entirely of whiteboard and two floor-to-ceiling windows that gave a spectacular view of the Los Angeles skyline. Tobias took in the opulence with a mixture of confusion and appreciation, then glanced at Sanchez as if to say, we're not in Pasadena anymore. It was 10:15 in the morning.

By 10:30, all of the principals for the meeting had arrived. Lieutenant Jacobsen from the fifth floor made the introductions: Jacobsen was simply there to facilitate fact-finding for the purpose of determining the best strategy for identifying the person or persons who'd murdered Zach Hassenback. He sat at the front of the table, a solid wall of whiteboard behind him. To his left, sat Dr. Randal Kabinski, a staff psychologist who specialized in mental profiling of suspects and victims of violent crimes. Directly behind Kabinski and a little to his left was the projection of Special Agent Rathman, one of the many on-call FBI profilers located at the Los Angeles field office located on Wilshire. After the introductions, Jacobsen pressed the red record button in front of him, then clasped his hands together.

"As part of standard procedure, I'm going to record this meeting. Obviously, feel free to take notes. A transcript of this meeting will be available immediately upon its termination." Then to Tobias, "Detective Tobias, I understand your team is having some difficulty digging up solid forensic evidence for the Hassenback murder. Is this correct?"

"Yes, sir. The intruder appears to have gone to great lengths to eliminate any possible sources of contamination, which has left us forensically challenged. Whoever this guy was, he apparently has no respect for departments like ours. Typically, we require a driver's license or at least a confession note to get started, and even then we sometimes have problems solving our cases."

The others around the table looked at Tobias and took the self-deprecating jab at his position with good humor. Tobias had visited several times with both Lieutenant Jacobsen and Dr. Kabinski in the past, and he knew they both enjoyed his off-center perspectives of the circumstances that sometimes plagued investigations. The projected image of Agent Rathman, however, looked a little perplexed by Tobias's flippancy.

"What do you actually know so far?" He asked.

The tone of the question made Tobias's neck muscles tense a little. A lot of the time, he knew the FBI could be very helpful with cases like this; at other times, though, especially when you didn't have a lot of information to work with, they could be real pricks. Tobias looked to Sanchez, who'd tagged along as an observer, gave him short dead-pan look, then turned toward the camera mounted in the wall above the agent's projected image.

"What we have, Agent Rathman, is a lot of nothing. Our techs spent the better part of two days on site, did everything but take duct tape to the whole mess. Every single print lifted from inside of the house belonged to the vic. There's very little collected fiber that cannot be attributed to materials already in the house. The fiber not matching what's in the house amounts to little more than trace amounts, most of which is more than likely contamination from materials brought in underfoot …"

Rathman interrupted, "at this point, detective, what we really need are statements of facts and observations, not quick conclusions from your individual analysis, notwithstanding their underlying well meaning." Rathman paused for effect, staring out of the wall like a god pressed in whiteboard, then said, "Continue, please."

Tobias contemplated Agent Rathman's image on the wall. He looked like a college professor, dressed in a cheap tan jacket that was unbuttoned to show a crumpled light blue tie swimming atop a starchy white shirt, the buttons of which were feeling the undue stress of an expanded midsection. His face was scrunched as if in deep thought, he had probably two days growth on this chin, a bulbous nose, furrowed brows, and a heavy pair of thick glasses that obscured his eyes and weighed down over his face. The entire assembly had the effect of making him look almost intelligent.

"That's really it, sir, with respect to physical contamination," said Tobias.

"You said almost all of the fiber collected could be matched back to materials in the house?" Asked Rathman.

"That's correct."

"Did your lab test all of the clothes in the residence?" Rathman pushed his glasses up his nose with meaty fingers.

"Negative. We tested a sample of them. We would have tested all of them, but by the time we determined the probable size of the assailant, we discontinued."

"Okay, so there's solid evidence to suggest a wide disparity in size between assailant and vic?"

Tobias noticed Rathman looked bored, as if he didn't really appreciate having to rehash facts already spelled out in a report each of them had received and already read over. No one really did. But it was necessary, and it sometimes revealed small holes in procedure or flaws in strategy with respect to contaminant collection. Different perspectives on the same data often lead to fresh directions in pursuit, which was the reason they were all there in the first place.

"Correct." Tobias answered. "From the damage caused by the forward roll on Hassenback's neck, we estimated the assailant must be well over 200 pounds, probably somewhere between 220 and 240."

Rathman switched from playing with his glasses to playing with his right earlobe, gave a small sigh that signified he knew his line of questioning was probably less than useful and most likely not going to reveal any gaping holes in procedure, but he continued anyway.

"So the assailant leveraged his weight on a forward roll from behind to break the vertebra at the base of the victim's neck," Rathman stated.

"Most likely," replied Tobias.

"And given the vic's relative size, his age, his position relative to his attacker, and his obvious lack of readiness, your team estimated the likely weight range for the assailant given the amount of damage to the vertibra and the surrounding tissue."

"Correct."

Rathman leaned back in his chair slightly, pushed his glasses back to the top of his nose, then stared out of the wall and said, "Look, I really have no problem with the way the evidence was gathered here. Further, I agree with the height and weight estimates. I looked at the numbers myself. The broken clavicle and bruise to the sternum support a weighted forward roll of the victim's neck. Combined with a urgent forward motion, a guy weighing around 220 could easily cause that kind of damage. Then there's the bruising on the jaw, after the rotational motion on the neck, which happened after the forward motion. It was not digitized, meaning no finger marks. A shorter guy would have left finger marks on the victim's jaw. But there was only a single, long bruise toward the bottom, which indicates the attacker was tall, had to use his thumb to leverage the rotation. Given the numbers taken from the scene, I'd say he's perhaps in the six-three to six-five range. In short, with respect to the physicality of our perp, I really don't have any problems with what you came up with. Let's move on to the lack of material evidence, review what you actually have, and then we'll decide if it amounts to nothing."

Tobias leaned back in his chair, considered reminding Agent Rathman he'd already told him they had nothing in the way of material evidence, thought better of it, and then replied, "From the trace amount of cotton/polyester fiber we retrieved from the body, especially around the jaw, we're certain the attacker was wearing gloves made from those materials. The ubiquity of those types of fibers, however, eliminates them as suspect filters. There was also a lesser amount of grey polyester material at various spots on the carpet that doesn't match any clothing owned by the victim. This material is used mostly in sportswear, like sweat pants and jackets, but its proliferation in the marketplace again eliminates it as a tool we can use to track recent purchases."

Rathman looked at Tobias, asked, "so you did the door-to-door?"

"That's correct. Nobody saw anything. So I guess we can take that to mean nobody saw a really smart big guy running around the neighborhood in a grayish jogging suit either."

Rathman rolled his eyes, shook his head as if personally offended. "What about the vinyl flooring?" He continued. "You cut out a piece,  right?"

"Minor discoloration that amounted to nothing more than chemical garbage, probably a mixture of food coloring, grease, and dog poop. None of the surface area cut away retained anything in the way of fibers or organics that we could use."

"Anything else we're missing from the evidence manifest?" He asked.

"We found one more thing. A thumb drive. I brought it with me this morning. It should be with Computer Forensics now," replied Tobias.

"Where did you find it?" Asked Rathman, the intonation of which asked, Why wasn't this found during the initial processing of the scene?

"We found it behind a wall plate. No one noticed it at first."

"So it obviously belongs to the vic." The big head on the wall turned toward Jacobsen's side of the table. "Any chance we can get that processed and have the results up here this afternoon?"

"Guys in CF are already on it," replied Jacobsen.

 "Well, gentlemen," Rathman said, "looks like this is going to be interesting. Without any available forensic evidence, we're going to have no luck figuring out if this guy's in the system. Further, we have no avenue for tracking him with recent purchases of goods and materials. All we know for certain is the assailant was tall, large, and very smart about what not to leave at a crime scene. I do have one admonishment, however." Rathman looked toward Tobais.

"Enlighten me, sir," Tobias said.

"I know you guys in Pasadena are in love with your little lab down there, and I know it does a pretty good job servicing the outlying communities. In this case, however, it may be lacking the horsepower you need. I would recommend you take whatever forensic evidence you have left and get it processed again at the lab in LAPD headquarters. It is, after all, brand new and the best crime lab on the planet outside of the FBI lab in Virginia."

"Brought in this morning," replied Tobias. He turned, gave Sanchez a look that broadcasted in no uncertain terms to the kid it was going to be a very long day. He had no idea.

Clark glanced at a printout of the document he'd found the night before, felt confident it held an IP address. He took another sip of caffeinated tar, made a face as he swallowed more grounds than coffee. He briefly wondered again if he shouldn't invest in a coffee maker, then dismissed the thought. He took a bite of a protein bar, then set both his coffee and his breakfast down on the dilapidated table that supported his laptop. His computer was still crunching through Hassenback's images. Prolific collectors of pornography. They all were. The numbers in the email leapt from the page. Save for the zip code, there were four of them, and each had a value between zero and 255: 

Rate $209 per month, fee of $158, 85 West La Brea, Suite 109.

That's why he included values from zero to 255 in his document searches. To find IP addresses. Every IP address is composed of a set of four numbers separated by periods, each of the numbers holding values between zero and 255. IP addresses are used to uniquely identify a computer on a network, such as the internet. Most people get around the internet using URL's consisting of domain names, such as google, abcnews, or espn. But domain names are simply alphanumeric abstractions of IP addresses, and in fact have to be swapped out for their associated IP address before a connection to a requested site can even be made. This happens through an entity called a Domain Name Server, and the whole process adds time to the requested transaction. For instance, typing http://206.159.68.112 into the location field of a web browser would result in the website loading more quickly than typing its associated URL built with a fully-qualified domain name, which might look something like http://www.myfavoritewebsite.com.

209.158.85.109. It had been couched innocuously enough, and obviously if he hadn't been looking for it, he wouldn't have found it. Not a chance. The rest of the email more than likely presented an invitation for an exclusive membership to a brothel of sorts staffed with children, all of whom were 12 years of age or under:

San Andreas Bank has cultivated a stable of new, very young funds, all of which have been in the market for 12 years or less … Our funds are all managed in-house, and all of your transactions hosted on site!

 

On the face of it, the message could have been an actual invitation for an investment opportunity with San Andreas Bank. The numeric values making up the IP address could have been nothing more than what they represented. But there were three things that convinced Clark otherwise: one, the message was sent from one pedophile to another, and this is not the kind of conversation these types of people are likely to strike up with one another; two, there is no San Andreas Bank; and, three, the address listed does not exist anywhere in the Los Angeles area. With that in mind, very little scrutiny of key pieces of the text pointed to an obvious conclusion.

While his computer continued to crunch away at searching through and organizing images from the captured hard drive, Clark opened the control panel for his virus protection software and set it to its highest level. Any time a machine is connected to the internet, it's assigned a temporary IP address. That's how the magic behind the internet works: open a website in a browser, and all the documents associated with that site are streamed to the requesting machine, sent from the IP address of the server hosting the site to the IP address of the machine making the request through a browser window. Send a instant message from a one machine to another, the alphanumeric chain is sent from the originating IP address to the destination address. In other words, when a computer is connected to the internet, it participates in both sending and receiving information.

And that opens connected machines to the possibility of attacks from malicious software stored on questionable sites. Computers connecting without protection are ultimately doomed to infection by worms and viruses that do anything from collecting harmless information to making them a destination for storage of stolen or nefarious media. But that's not all the bad news about connecting to the internet: that temporary IP address assigned to the computer can also be used to determine its approximate location, in much the same way a phone number can be used. And the last thing Clark wanted at the moment was to be found, so he launched an inexpensive program called IPLock, which would arbitrarily change his IP address to make it look as though he were surfing from Malaysia or some other obscure part of the world, rather than from a seedy strip just outside of Los Angeles. Electronic prophylactics in place, Clark launched his browser and typed http://209.158.85.109 into its address field, then clicked go.

The web page loaded and presented a well done corporate-looking design, which surprised him. A banner sat at the top of the page with a decent looking logo for the fictional San Andreas Bank on the left followed by a fault line that cracked and winded its way to a pot of cash on the right. Jumbled over the fault line was etched text spelling out the name of the bank. The whole banner was done up in desert shades and captured the Central California geologic hue almost perfectly. Down the left side of the screen was a dark gray rectangular menu. Its components were black with centered, white text. Clark passed his mouse pointer over each item. As he did, their backgrounds turned an eggshell white as the text color changed to an off-black. There were five items in the menu: Home, News, Portfolio, Membership, About Us.

The main body of the page was split into three sections. Its upper leftmost corner held a marketing message that accurately regurgitated the highlights contained in the captured document. Directly to the right of the message was the image of a professional-looking woman. She wore smart-looking tortoise shell glasses supported by a cute nose that separated two crystal-clear blue eyes. She had long brown hair pulled back into a ponytail, a pretty, toothy smile, and she appeared to be contemplating something on a sheet of paper. It was clearly a stock image used by thousands of sites all over the world, most of them probably sales-related. The bottom of the page contained bulleted phrases and words describing the bank's products, each item in the list marked on the left by a tiny, family-oriented image. The page as a whole struck Clark as very professionally done, nothing at all like most of the sites that pedaled the flesh of children, or even adults for that matter. This had to say something about the organization that ran the operation, something that deserved a little more thought.

Clark hovered his mouse pointer over the About Us menu item and clicked. As he expected, his antivirus software dutifully alerted him that an unknown program was requesting permission to write to his hard drive. Clark clicked on the warning, forbidding the execution of the script. It could have been a virus bent on wreaking havoc on his machine, but he really doubted it. The site itself was more than likely a portal through which members communicated and set up appointments, so the script requesting access was probably a program meant for protection against unwanted scrutiny. And if it was a portal, then its users probably paid a lot of money to use its services. Infecting their computers with malicious software was the last thing the operators of the site would want to do in that case. Clark thought about it a little more, figured the script was built to check the IP address of the requesting computer against a manifest of clients approved for access to the site. Clark suspected the program might even have the ability to silently hijack video and voice software on the requesting machine to gain a better understanding of who was visiting and whether the visitor was the owner of an IP on the aforementioned manifest.

He knew he'd probably have luck with just a single menu item, so he clicked it. A half second later, the membership page was presented within the site's content area. It had a single line of text centered both vertically and horizontally within its borders:

Access to this site can only be facilitated by logging on using your sponsor's computer.

Smart. The site was professional-looking, enough so to completely fool casual observers. It was also armed with a script that likely had something to do with controlling which computers and users could functionally attach to it, a gate-keeper so to speak. Finally, the only way a potential member could gain access to the site was through registration from the computer of the source who turned him onto it. Very secure. But that shouldn't be a problem. He'd just have to find Eduardo, the guy who'd sent Hassenback the email in the first place. Then he'd have to hope like hell the man had made a connection with the organization behind the website. Or that he knew of someone who had.

There was nothing else to do with the site for now. He saw it. He was sure about its purpose. He could go no further with it until he located Eduardo. He closed the browser window, then spent the rest of the afternoon looking through the result set returned from the search he unleashed on the copy of Hassenback's former hard drive. The search had returned 14,657 images. Hassenback had archived some of his images in a password-protected folder. His active images were contained in another folder just under the root drive. It was named Projects, and each of the images within the folder was misrepresented by a thumbnail depicting an icon associated with a CAD (Computer Assisted Design) document. Smart for hiding these types of files from the casual observer, not so smart for the want of any kind of CAD software installed on his drive.

Clark assembled a random sample of fifteen percent of the images collected. He then meticulously worked through them, trying to focus on the faces of the adults and children while scrutinizing the image backgrounds for details. He knew he'd more than likely never physically visit the scenes contained in the images, but he studied them anyway: the way rooms were put together, the lack of or inclusion of closet space, the type of electrical outlets, the condition and cost of appliances and electronics, the color and composition of carpeting, the material used on the walls, the quality of decorations. All of this information gave him a sense of where and when the action had occurred: overseas, in country, left coast, right coast, central, upscale neighborhood, urban blight, and the approximate year of the setting. If he did happen to find a concrete lead in one of the images, the ancillary information he looked for could give him an idea about where he'd need to go for a follow-up. It was all very low probability. But he did it anyway. Because there was a chance. And while there was a chance, he worked to foster it into opportunity. It's all he could do.

After several hours of scrutinizing the images he'd selected, Clark worked his way down to the last few. He'd found nothing. But he really hadn't expected a lead. The lion's share of his progress from the very beginning had come from interrogation and document searches, not images. But the value provided by a potential hit on an image kept him going. It was like playing the lottery: he knew he could do it for the rest of his life and find nothing. But if he did, if he found a concrete clue within an image, it would change everything, provide him with details he just couldn't get from documents and interrogations. So whatever time table was required, he'd put in the work.

Clark closed his laptop, then leaned back in his chair. He was tired. He allowed his mind to wonder. He thought about Carri, wondered if she was OK. He wondered when her luck would run out with Chuck, wondered how long it would take the guy to find her and then really make her life miserable. He wasn't really sure why he even cared. His mind was racing and standing still at the same time. Maybe he just needed a diversion. He knew he shouldn't, but he took out the card Carri had given him, flipped open his pay-as-you-go phone, and dialed her number.
