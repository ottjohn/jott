Chapter 2

 

 

Detective Paul Tobias parked his unmarked cruiser on the 1100 block of East Wilshire Avenue and stepped onto the dry, hot pavement. He was in Orange County, just south of Santa Ana, and more than 35 miles north of the Boulevard of the same name made famous by the riots of the sixties and nineties. The air temperature went well beyond uncomfortable and may have even surpassed oppressive. It was like a hot, heavy hand that pushed the entire southwest with an unrelenting preternatural force. Halfway across the spongy roasted blacktop he stopped, removed his camera-equipped PDA, and took a quick snapshot of the property in front of him.

The house was decent enough: a three bedroom ranch tucked into a place called Corner Stone Village. It sat back from the street a good thirty feet, a large bay window prominent above a mess of poorly maintained evergreens. A beige front door stood atop three concrete steps, positioned just to the left of the big window. The door was nested within a small nook, providing an illusion of protection against inclement weather. There appeared to be two bedrooms to the left of the door, each with a small window. The whole facade was done up in very old, off-white, siding that was cracked here and there and quickly approaching a neglected state of misery. The front yard sloped slightly to a barely functional sidewalk. The grass was brown with tinges of yellow. A large Russian elm burst from the middle of the yard. To the right of the property, a single-wide concrete driveway ramped up to an attached garage. The house was pretty typical of those that got their start in the 1950's, during the post-war boom. It would have been an almost peaceful sight if not for the police cruisers, crime-scene tape, and a bustling crew of responders and specialists who were now there working a murder scene.

"So, what have we got here, Sanchez?" Tobias asked the sergeant who'd been one of the first responders.

"Stiff. Male subject. Probably been dead a couple of days."

"Where's the body?"

"In the living room on a couch. Broken neck. Probably killed instantly."

"Shall we have a look?"

Sanchez didn't answer, just turned and led the way to the front door. Tobias was impressed by how the sergeant had secured the area: every square inch of the front of the house was marked with police tape with one exception: a service path from the middle the curb to the front door. He had posted an officer at the bottom of the driveway to direct foot traffic and to discourage nosy neighbors from interfering with police work. As Tobias entered the house, he saw two specialists. One was working the area immediately around the victim, the other getting ready to process another area toward the rear of the house. There was a pathway from the door to the kitchen and all around the couch made of butcher paper. It was used for two reasons: to protect potential evidence beneath the people working the scene, and to protect the people working the scene from the potential evidence beneath. It wasn't standard protocol, just an extra layer to manage debris that might make its way around the crime scene.

"Looks uncomfortable," said Tobias, motioning toward the body.

"Yeah. Hell of a break. Freakish. Think he did that to himself turning around too fast? I always worry about that."

Tobias smiled at Sanchez, then figured he'd get in his first stab at seeing how much the kid was picking up. "So how do you figure the body has been here two days?"

Sanchez smiled, as if he were waiting for the question. "Well, there's no rigor, so the body has to have been here for at least a day. We really can't rely on body temperature because of this freaking heat, I mean ambient is pretty close to normal body temps right now anyway. The face, however, is mottled and discolored, the smell is pretty bad, and the body has a greenish tinge to it."

Okay, that pretty much covers 48 hours. Why not longer?"

"No fluid excretions and no bloating."

"Very good! I'm going to make homicide detective out of you yet!" Tobias gave Sanchez a slap on the shoulder, then took in the interior of the house.

"Okay, I'm assuming since I don't see a latency team here, the place has already been dusted and glued?"

"Yeah, they left about an hour ago. The photographer's been here as well."

"He do the whole house?"

"Yep. He did the front yard and the back too."

"Where's the log?"

"Out front with officer Jenkins. I'll get it."

Tobias watched Sanchez as he slipped out the front door, smiling to himself at the kid's aptitude, dedication, and exuberance.   Lately, any time he arrived at a crime scene, he did two things: he talked to Sanchez to get a snapshot of what was happening, then he looked at the logs. Logs provided the details: who'd been processing the scene and what had been processed.

Sanchez returned, Tobias glossed over the tabulated details, annotating in his PDA the current state of the scene: the place had been dusted and photographed; control samples of carpets and flooring had been taken, probable zones marking the intruder's position in the house had been identified, and test samples had been taken from them. A general inventory of disturbances to the premises had been itemized; elimination samples had been taken from responders to remove them from the eventual suspect list; and the condition of the victim's body had been summarized. The last remaining business appeared to be the completion of forensic analysis of the body and the vicinity immediately around it. Tobias figured he had at least another hour before he could actually do a detailed walk-through of the scene. Nothing to do until then, so he turned to Sanchez and said,

"Let's go get some coffee."

 

They claimed the rear booth of a dirty, nearly empty diner called Santa Ana Brew. The air wafted a mixture of grease, syrup, and strong coffee. The red vinyl cushions on which they sat were cracked in regular intervals all along the surface, exposing a crusty yellow, spongy material. The table reflected a style of decades past: its perimeter wrapped with ribbed aluminum; its surface colored white with blue speckles, the uniformity of which was interrupted here and there with ancient, ringed coffee stains. The floor was a dirty linoleum that might have been white once; the walls were adorned with strategically-placed prints of movie stars, each employed to cover a crack or a blemish on the wall behind it. The place was a mess and probably in dire need of a visit from the health department. Tobias and Sanchez felt right at home. They ordered two coffees from a middle-aged waitress sporting blonde hair a little too big and a red skirt a little too short.

"Okay, what do we know?" Asked Tobias.

"Did a background check on the vic. Turns out, he did time for child molestation several years back. Four years, but got released early for good behavior."

"Had a chance to talk to his parole officer yet?"

Sanchez took a sip of his coffee, then returned the cup to the table, centering it within an ancient caffeine ring.

"Affirmative. His name's Larry … " Sanchez flipped through his notebook, found the name. "Banderman. He told me the deceased, Zach Hassenback, gave him no problems. In fact, he petitioned to have his ankle bracelet removed a full six months sooner than the conditions of his parole specified. That was approved."

"So far as we know, Hassenback had rehabilitated himself, was working toward becoming a righteous, productive member of society, until some guy comes along and almost tears his head off."

"As far as we know, that basically sums it up," said Sanchez.

"Okay, so I'm assuming from your report that our vic remained clean for his entire time on the outside?"

"Appears to be the case, yeah." Sanchez took another sip from his coffee just as the blonde waitress dove in to top them off.

The interruption gave Tobias a chance to make some annotations in his PDA. He pressed its tiny keyboard with his fingers, prodded it's screen with his stylus, poked it some more, and placed it back on the table.

"Okay, how about basic observations?"

"First, the body: the guy's neck wasn't just snapped. His head was damn-near ripped off. I talked to the pathologist about that. Seems Hollywood's got that all wrong: if a guy's prepared, it's almost impossible to break his neck by twisting it to the side. The muscles and tendons are too strong. Even if the victim's not prepared, the most a move like that is going to gain is torn muscles and tendons. Hell of an injury, but no broken neck and no kill."

Tobias took a sip of his coffee, felt his stomach churn out a rumbling protest.

"So what do you think happened?"

"Hassenback was caught unaware. So the guy must've come up from behind. Could have been a guest, but it's not looking that way. Whoever killed him was more than likely laying in wait in a rear room. He picked his opportunity, then snuck up from behind and broke the man's neck. But that's where I get a little confused, boss, based on what the pathologist told me."

"You mean with regard to the force required to break a neck?"

"Yeah. He said it was almost impossible."

Tobias considered the point for a moment. "My bet is they'll find a chin-sized bruise on the vic's sternum. That would indicate the neck was broken with a violent, forward-rotating motion. The side motion was probably an afterthought, something the perp did to ensure the kill."

Sanchez looked up to the ceiling, rolled his head forward, then leveled a questioning look at Tobias.

"So you're saying it's easier to break a guy's neck with a forward versus a side rotation?"

"No. It's all about force. There's only so much of it at your disposal with a side-to-side motion. You're really at the mercy of arm strength. With a forward roll, the perp could have used his whole body weight to help with the break. I've seen it before. If the guy was big enough, just his body weight alone could have done the job."

Sanchez nodded.

"Prints on the body?" Tobias asked.

"First pass indicated none, besides Hassenback's own."

Tobias leaned back, made a few more annotations in his PDA. "Perfect. No prints on the body; no prints anywhere, more than likely. So the guy either cleaned up, or he was wearing gloves. Maybe both. Which means we're going to be lucky if we find anything at the scene."

"Yeah, seems to be how it's panning out," Sanchez offered.

"Okay, what else? Anything?"

"Yeah, one other thing sticks out. Little bizarre, actually. The vic's computer. The hard drive's missing. I guess we could assume the computer was down for repairs, but the vic was a convicted pedo, and those types don't like to go without their electronic fixes for very long."

"Yeah, a functional computer would definitely be a priority. That it? Nothing else?"

"Nothing but the usual: entry point was through the back door. A pane in the window was taken out and placed behind some bushes in the back yard. And the house was tossed. Almost every square inch appears to have been searched."

Tobias a sip of his coffee, and ruminated. As the leggy waitress with the big hair left the check on the table, he looked at Sanchez and said,

"Here's what I want you to do: get the file on this guy, his whole history. I want to know how many times he's been incarcerated and why. I want to know if any of his convictions have been tossed due to technicalities. We need a comprehensive victim list, and then we need a list of all the family members. We're going to have to start with them."

As Sanchez scribbled in his notepad, Tobias lost himself in his coffee mug, in the contrast between the bone-white color of the ceramic and the deep brown color of the coffee it contained. Something was bothering him, but he couldn't piece it together. Something trivial. He looked up, found Sanchez staring at him.

"You okay?"

"Just one issue. We have a murder victim, a scene that appears to have been set to make the whole thing look like a robbery, an attempt at misdirection, but …" Tobias looked down at his PDA again, thinking.

"But what?"

"If the perp was trying to make this look like a robbery, why take just the hard drive? Why not take the whole computer?"

"Good question."

"You ready for the walk-through?"

"Yeah, let's go."
