Chapter 22

 

 

The last remaining man, the boss, had thrown his weapon well in front of him, flung it about twenty feet. Clark and Tobias approached him from opposite sides, carefully, firearms trained on him all the way. When they were certain there were no other immediate threats, Clark made the guy lay face down on the ground. He placed his right foot on the man's neck, removed the nylon rope from Tobias's wrists, then pressed his knee into the man's lower back and used the rope to bind his hands together tightly. He tested the knot, decided it would hold, then pulled the man up from the ground.

"What's your name?" Clark demanded.

"None of your fucking business! You two are in a shitload of trouble, you know that? Come on to my property and shoot up the fucking place like it's some kind of vid ..."

Clark stopped the man's tirade with a vicious backhand to the face, which returned him to the ground. Clark helped him up again, then re-iterated his question.

"What's your name?"

The man looked up at him, eyes wide with contempt. He instinctively tried to move his hands to his face to massage his freshly-bruised jaw. But he couldn't. He licked his upper lip, spat out the blood deposited there from his right nostril. For a moment, Clark thought he was going to continue with his threats, but then he cast his eyes downward, and his shoulders relaxed.

"I was only trying to protect my property." He looked at Tobias. "Didn't want to get the cops involved; didn't mean for any of this shit to happen. My name's Gerkowitcz, Dean Gerkowitcz." He said it softly, laboriously, as if the air had been let out of him.

"Okay, Gerkowitcz, we're going to take a short walk to my truck, then we're going to drive back to your place, and then you're going to give us a tour."

Gerkowitcz said nothing, so Clark shifted his attention to Tobias. He was standing just to his left, massaging the raw skin on his wrists. His face was cut up with lacerations, and his left eye was beginning to swell shut. Other than that, Clark figured he looked okay.

The walk to the truck took less than ten minutes. Tobias claimed the passenger seat while Clark stuffed Gerkowitcz into the rear cab, face down on the bench seat. He slammed the door on the corpulent man, climbed into the captain's chair, then fired up the big diesel for the drive back to the facility. Besides the labored breathing coming from Gerkowitcz, the return trip was filled with a deafening silence. Clark didn't feel  the need to say anything. There'd be enough talking later. He figured he'd do what he'd planned with Gerkowitcz, then keep his word and hand himself over to Tobias. What else could he do? They knew who he was. He could run, but they'd find him. He might be able to evade for a few months, maybe even a year. But he'd have to look over his shoulder constantly, hide everywhere he went. He'd lived a life of preventive evasion for almost two years, found it wasn't the kind of existence he lusted after. If he gave himself up, maybe they'd give him life, under the circumstances.

As he drove a circuitous route back to the front of the property, the buildings once again came into view. Clark found an unimproved side road into the gravel parking area that fronted the main building, then stopped the truck just short of its concrete steps. As he placed the truck in park, he looked over at Tobias, noticed the man was sweating a little and his face had gone pale.

"You okay?" He asked.

Tobias rubbed the beads of sweat away from his forehead. "Not really, Clark. I lost my partner today, we just passed the spot where that big son of a bitch pushed his rifle barrel into the back of my head, and I'm beat all to hell."

The response rang with stinging irritation. Clark could think of nothing to say, so they sat in silence for a moment.

Tobias looked out the passenger window, lowered his voice to barely above a whisper. "Sorry. It's been a rough day to say the least. But I'll survive. Besides, we have more important things to worry about than how I'm doing." He motioned with his head toward the door of the building in front of them. Clark acknowledged the taciturn objective with a nod of his own

"You fire off all the rounds in that handgun I threw you?"

Tobias picked up the Steyr, ejected the clip, then held it up for inspection.

"I have eleven left."

"That should be sufficient. I doubt there's anyone left. Pretty sure these guys shot their load with that last press; but just in case, let's not take any chances. Chamber a round, and let's go in weapons drawn."

Tobias and Clark exited the truck, then Clark opened the driver's side rear door of the crew cab and pulled Gerkowitcz out from the back. He turned the man around, looked into his eyes.

"Where do you keep them?"

Gerkowitcz hesitated for a moment, as if he were weighing his options. Clark noticed he was standing more erect; he no longer looked defeated. There was an arrogance there, but it was mixed with a palpable nervous energy that seemed to seep from his pores, flood out of his body with the prodigious amount of sweat that was now soaking his clothes.

"Keep who?" He asked.

"The girls."

Gerkowitcz put on a look of surprise and bewilderment. "I have no idea what you're talking about. If you'd have let me finish what I was saying earlier, I could have explained everything. This whole fiasco was a God damned misunderstanding! My fucking chief of security thought you boys were a rival mining outfit, industrial spies sent out to hassle us. We've had problems with them before, violent problems. A whole bunch of my men got beat up and put in the hospital just a few weeks back because of their shit. That's why there's hardly anyone here."

"So you're a mining outfit?" Clark deadpanned.

"Yes, God damn it! This whole fucking mess is one big fucking mistake!"

"So you've been hassled by this rival outfit; you figured we were with them, so you simply did what you had to do to protect yourself and your crew?"

"That's what I've been trying to tell you! Now if you don't release me, I'm going to lawyer up and sue the shit out of the both of you! Now God damn it untie me!"

With each syllable, Gerkowitcz appeared to grow more confident. The nervous energy Clark had noticed only a moment ago appeared to have ceased altogether. He looked around the grounds. It was a large property, and he knew the girls wouldn't be stashed anywhere obvious, even in a place like this out in the middle of nowhere. He could search the place, tear it down board-by-board, but he wanted closure quickly. The day hadn't yet reached its hottest point, and he wanted to wrap things up before that if he were going to follow through with what he'd planned. So he walked around Gerkowitcz, grabbed a stubby forefinger, then bent it back until it snapped. The man let out a howling scream, crumpling to his knees in pain. Clark walked back around him, gently lifted his face so they were looking eye-to-eye.,

"Listen, asshole, you've got four more fingers left on your right hand, and you've got five remaining on your left. I'd be more than happy to spend the next few minutes breaking them one by one until you tell me where you're keeping the girls. This is a big place; I don't have a lot of time. So why don't you make this easy on the both of us and just tell us where you're keeping them?"

The rest of Gerkowitcz's fingers remained unharmed, and after he lead them to the shelter, Clark set about doing three of the four things he'd planned to do before leaving: isolate the clients, secure the girls, and if it were still around, retrieve his daughter's stuffed bear.

 There were only three customers remaining, each locked in his own room with a girl. As Tobias kept an eye on Gerkowitcz, Clark removed the men, placing them one by one into an empty room and locking it from the outside. Two of them had demonstrated a lack of judgment and resisted, and both had received injuries that would require hospitalization before finding new accommodations in prison. When he was finished, Clark once again took possession of Gerkowitcz, which freed  Tobias to round up the girls and begin the process of identifying them and reassuring them they'd be home soon.

The bear was in a makeshift meeting room to the rear of the shelter. It was in a glass display case with about ten other items that had been collected from girls Gerkowitcz had conscripted as sex slaves. When he saw it, he could barely contain himself. He wanted to tear the place apart, mop the shards of broken glass from the case with Gerkowitcz's face. But he knew he couldn't do that. He might damage the other mementos, and those were items the surviving girls might want back, something the parents of the girls who didn't make it might want to keep as reminders. And he wasn't going to kill Gerkowitcz, either. He had other plans for him. So he simply retrieved the bear, then walked Gerkowitcz back through the great room, pushing him quickly past Tobias and the girls. The less they saw of the man, the better. He walked him out of view behind a shed in the middle of the room, had him lay face down on the floor, then placed his boot on his neck to keep him from moving.

Tobias was the reason he didn't have to complete the forth item on his list. He'd planned on calling Carri, planned on waiting for her until she showed up to watch the kids until the authorities arrived. But he didn't need to do that now. And he was conflicted about that. He wanted to see her again, even though it would've been difficult, even though they would've had to say goodbye one more time. But it worked out best for her. She wouldn't have to get caught up in the mess. He pivoted on his foot, slightly grinding into Gerkowitcz's neck as he did. The fat man protested feebly, and from his reaction, Clark figured the fight was mostly out of him.

"Tobias!" Clark called.

Tobias walk around the shed and stopped just short of Clark, body angled so he could still see the girls. He looked much better than he had just minutes before. Finding the girls was definitely a plus for him, gave him something to keep his mind from the nastiness of the day.

"We're heading out now. I just need a few hours."

"You have a cell?"

"Yeah."

"Hang on for a second."

Tobias disappeared around the corner, then returned a moment later with a notepad and a pen. He wrote his cell number down, tore the sheet from the pad, folded it, and handed it to Clark.

"I owe you my life, Clark. You saved my ass out there today. Twice. The least I can do is give you some lead time. I owe you that. If you want to run, you go ahead and do it. I'll tell you this, though: life on the lamb isn't fun, and one of these days, you'll get a little careless, and you'll get picked up. But I'm not going to push this and make you turn yourself in. Not after this. But if you are going to run, get out of state. Hell, get out of the country if you can. But don't stay anywhere near here. If you want to turn yourself in, call me. I'll pick you up myself. It'll be painless and discreet."

Clark allowed himself a single, brief grin. Then it was gone. He'd been prepared to go to prison. But he'd lied to himself about taking four stone walls over freedom, any kind of freedom. He'd allowed his mind to wander, to contemplate the possibility, to try on the idea like a new shirt. But he knew that's all it was. Given the choice, he'd run. Given the window of opportunity provided by Tobias, he'd take it. He looked at the officer, didn't know what to say.  After a moment of getting used to the idea of walking away, he finally put together a reply.

"Thanks."

Tobias just nodded, then stuck out his hand. Clark took it in his, pressed it firmly, then turned to walk away. As he steered Gerkowitcz toward the hatch, the man finally broke his silence.

"Where're you taking me?" He asked.

"Nature walk."

 

He'd read a news article online almost two years before about a guy, an athlete, who thought he'd try to cross Death Valley with just a quart of water. In the middle of the summer. He'd almost made it, had come as close as a quarter mile from where he'd parked his car. A group of tourists had found his body the same day. The article claimed the autopsy report stated the man had lost almost sixty pounds during his trek. In a matter of hours, he'd had the water sucked right out of him. He'd been mummified.

It seemed like a fitting end for the monster who'd caused the death of his daughter, had left her out in the desert to die like an animal. And it would be torturous. It would all start with an incredible, unforgiving thirst, the discomfort of which would quickly be compounded by superheated skin, a racing heart, and delirium. Death would come, but it would an agonizingly slow process. It would give the bastard a few hours to think about what he'd done to his daughter, and all the other little girls he'd abducted and abused. He knew too, though, that after some time, he'd change psychologically. He'd lose himself, become mad from heat stroke. And then probably an hour later he'd be dead. To make things more interesting, Clark had decided to dial up the pain a little, give him a canteen spiked with laxative. So along with the overwhelming distress from exposure, he'd have to deal with accelerated dehydration and painful stomach cramps as well.

He drove into the desert for a good hour, found a spot in the middle of a flat valley that spread out about twenty miles in all directions. It's surface was colored white with alkaline, and the air above it shimmered with heat, distorting what lay beyond it. He looked at the digital clock on his console. It said 2:38 PM. The outside air temperature gauge next to it reported 110 degrees. He figured the valley floor had to be about ten to fifteen degrees hotter. As he exited his truck, he was sure he was right. The air was oppressive, and the desert scalded his feet even through his boots. He went around to the passenger side rear door, opened it, and dragged Gerkowitcz out.

"What are you planning to do with me?" The boastful man he'd been was gone, his pompous exterior now replaced with an outward composure wrapped up in fear and self-concern.

Clark simply looked through him. "Nature walk. I told you. I just need one more thing from you, and then you're free to go."

"What's that?" Gerkowitcz was visibly shaking, as though he were freezing.

"Your clothes."

"I can't." He began hyperventilating. His shaking worsened.

"You can, and you will," Clark paused. "Then you can walk out."

Gerkowitcz just looked at him, uncomprehending. He cast his gaze into the desert. His eyes shot wide with fear.

"Why are you doing this?" He paused, lowered his head as if he already knew the answer to the question. Then he looked up quickly, his face suddenly lit up.

"I've got money. Loads of it. I'll give you all of it. Anything you want. Just please don't do this. I have a ..."

He started wheezing, sniffling. His expression changed, now cast with a cold understanding, a realization of a truth he was trying desperately to avoid. Then the tears came, exploded from his face, landing on his shirt and saturating it with salty dampness.

"But ... " He paused again. "I have a family. I have a daughter. I'm never going to see her ... " He choked. "I'm never going to see her again. I have to." The last words barely escaped from his mouth, riding into the air in a lightly pitched whine.

Clark moved closer so his face was only an inch from Gerkowitcz's. He reached around the man's girth, grabbed the middle finger of his left hand, and that was all it took. Two minutes later, he was unbound, undressed, and standing naked in the desert. The only thing he had to protect him from the sun was a one-quart canteen.

Clark got back into his truck, fired it up, and began backing away. As he did, Gerkowitcz made a desperate run toward the driver's side door, screaming all the way.

"No! No! I have to say goodbye! No! Please. Stop!"

Clark stopped, rolled down his window. Gerkowitcz quickly grabbed the door. His eyes were bloodshot, he was bouncing from one foot to the other, trying to keep the hot desert from burning him from the ground up.

"Please." He begged. The tears had stopped. He just looked desperate, overwhelmed. "Please. Take me back. Let them put me in jail. I'll do anything. Just let me see my daughter. I have to say ... " He choked again. "Oh God. I have to say goodbye."

"That's the one thing any parent would want to do, isn't it?"

"Yes. I'm so sorry. But you understand. I'll take prison. I'll take the death penalty. I take anything you want. But I have to say goodbye. Please." His words came out fast, riding atop heavy, labored breathing.

"Funny how things fall into perspective when you know you're never going to see the one person who gives your life meaning ever again."

"Please, sir. Don't leave me out here. Send me to prison. Just let me say goodbye. I'll give you anything. I'll do anything. Please let me see her one more time." Gerkowitcz stood back from the truck as if waiting for Clark to invite him back in. Clark felt nothing for him. He was a scab on humanity, and in a matter of hours, he'd be dead. Soon thereafter, he'd be consumed by insects and wildlife. He'd be recycled, and that would be the greatest contribution the man would ever give to the world.

"You didn't give me that chance. I couldn't say goodbye to my little girl. You sent her to the desert to die. You threw her away like garbage. Now it's your turn to get tossed you sick fuck!"

Clark took his foot off the brake, then pressed hard into the gas. The rear wheels of the truck dug into the desert, shot a trail of dust and gravel behind it as he sped off. As he headed back the way he'd come in, he looked in his rear view mirror. Gerkowitcz hadn't moved. He just stood there, bouncing on his feet, watching the truck as it quickly faded from view.

Tobias sat in the kitchen, Carol behind him rubbing his shoulders. His daughter and her husband were there. His granddaughter,  Samantha, was running around with the family dog, mercifully oblivious to the day's events. He'd buried Sanchez only hours earlier, said goodbye to his partner, the young man he'd considered more of a son than anything else. The service had been beautiful and brutal at the same time. He'd walked to the podium, offered up some final thoughts on the kid, but got too choked up to continue. He had to be escorted out by his wife, and now here he was, drinking his fifth glass of wine, remembering Sanchez, wondering what his own future held.

It certainly wasn't with the force. He couldn't do that anymore. Not after what went down in the desert. He'd killed a man. Not in cold blood, but in an act of passion. And he'd covered it up, too. In fact, he'd fixed the whole crime scene. All around Tiny, the big black man who'd gone in with Clark to help out. Tiny was just about the right size, maybe a little too big. But Tobias had re-arranged timelines, falsified reports, and massaged the data just enough to land the responsibility for Hassenback's, Edurado's, and Chuck's deaths all on him. It was all very elegant, but it was all very wrong just the same. And he was sure if the case were ever re-opened, if it were scrutinized even a little bit, his whole house of cards would come crashing down. And then he'd be asked to leave the force.

So he beat them to the punch and resigned after he'd cleared all of his responsibilities from his plate. His resignation didn't come out of guilt, though. It came from a realization that the justice system, while tolerably efficient in most cases, lacked the sharp edge it required to sever the slime from the rest of the law-abiding citizens. It came from a realization that while Clark may not have been right about what he did, he wasn't wrong either. He'd learned that, sometimes, murder is righteous. As he continued thinking about his new perception, his corrected understanding of the world he walked in daily, he faintly heard the sound of Carol's voice.

"I love you, sweetie."

Tobias reached over his shoulder, held onto her hand with one if his. It felt warm, comfortable. It felt like home. He laced his fingers in hers, but said nothing, just stared into his wine glass. He was somehow struck with the burgundy color of the liquid it contained. He found it beautiful.

"We can make it, you know. You can retire right now if you want to," she said.

Tobias shook himself out of the wine glass, withdrew his hands, rubbed his eyes, then took another sip of wine.

"I'm thinking about hanging my own shingle, maybe part time. What do you think of that?"

Carol walked around the table, sat in the chair across from him. "I think that's a great idea, honey. Whatever you want to do, you know I'm going to support it." She patted his hand, then got up and gave him a kiss on the cheek. "I'm going to go check on Sam. Let me know if you need anything, okay baby?"

Tobias nodded, then watched his wife walk away. As he took another sip of wine, his mind shifted to Clark. He should have gotten his number. He could have called him, told him he might not have to run so far after all. But it'd been a week, and for all he knew, Clark was in Mexico or Canada by now, well on his way to a new life. At least he hoped so. He banished Clark from his mind, then focused again on his wine glass. It was half full. Or maybe half empty. It all depended on perspective, and he figured his needed to change. He'd been dressed up in black inside and out for about a week. It was time to switch colors, time to stop dwelling on the past, start living in the present. He'd place the word private before detective and start thinking about the future too. He drained the rest of his wine, pushed back from the table, then made his way into the living room to join the rest of his family.
