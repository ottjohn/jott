Chapter 19

 

 

Clark looked down at his almost-empty plate, the nutrients it held minutes ago now participating in his digestive process, working to repair his muscle fibers, helping him to think more clearly, and, unfortunately, tipping him toward the characteristic lethargy that accompanies epic consumption. Carri had grilled the steak to perfection, a medium-rare rib eye, well marbled, with a tender, juicy consistency he could only describe as moving. The steak had been accompanied by silky smooth garlic mashed potatoes, and a salad made with lots of green leafy foliage, nuts, fruit, and avocado. He'd never been much of a fan of salads, but he figured if he could get them all to come out like Carri's, he might just give them another chance.

"Really delicious. I'm not sure when's the last time I had a meal this good. Thank you."

"You bought the food." She smiled, cradling her face in her hands as she looked directly into his eyes, as if daring him to hold her gaze.

"Anyone can buy food," he protested. "It's the preparation that's the most important part of any meal, so it's the cook who's owed the gratitude."

Clark took a sip of his beer, some imported German brand he'd never heard of before. He took the final bite of his steak, diminishing the quantity of digestible nutrients on his plate to trace amounts. He pushed his chair back from the table, then stretched his hands high above his head and took a large gulp of air with a single, giant yawn.

"Sorry."

Carri waved off the apology. "No need. When's the last time you slept?"

"I think I might have gotten a couple of hours' last night."

Carri stood up, walked around the table, and took Clark's hand. He got to his feet, and she lead him through the living room and down a narrow hallway that provided access to three bedrooms and a large bathroom. He followed her into one of the bedrooms, where she walked him to the foot of a over-sized queen bed. It was decked out with a pink comforter adorned with red heart shapes, a mountain of large pillows in black silky cases almost spilling over both edges. The bed itself was tucked neatly within a wooden, four-post frame.

She looked up into his eyes, then pointed at the bed. "You need to get some rest."

He didn't know what to say. He was tired. He needed rest. But he also knew sleeping now would take away much of the remaining time he might have with her. He wasn't sure what the night held for him, but he was confident a few cups of strong coffee would get him through whatever he'd have to face.

"I appreciate what you're doing, Carri, but I'm okay. I just need a little coffee."

Carri shook her head. "No, Jack. What you need is a little sleep, just enough to recharge you." Then her voice choked just a little, "Please? I want you to be okay, Jack. Don't go out there tired."

Clark closed what little space there was between them, wrapped his arms around her.

"You're probably right. But I only need about a half an hour. Anything  more is going to add to my exhaustion."

He kissed the top of her head, then sat down on the bed and watched her leave. Physically, he wasn't that tired. Mentally, he felt drained. But he was accustomed to operating like that. Over the past couple of years, he'd grown used to the endless nights that had so many times melted into the morning light. And he wanted to spend more time with her, didn't want to lose even another minute. He thought for a moment about walking back into the kitchen, taking her in his arms again. It felt good to hold her, felt like home. He allowed himself a smile, something he'd noticed he'd done more of since he'd met her.

Then reality hit him like a runaway Mack truck: he had a promise to keep. Nothing could stand in his way. Not even Carri. He was suddenly struck by a jab of anguish. A man lost in revenge, past the point of no return. Out of nowhere, an angel had come to him, had shown him he actually could enjoy another's company, smile again. But the smiles came with a painful longing of what might have been, hardened with the awful acceptance of what could never be. He stripped to his underwear, then lay down on the bed. His head hit the pillow like a lead weight. Less than a minute later, he was out.

 

He woke to the feeling of a slight vibration, wondered through sleep-induced fog if there had been an earthquake. If it were a temblor, it couldn't have been too bad. Probably not even a four. Nothing to worry about, so he decided to close his eyes for a few more minutes before getting up to prepare himself for what lay ahead. Then he felt another sensation. Hands. On his back. Carri's hands. He rolled over, found Carri lying next to him, stripped to her underwear. He figured Carri was a woman who'd look good, elegant even, dressed in a garbage bag. The site of her in her underwear, however, did nothing short of take his breath away. He spent a moment drinking her in, found himself a little self-conscious at his reaction. Carri seemed to understand, put her forefinger to her lips.

"Relax, big guy. I'm not going to bite you. Just want to spend more time with you." Her smile quickly disappeared and she cast her eyes down at the comforter. Her nose crinkled just a little, and her eyebrows furrowed as if she were in pain.

"Jack. I uh ... I'm never going to see you again after tomorrow, and that hurts like hell. I know this is going to sound crazy, but I've fallen for you. Hard. But at the same time ..."

Small tears welled up in her eyes, flowed down her cheeks, and plunged to the soft fabric of the comforter, soaking it with the salty sorrow of a lost past that could only be realized in a blissful dance through an impossible future.

"At the same time, I know you have to do what you have to do. I want you to. I want you to find peace. I want you to be happy. But for now ... for right now, please don't deny me from experiencing just this once what might have ..."

Before she could get out another word, Clark stopped it with a soft kiss to her lips. He wasn't sure if it was the right thing to do, but he no longer cared. He'd rescued her; then she'd returned the favor. They'd built a bond in a matter of days that takes most people a lifetime create. As he kissed her lips and the nape of her neck with more urgency, he pushed the anguish of having to leave her to the back of his mind. He focused on the present, on the little piece of heaven he'd found in her.

 

Two hours later, Clark was making his way toward Huntington Park in Chuck's Mustang. The evening had cooled enough for him to roll down the vehicle's windows, but he chose to keep them up, use their tint to obscure his identity as he drove along the 110 toward the Florence Avenue Exit. He'd driven the entire way in compromised silence, using the blast of the air conditioner to help drown out the noise of the busy L.A. traffic. He needed to think, and generally, in the absence of quiet, white noise helped him do that.

The initial part of his trip had been dominated by thoughts of Carri. He'd enjoyed the moment, every second, yet he felt a stinging sense of regret. Making love to her was wonderful, but it had opened a conduit of doubt about what he'd been doing. About what he still needed to do. It had demonstrated to him the possibility of moving on, of starting over. Given his present circumstances, however, he knew that possibility was hollow at best, simply a plastic shell encasing a reality that was at once void of future contemplation and over-stuffed with the bleak certainty of a small cement cell he'd likely call home for the rest of his life. Was it worth it? The memory of his daughter, what had been done to her, and how she'd been found pushed all the doubt away. He was on a road with no exits, shuttling at high velocity toward a outcome he'd been seeking for over two years. His destination was still a little vague, but he'd get there. Or he'd die trying.

As he exited the 110 and headed east on Florence Avenue, the white noise from the air conditioner lost its effectiveness against the external sounds pressing in from the traffic around him. His introspection moved to vigilance as he heard a heavy bass beat vibrating through the tinted windows of a tricked-out Suburban that weaved by on his left. Suddenly the Suburban slowed, cut across a busy intersection, and sped up a residential cross-street. A Gang Task Force police vehicle, coming from the other direction, broadcast a piercing wail and gave pursuit. He didn't bother watching the drama unfold, simply checked his mirrors, looking now for two kinds of trouble: police presence and the unwanted attention from pedestrians who might want to get a closer look at Chuck's Mustang. He reached under the seat with his right hand, felt the re-assuring grip of the Colt 45.

As he continued his short trek down Florence Avenue, he started to gain an appreciation for the vehicle he was driving: the model was popular enough to help him blend in with the traffic around him; the tinted windows ensured his anonymity with respect to law enforcement. As long as he didn't break any laws and remained accident-free, he figured he'd be fine. But the Mustang wouldn't perform in the desert. He'd need a different vehicle for that. So before he left, he'd asked Carri to search the want-ads for a four-wheel drive vehicle. He didn't much care about the make, so long as it was newer, in good condition, and ready for the rough terrain he expected to find just east of Baker.

He took a left on Avalon, then an immediate right into a corner gas station. He parked next to an older Toyota Minivan. Two black males were leaning against the rear of it. The man on the left was Joe. Clark noticed he hadn't changed much. Domestic life had added a little paunch to his six foot frame, but he still had the same boyish face, large, almost bovine eyes, and jug ears that jutted out from a cleanly-shaven head. He was dressed in newer, loose-fitting jeans, a black Raiders t-shirt, and white tennis shoes. The whole ensemble could be described as gangland conservative: toned down enough to allow him to blend into the mainstream, yet presenting the appropriate message to keep him out of trouble in South Central.

He guessed the man on the right to be Joe's brother, something he would've had difficulty telling under different circumstances. Jerry wore baggy sweat pants pulled down to the tops of this thighs, dark blue boxers, a dirty white tank top, and an over-sized jacket dyed military green. Decorating his head like a trophy from a mop manufacturer was  a mess of thick, unkempt dreadlocks. He had the same large eyes as his brother, but contrasting the chocolate-colored complexion on his face was a long, white, curved scar that stretched from his right ear all the way to the bottom of his chin. His sweats draped over white sneakers, partially obscuring them; his hands were thrust deep into the pockets of his jacket. It was 85 degrees outside. Clark didn't understand the jacket, just chalked it up to another irrational expression of gangland fashion.

As discreetly as he could, he reached under the front seat, withdrew his gun, and tucked it snugly between his beltline and the small of his back. He then got out of the Mustang, walked around the back of the car, and shook his old friend's hand.

"Good to see you, Joe."

Joe gave Clark a weary look. "Good to see you." He then turned to his brother.

"Jerry, this is Clark, the friend I was telling you about."

Jerry gave Clark a look, then uttered a single, lazy syllable.

"Sup?"

Clark thought about extending his hand, but Jerry still had both of his in his jacket pockets. Like it was the middle of winter and he was cold. So he simply gave him a nod and turned back to his friend.

"Thanks, Joe. I really appreciate this. I got it from here."

A mixture of surprise and resignation crept into Joe's facial expression.

"You sure? I can stay if you like."

Clark smiled. "You don't need to be in this any deeper than you are. I really appreciate the offer, though. I hope someday I'll have the opportunity to pay you back."

Joe started to raise his arms in protest, thought better of it, shook his head, and returned to his van. Jerry and Clark took up position behind the Mustang, and watched Joe drive away, neither of them really sure about the other, neither really sure about what to say next. Finally, Jerry broke the silence.

"This your ride?" He asked.

"It could be yours. Consider it a gift in return for something I need from you." Clark assessed Jerry as he spoke, concluded he wasn't a threat. But Jerry appeared to be sizing him up. Jerry backed away just a bit, then threw on attitude as naturally as debutante might throw on a new pair of shoes.

"What the hell can I do for you, crackah?"

"Crackah?" Clark replied.

"Yeah. Whitey, honkey, boy, mutha fucka. Whateva you want. And what you gonna do if I decide to take your mutha fuckin' car anyway, nigga?" Jerry's arms were moving out from his body as he spoke, his face pointed up at Clark's, the tone of his voice purposefully inflected to intimidate. His act may have worked on a lot of people, but not with Clark. He leaned in close to Jerry's face, getting near enough so the man could feel his words burst onto him like cannon fire.

"Let's get one thing straight, son: I'm not a crackah, whitey, homey, or any other derogatory slang you'd like to throw my way. You will call me Mr. Clark. I'm here to do business. This car ..." Clark pointed to the Mustang "...is yours in exchange for a little help. In the meantime, all I ask for is your undivided attention. Now, if you don't think you're capable of that, Jerry, I can take the car and my money and find help elsewhere. And if you try to stop me, I'll put you in the ground. Is that clear?"

Jerry looked momentarily confused, then got a look on his face not unlike that of a dog after having been scolded for messing on the floor. He'd been put in his place, probably a customary one. Clark briefly wondered why the kid had remained in the life for so long if he couldn't climb the social rungs.

Jerry, gave the car another look, then smiled.

"You gonna give me that if I help you?"

"That's correct, Jerry."

Jerry shrugged, then extended his hand. "How can I help you, Mr. Clark?"

Clark took the man's hand, grasped it in a firm grip. "What I need we can't talk about here. You have some place we can go?"

"I gotta place. Share a crib with my niggas up north of Compton. You square with that?"

Clark didn't answer, just threw Jerry the keys to the Mustang and headed toward the passenger side of the car.

After a little more than an hour's work, Tobias and Carol concluded that most of Eduardo's larger purchases were centered right around his apartment in Santa Ana. There'd been plenty of transactions further away, but those were all well within the boundaries of the greater metropolitan area. All except for three, that is: the first occurred on March 15th. Eduardo had paid for the services of a company called Desert Tours. Tobias had quickly searched the company name on the internet but came up with nothing more than a P.O. Box and a short, vacuous description, the kind that's not made by the company itself, but by some third-party web crawler. The second and third transactions occurred on the same date and reflected two separate purchases at a small gas station within the town of Baker, California. A single additional transaction on the 14th of March showed Eduardo withdrawing $500 from a local branch of his bank in Santa Ana.

"Baker." Tobias said, mostly to himself.

"You think his visit there had something to do with his murder?" Asked Carol.

"Dunno." His answer was short, almost terse. It was a reply built out of a focused immersion into the possibility, blurred around the edges by a nagging exhaustion. He spent the next several minutes contemplating Baker, weighing the subjective probabilities, trying to tie the pieces together.

"You're being quiet." She said, frowning.

"Sorry. You might be onto something with the Baker angle, though."

She smiled, put her hands on his shoulders. "Let's share our theories. You know how I love mutual postulation."

Tobias sighed. "I can't, honey."

"Why not? I can handle it. I'm a big girl." She wagged her forefinger in his face in mock admonishment.

Tobias yawned, stretched his hands above his head. "I know. Sorry I'm a little cranky. Just really tired."

"I know you are Toby Bear." She pouted at him. "But you're trying to protect me from something ugly, and you know it. Do you want to talk about it?"

"No." He said it a little harshly, immediately regretted his tone.

"You know, Paul, you get these cases, and they take their toll. They weigh on you, sweetie, and, eventually, the stress fills you up like an over-inflated balloon. If you'd only talk to me about these things, maybe I could help you reduce a little bit of that stress. Don't you think?"

He understood she was right. He did tend to get too involved in some cases, especially the ones like he was working now. And each time he'd over-inflated, she'd bring him back. They'd never really spoken in-depth about the circumstances surrounding his work, nothing more than what she'd get from a newspaper, anyway; but even without the details, she was somehow able to moderate his stress, make the world feel right again.

He said, "I know you mean well, but I just can't. Please understand I just don't want all this sleazy grime dripping off you too."

Carol wrapped her arms around his neck and shoulders.

"It's okay. I just worry about you. That's my job, you know."

Tobias said nothing, just grabbed his wife's hands and gave the left one a kiss.

"Is Juan still coming over?"

"Yeah." Tobias looked at his watch. "Shoulda been here by now, actually."

"Okay, well, while you're busy in here all by yourself protecting me from God knows what, I'm going to get the burgers started."

He watched her leave, then looked at his wine glass, noticed it was empty. Just as he was about to heave himself from his chair and trudge to the pantry for another bottle, he heard the distinctive growl of Sanchez's car pull into the driveway. He went to the door to greet him. A minute later, they were both back in the kitchen.

 

"Okay, so what'd you find out about our guy?" Asked Tobias.

"For starters, a name: Jack Clark." Sanchez beamed triumphantly.

Tobias gave Sanchez a prideful nod. The kid had been instrumental in a lot of cases he'd worked in the past, but this is the first time he'd broken one wide open.

"Good work, kid."

Sanchez remained quiet, standing in front of Tobias, drinking in a rare victory.

"So are you going to tell me what's in the envelope you got there, or do I need to wait for some kind of end zone dance?"

Sanchez looked down at the envelope in his right hand, gave Tobias a sheepish smile.

"I've been a busy guy."

"Do tell."

"As luck would have it, the only vehicle in the lot not registered with the office was an older model Ford truck. I had a guy from the motor pool come out to open it up. At first, we found nothing. No registration, no insurance. Nothing. Ran the plates. Empty again. Turns out, they're stolen."

"That just leaves the VIN." Tobias offered.

"Exactly. Little metal plate, driver's side, at the very end of the dash, right? It's gone, too."

Tobias looked at Sanchez, puzzled.

"So how'd you find him?"

"Auto shop." Sanchez smiled. "In high school, I learned the VIN number is also stamped onto the engine of every car, and it's almost impossible to remove. Once I got the VIN off the block, I used it to track everything about the history of the truck, including insurance and registration. Once I got his name, I punched it into our system. It spat out two reports. "

Tobias grimaced. "Let me guess: the first one's for his kid, and the other's for his wife."

"You got it. Only child was a daughter; wife's dead. He's alone, he drives a larger vehicle, and the stats on his registration state he's six three and 240. Can you believe that? All those hours we spent with the brass at headquarters appear to have pegged the guy accurately. Pretty crazy, huh?"

Tobias nodded; Sanchez opened the envelope, handed his boss decent headshot of Clark, probably from his driver's license.

"So, you want the details?" He asked.

Tobias leaned his head forward, brought his hands up to his face, and rubbed his eyes. He really didn't want to look at the files, tried to convince himself he could get by without having to. He knew for certain they wouldn't contain any concrete facts he didn't already know. Or could guess. So why should he? They now had a face and a name to go with the crimes; and to top it off, he had a general idea about where Clark would be in the next couple of days, if not sooner. But he knew better: information is power, notwithstanding its sometimes wicked message. While the information in the files might not give him anything more about the physicality of the man, it might help him understand his psychology a little better.

"Give me the short version. I don't need dates, just the details."

Sanchez took a breath, then let it out.

"Little over two years ago, Clark's daughter, Jessica, was abducted from a park just outside of  Glendale. Apparently, her mother was checking on a sick dog some kids had found underneath a tree. Report says she had her back turned for at most a couple of minutes, but by the time she turned around, her daughter was gone. An Amber alert was issued, search parties were formed, but she was never found. At least not in time. Fast forward six months: a couple of desert rats are out riding their dual sports up in the wilderness just south of San Bernardino. One of them sees what he thinks is a dead animal. He rides up for a closer look and finds Jessica instead. Coroner report said the girl was infected with all kinds of STD's. It also said she was left out in the desert alive. She had a broken leg and was likely in shock, but in the end dehydration and exposure got her. She was eight."

"Jesus Christ. What about the wife?"

"Suicide. Clark's own words in the report expressed she never got over the abduction, blamed herself for it. She slipped into alcoholism then depression, finally couldn't take it anymore and took an overdose of prescription medication on the anniversary of her daughter's disappearance."

Suddenly Tobias felt very tired again. But there was something else there, too. Anger. "The desert?" He said it like it just hit him. "My granddaughter's about that age. What in God's name is wrong with these people?"

"These aren't people, boss. They're less than animals. Don't even belong in our gene pool, in my opinion."

Tobias got up from his chair, walked over to the pantry, and pulled out a fresh bottle of wine. Pinot Noir this time. He didn't want something too heavy. He uncorked the bottle, poured three glasses, gave one to Sanchez and placed the other on the counter for Carol.

"This guy's fueled himself with rage for over two years. He's been visualizing an outcome, and he's committed to making it happen." Tobias said, almost matter-of-factly.

"Yeah, and he's well-prepared too." Sanchez added.

Tobias took a sip of his wine, then placed the glass on the table.

"Can't be that well-prepared; otherwise, his truck wouldn't be sitting in the parking lot of that apartment complex. Unless, of course, you did roll up right as he was finishing up."

"Unlikely, unless he likes babysitting corpses. The pathologist at the scene said the body temp was right at 96 degrees, which means the time of death was fairly early in the morning."

"He might have abandoned it, then. But why would he abandon it there? Makes absolutely no sense. He's been smart all along. This isn't his style. Something must have forced him away from his truck. Maybe he intended to retrieve it later."

Sanchez  just shrugged and shook his head.

"At any rate, I think we're going to have to assume he knows where they took his daughter. But he's tied up now because he needs a vehicle. That's going to take a little time, which gives us a little breathing room." He paused for a moment, thinking, then said, "But he has to know we're closing in. We've got his truck. Even with all the work he did to deflect discovery with respect to its ownership, he's smart enough to know we're going to be able to get a history from it. So his sense of urgency has to be in orbit right now."

"Guy like that, motivated, he's probably already got another set of wheels. Hell, he's probably there right now."

"Maybe not. My money's on him hitting this place sooner rather than later, but he's more than likely going to do a little recon before he moves on it. From what we know about him, that's just the way he works. But once he gets the lay of the place, he's going to be on them like the plague."

Sanchez took a sip of his wine, made a face a child might make when biting into something bitter.

"Don't you people ever drink beer like the rest of us po' folk?"

"Beer, my son, is for pedestrians. You stick with me, and I'll show you how to pass off affluence like a bad haircut. But it all starts with the wine. Besides, it's good for you."

Sanchez was quiet for a moment, then said, "So what are we going to do, boss? Hell, we have no idea where this place even is. By the time we find it, he'll have done his business and split."

"But we do have an idea about where it is."

"We do?"

"We've got a general location. The only anomalous activity in Eduardo's bank account points to Baker. More than likely, there's a dummy corporation hanging out up there doing business as Desert Tours. It's probably used to ferry customers to and from their more lucrative operation. You up for a trip to the desert tomorrow?"

"Tomorrow?"

Tobias gave Sanchez a tired smile. "Under the circumstances, I don't see we have much of a choice."

Clark watched the scenery roll by as Jerry drove the Mustang east on Florence then south on Compton. Until recently, he'd been accustomed to the relative comfort afforded by a middle-class lifestyle. He never had a lot of money. He worked hard at what he did, saved, invested, bought a house, built a reality around him far and away foreign from what he was now watching. Underlying the dirt and the violence and the tension in South Central L.A. was a layer of security he figured was a simple reality for the working class people who did their best to get by in a community under siege by gang violence. Wrought iron bars were everywhere. Roll-up metal garage doors sealed tight the fronts of shops and street-facing windows. Patrol cars specially equipped to survive bullets, explosives, and riots patrolled the streets. He'd lived in places where he could leave his home and his vehicle unlocked without having to worry about theft. At one time in his life he was naive enough to believe that a sense of security was supposed to be part of the American dream. As he grew older, he understood there was no such thing. But Compton didn't even look like America. He may have just as easily been in Beirut. He felt his pocket vibrate, pulled his attention away from the urban blight scrolling by. He removed his phone, saw it was Carri.

"Clark." He always answered the phone by simply stating his last name. He felt a little awkward doing so this time. They had shared something special, and he felt she deserved a little more than a terse recitation of his surname.

"Hi. I found two vehicles you might be interest in so far. You have a minute?"

"Yeah, tell me about them."

"Okay, the first one's a Jeep. It's a 2003 Wrangler, has 85 thousand miles, and it's supposedly in really good condition. The second is a 2002 Ford Crew Cab. I thinks it's like a ... yeah, the ad says it's a 350? It has 150 thousand miles on it."

He did a quick analysis: the Jeep would give him versatility, but it would fall well short of offering him the protection he was looking for. That's the risk of driving a low-profile vehicle. Further, it wouldn't provide any kind of sleeping space he'd require if he were lucky enough to get out of the mess he was heading into. The truck, on the other hand, would be a rolling fortress, would have plenty of room to stretch out on the many nights he'd find himself without a bed. The downside would be the exposure he'd risk driving such a large vehicle. And it had a lot of miles.

"What kind of fuel does the truck take?" The question was posed to break the stalemate.

"The description said it's a turbo diesel. So I guess that's the kind of gas it takes, right?"

"How much?"

"The guy wants a little more than 19 grand for it. The Jeep has an asking price of a little over 14."

"Call the guy with the truck. Tell him you'll pay what he wants. You know where the money is, right?"

"I'm assuming it's in your briefcase."

"Right. Take 25 out. When I finish my business here, I'll give you a call and we can pick up the truck later tonight. Make sure the guy's willing to sell tonight, okay?"

"Will do." She paused.  "Jack?"

"Yeah."

"Are you okay?"

He thought about the question. Okay with respect to what? He was presently driving toward Compton in the passenger seat of a stolen car that belonged to a dead man. He didn't know the driver at all, and he had no idea what sort of challenges his current destination might bring. But he felt comfortable with it. He'd already convinced himself he'd get through this part of his mission. His relationship with Carri, though, troubled him. Much more so than a pending confrontation with a bunch of hardcore gang members. He found it terrifying and exhilarating all at once. But the destination with respect to Carri was far from uncertain. It was a dead end. They both knew it. And he figured neither one of them were okay with it. But in the end, he knew meeting her was something he'd never regret.

"I'm fine," he lied, then slapped the phone shut.

 

By the time Clark ended his conversation, Jerry was pulling into the short driveway of a green stucco home. It was a squat, simple, blocky house, one of tens of thousands built in California during the 1950's post-war boom. And it looked well-maintained, too. Compared to what he'd seen on his way in, the house and the others around it were in a great state of repair, their lawns looked well kept, and their street was free from clutter. At the front of the house was a small porch, its perimeter stretched by a dark brown, decades-old couch. There were two black men sitting on it, enjoying a beverage and an evening smoke. They were illuminated by a feeble yellow light that was swaying gently in the dying Santa Ana wind.

The man on the left was so small the cushions on the couch nearly wrapped completely around him, obscuring much of him from view. Light from the lamp reflected off the top of his balding head, which was girded around the sides with close-cut gray hair. He had a razor sharp face and a wide nose, upon which was perched a pair of thick glasses set in black plastic frames. His emaciation was exaggerated by over-sized clothing that clung to his body, fabric that desperately attempted to find support in sharp, angular places, succumbing to gravity where it couldn't. He looked distinguished, like he could've been an Emeritus Faculty member at any number of colleges or universities in the area.

The man on the right was a different story altogether. He was large. While he couldn't determine his height, Clark figured the guy had to weigh north of 300 pounds. He had a big, round, meaty face and a bald head. He wore a black, short-sleeved shirt that barely accommodated the massive muscularity that rose and fell on him like mountains off a valley floor. Upon seeing Clark, the big man got up from his seat, revealing a stature that would put him about a half a head above his own height. He then moved in front of the older man to assume a protective position. The light on the porch bounced off his forearms, illuminating a proliferation of tattoos. The kind a guy might get in prison.

Clark and Jerry exited the car, then moved toward the two men. The thin man used his right hand to move the big man to the side.

"What's this?" He asked, gesturing toward Clark.

Jerry replied, "Friend of my bro's. Said he need our help. Gave me this car to get it. Said he need sumthin from us."

"Like what?" The thin man's countenance changed. He looked at Jerry with impatience mixed with a little anger.

Jerry said nothing. The thin man fixed his gaze on Clark, giving him a look void of trust, stuffed with suspicion. He stubbed out his smoke on the railing in front of him and exhaled.

"What you want, coming into this neighborhood, boy? Don't you know there's no whites here? Could get yourself killed."

The thin man was the leader, and that was probably good news. Generally, no matter who you are, age comes with patience and reason. But it can also come with the heavy weight of perception, cemented firmly by a lifetime of negative experiences.
