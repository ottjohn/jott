Chapter 12

 

 

Sanchez arrived to pick up Tobias just before one in the afternoon. He exited the unmarked and walked a meandering concrete path that lead from the sidewalk to the front of Tobias's large tri-level home. As he approached the house, he noticed Carol was again busying herself with the flower garden that tightly bordered both sides of the walkway. Having been semi-retired for the last couple of years, Carol had used almost all of her spare time reading home improvement magazines in an effort to maintain the house and beautify the yard. When she first started her home improvement journey, her work was at most mediocre. As Sanchez approached the porch that lead to the front door, however, he saw a transformation that looked both artistic and professional. He sprang up the five concrete steps to a small patio bordered by black wrought iron fencing and gave the house's heavy wooden front door three hard raps. A moment later, Carol appeared.

"Good afternoon," he said, smiling.

"Yes it is!" She replied. "You're getting the grouchy guy out of the house so I can finally get some work done!" She gestured with her head toward the interior of the house, signifying Tobias was still inside, getting ready.

Sanchez smiled and motioned toward the walkway. "I noticed you're in the middle of another grand production out here. What are you doing with it now?"

"Oh that. I decided to rip out the miniature roses and put in Agave, Artemisia, and Baby's Breath. Most of it's going to be Agave. They're fairly drought-resistant, which is important these days with the Colorado drying up and water being so scarce and all. I'm hoping the Artemisia and Baby's Breath will add some really nice colors and texture around the thick, broad leaves of the Agave. Paul kept complaining about bees infesting the rose bushes, too, and these plants seem to attract less insects, at least the stinging kind."

"I'm sure they'll all go together really well. It's already looking very nice," he said, honestly wishing he had the patience to do something like that with his small yard.

Carol glanced expectantly over her shoulder into the open doorway, looked at Sanchez, and shrugged. She said, "Hang on a second, dear. I think Paul wants to get out of here, so let me make sure he knows you're waiting." Carol left Sanchez on the porch with the door open, a tacit invitation to either stay where he was or enter. He stayed put. Less than a minute later, Tobias arrived at the door, two travel mugs full of coffee occupying both hands.

"Afternoon Sanchez." Tobias said, mock frown plastered on his face. He motioned toward the freshly-started yard work and said, "Let's get going so I can get back here and keep an eye on Carol."

Carol gave Tobias a soft punch to the arm. Tobias looked at Sanchez, rubbed his shoulder, feigned pain, and said,

"You see what she's doing, right?"

Sanchez looked toward the yard, replied "Yeah, it's looking good, boss. Wish I could get her to do this to my yard."

"Yeah, well in my world good is synonymous with expensive, pal. She probably didn't tell you about the little porcelain wood nymphs she wants to plant around all those alien-looking plants, did she?"

Sanchez gave Tobias a blank look.

"Well, they're about fifty bucks a pop! And they're just going to sit out here in the elements and get trampled on by neighborhood kids and dogs. They're a monumental waste of money, too, in my opinion. I mean, I may as well just take a bunch of fifties from my wallet and plant those in the ground. Same effect, right?" Tobias smiled at his wife, kissed her on the forehead and said, "Just kidding sweetie; but, like you said, a man's gotta complain about something, or he's just not being a man!" He then walked down the steps, past her work, and to the unmarked.

Sanchez didn't move, gave Carol a quizzical look.

"Told you. He's being a grouch. And he didn't get a lot of sleep last night, either. Something's bothering him about one of the cases you two are working." She rolled her eyes dramatically. "You know how he gets." She patted Sanchez on the back and then said,

"Keep him safe, Juan. He's getting close to retirement, and I'd like to keep him around for a while so we can actually spend some time together."

Her message was delivered with a lightness that betrayed its underlying seriousness. Sanchez knew Carol would be lost without Tobias. Hell, either one of them would be a basket case if something were to happen to the other. In all his life, he'd never seen two people so perfectly matched. Their relationship was like something out of a fifties television show. It was  different for him, though. He was so distant from his family he was barely able to work out a one-week visit every couple of years. The sum effect of that reality gave him a constant feeling of disconnect, which built with time. Somehow, Tobias was able to sense this, just knew when he needed to unwind on neutral ground, be in a place where he could feel safe. Whenever he needed the sanctuary of home, Tobias and Carol were always there for him, holding their door open, accepting him as a member of their family.

Sanchez gave Carol a look of reassurance, said, "I'll watch out for him."

Carol grabbed Sanchez's hand, gave it a gentle squeeze. Sanchez gave Carol a peck on the cheek, then turned and walked down the path toward Tobias, who was already waiting in the car.

 

Sanchez pulled away from the Tobias residence and made his way to the 261, which took him out of the upper middle class community of Lemon Heights, and sent him hurtling toward the perennial metallic logjam that is Interstate Five.

The first time he'd visited Tobias's house, he was overwhelmed by its size and spaciousness. The exterior presented an older, three-level ranch that just about screamed Brady Bunch. Behind its ordinary facade, however, was almost 5000 square feet of ceramic tile and hardwood floors, two fire places, a large gourmet kitchen, three full bathrooms, and four bedrooms. The master bedroom sported a pair of walk-in closets and a bath with a very large Jacuzzi tub and a separate shower. The house had large windows all around that offered spectacular views of both the valley and the full acre of land on which it was situated, which was populated with various types of trees, gardens, and a large Koi pond.

At the time, Tobias had occupied the home for more than ten years and so had become accustomed to his guests'  trouble with reconciling a cop's pay with the obvious immensity of the mortgage payment that would accompany such ownership. The key, Tobias had told him, was to not buy the property but to inherit it. As it turned out, Carol had a very rich uncle who'd worked in the financial sector in New York City. The house was a winter retreat for him. He had no children, so he had essentially adopted Carol as his own child when she arrived in the L.A. area to attend college. Upon his passing, Carol had inherited the property, which was fully paid for. Place like that in the country, the relative quiet compared to the big city, and nothing to pay for but property tax. Some guys have all the luck.

Tobias had remained quiet since they left the house, simply leaned back in the passenger seat and appeared to be ruminating about what the day might hold. The inconsistency in the flow of traffic on the five, though, brought him out of his meditation; so he retrieved his PDA, punched it a couple of times, and began focusing on that.

"Whatcha looking at?" Sanchez asked, attempting to break the silence.

"One of the documents we got from the thumb drive."

"You got that on your Palm?" He asked.

"It's actually a Samsung, but, yes, I scanned it this morning and transferred the image."

"I thought there were ten documents."

"There are. None of the others are terribly interesting. Just the sort of crap you'd expect pedophiles to send each other. This one, though, is different. Strange, actually."

Sanchez crawled in the traffic, finally passing over Newport Avenue. He briefly glanced over at Tobias, then said,

"So what's so special about the document you scanned?"

Tobias pondered the question for a moment, then replied, "Well, on its surface, it has nothing to do with pornography. It appears to be a marketing blast from a local bank about an investment opportunity."

"So what's so strange about that? I get crap like that in my inbox all of the time."

"There's something not right about it. The thumb drive was all about porn, except for this one document; and, you have to admit, it's certainly not representative of what we would expect a couple of pedophiles to exchange."

Sanchez though about that, then offered, "Are you sure they're both pedos?"

"Yeah, there was another document that was signed the same way as this one. It was a copy of an email suggesting Hassenback visit a particular pornographic site. It had lots of images of little girls with their clothes off. I'm sure the forensics team at LAPD Headquarters is already working with the Internet Service Providers to track those responsible for the content, but I'm going to give them a call later today just to make sure. If we're lucky, they'll all be in the states and we can get the sites removed without much of a problem. They're probably in China, though, in which case there won't be much we can do."

Sanchez took the interchange and merged onto the 55, heading southwest. Tobias sat next to him, furiously typing something into his PDA. A moment passed, then he smiled and let out a barely audible chuckle.

"What?" Asked Sanchez.

"It appears the bank in the document, the San Andreas Bank, doesn't even exist. In fact " He punched his PDA a few more times, waited a moment, and continued, "neither does its address."

"You're getting all that from your Palm?" Asked Sanchez.

"PDA. Personal. Digital. Assistant," replied Tobias, giving Sanchez a mock look of impatience. "And, yes, I'm getting all of this as we drive."

"Damn, boss. I gotta get me one of those!"

Tobias said nothing.

"So we're looking at either a marketing mock up or some kind of cipher through which these guys communicate," said Sanchez. "A code seems pretty elaborate for bunch of pedophiles to be using, though, don't you think?"

"I'm really not sure," replied Tobias. "But at this point, I'm going operate under that assumption." He scanned the document and then continued, "Too many key words here are raising red flags. Given the context, I'm pretty sure we have something here. We just need to figure out what it is."

"Roger that," replied Sanchez. He took a left on East Chestnut Avenue, a right on Warren, looked for the third house on the left, then parked in front of it.

Clark sat at the table in his rundown weekly, rapidly bouncing his knee in a futile attempt to spend the nervous energy that pulsed through him like an electric current. At 3:15 in the afternoon, he'd sent an email to a guy named Eduardo. From the account of a dead man. A guy he killed, Mr. Hassenback, former child molester. The moment he sent the email, however, he'd wished he hadn't. Not because he felt he'd made a mistake emulating Hassenback's style, but because he hadn't really thought about whether Eduardo kept up with the news.

To be sure, the murder of Zach Hassenback had not been front page material. In fact, it had barely merited a mention in the crime blotter in the least-read section of the paper. In a city full of celebrity gossip and upper-crust crime, the story of a convicted sex offender struck down in a non-descript part of town held very little appeal to the masses. But there was still the possibility, and to send an email from a dead man would do Clark no good if its recipient knew the sender to be deceased. In fact, if Eduardo knew Hassenback had been murdered, such an email just might be enough to send him to the authorities for protection. That could take Eduardo out of his reach, and Eduardo was the only link Clark had to the organization that destroyed his family.

Clark silently watched his computer and waited for a reply, admonishing himself for his mistake and mentally stacking up adjectives to describe his uncharacteristic flaw in planning. His mental self-flogging, however, came to a quick end at 3:30 in the afternoon when his mailbox chimed to signify a newly arrived message. The very fact the email was now in his inbox told him two things: one, Eduardo had no idea Hassenback was dead; and, two, he now had a very welcome green light to continue with his operation, albeit much more carefully. The electric nervous feeling now replaced with productive energy, he took a sip of afternoon brew, a vanilla roast Carri had left behind, and opened Eduardo's reply:

H.

Good to hear from you! Yeah, let's talk. Whenever you want man. No worries. I can do it today. Not much going on. Just sitting around getting high and watching Gilligan's Island. Gilligan is a trip, man. Shit. Hey, you should come over. Let me know, dude.

 

Eduardo

Clark thought about the email. Based on his observations of Hassenback and his apparent revulsion for companionship, he'd figured there might be a chance Hassenback and Eduardo were nothing more than acquaintances bound together by a common interest and the internet. The email, however, left some doubt: your place or mine?

He suspected he wouldn't be so lucky as to have Eduardo email him directions to his place. That would be way too easy. Even if the email had given no intimation the two had previously visited, asking Eduardo for that would be too risky, stupid even. He would have to think of something else.

Perhaps he could get Eduardo to meet him at the Hassenback residence ... Clark grunted to himself, quickly dismissed the idea as a byproduct of a synaptic misfire and wishful thinking. The place was still more than likely red hot, and it probably still had remnants of police tape scattered about the yard. Even if the residents and the police were no longer actively monitoring the property, the tape alone would be more than enough to keep Eduardo from going anywhere near it. Going there was just out of the question. He took another sip vanilla brew, which was all but destroyed by the little coffee maker, and tried word association to spark some ideas: hot, red hot, uncomfortable, inadvisable, suffocating, flee the fire, room to breathe. The house was too hot. Perhaps he could make Hassenback too hot as well. He placed his coffee cup on the table, slipped into Hassenback's sloppy style, and wrote,

Ed.

Lets meet at 7. Cant meet at your place. POs been clingy. Fuckers been checking up to much. Meet at Gypsy Den in santa ana. You no were that is? Let me no.

 

H

PS: forget Gilligan man. You shuld be loking at mery ann. To old but still hot ;)

Clark scrutinized his email, gave the Gilligan's Island reference a few minutes' thought. Hassenback and Eduardo seemed to have an obsession with the old television show, having mentioned it in 16 of their 40 emails. No other shows, just Gilligan's Island. He figured it couldn't hurt to leave it in, understood, too, that leaving it out might be a mistake. He scanned the rest of the message for style flaws. He found it painful to write so sloppily, felt a little anxious about getting into a protracted electronic conversation. Several additional exchanges might cause him to become careless, cause him to send a message so inconsistent with Hassenback's style as to attract Eduardo's attention. After several re-reads, he could find no glaring flaws, so he let it go. Eduardo's reply came just five minutes later:

H.

I know where it is. Good idea. Fucking parole officers! We can have a cup of brew and talk about the opportunity. Then we can go to my place if you want to get with the program. Hope you do! The place rocks dude.  Yeah, too old man, but Ginger is better. You know it. But Gilligan is the man. You should see what he is doing with this monkey man. Ha. Trip. See you at seven. Hey, we should do another marathon!

 

Eduardo

Clark quickly scanned the email, felt a brief surge of satisfaction that his electronic diversion had worked. He looked to the lower right-hand corner of his laptop screen. It was 3:45 in the afternoon. He had a lot of preparation ahead of him, lots of things he'd have to work out before he could meet Eduardo. Of course, he wasn't going to meet the guy in public. Rather, he'd set up about a block away from the coffee shop and wait for him. He'd purposefully chosen the Gypsy Den because he'd been there before, knew it was a little out of the way and lacked large crowds. He had no idea what Eduardo looked like, but given the relatively few number of arrivals to these types of establishments in the early evening, picking him out wouldn't be too difficult. He'd just sit and watch for him, identify him, wait for him to get discouraged and leave, and then follow him home. Poor Eduardo. His little buddy was dead. And as soon as Clark got what he needed from him, he would be too.

The two men in the older model Jaguar were accustomed to driving ten mile circuits that would take them through mostly lower middle class residential areas clustered in and around the valley. Generally, they would watch four or five houses on any given route, the distance between each being approximately the same. The routes always included passes around public parks and libraries, and they were always nested within a web of streets, parkways, and highways. At the first indication of heat, the men could take any number of roads and simply disappear into the urban jungle. A full circuit of a route typically required about 45 minutes to drive, although the boss insisted they take at least an hour, but not more than an hour and a half. The standard operating procedure required the use of a different vehicle every two circuits. Upon completion of those, the drivers were to return to base to collect a new car and continue their assignment. The organization had seven cars in its fleet, ranging from the Jag they were now droving to a late model Cadillac Escalade. The operation itself was called trolling.

The driver, Rick, knew the present operation didn't fall into typical operational parameters. Instead of being tasked to watch four or five houses along a circuit, he and his partner, Jason -- his trainee, really -- were now watching just one. Further, the single house they were presently working was situated in a lavish neighborhood bordering Beverly Hills, a long way and a about two million dollars from some of the lower-end neighborhoods they were accustomed to cruising. While the route was unusual, Rick had run it before, each time tasked to watch the same house. He figured they were testing the waters. The product potential offered by such a route was enormous, but the risk was very high, which necessarily truncated the scope of their directive. He also figured it was a waste of time. Take someone's kid from the hood, and you'd need a shoe horn to get a cop out of his car to investigate, but take one from the neighborhood he was currently trolling? Forget about. You'd have so many cops up your ass, you'd need surgery just to get them out. But he didn't plan the routes, nor would he ever. They were planned a little further up in the organization, by people holding positions he figured he'd never fill. He really wouldn't want to, anyway. He didn't like the mission. But the pay was just too good to pass up. Once a route was planned, it was submitted to the boss, who either approved or killed it. When a route was delivered, it was to be driven. No questions asked. Bottom line, it was the boss's orders, and you simply did what he asked.

Rick had been driving the entire morning, listening to a proliferation of senseless babble spilling out of Jason for most of that time. After about two hours of mind-numbing stupidity, he'd turned up the radio almost full blast, attempting to drown out the white noise that seemed to just erupt from the man. The strategy had given him some relief but provided its own set of headaches. In the meantime, they got as comfortable as they could while driving the route, smoked cigarettes to pass the time, and caught periodic glimpses of a spirited little girl making random appearances in the front yard of the residence they were tasked to watch. At times, she seemed to be running with imaginary friends, calling out to them and beckoning them with gestures and squeals. At her heels every step of the way was a short, yappy dog nipping at her shoes as she ran.

Just as they were about to once again drive past the house and begin another circuit, Rick's phone rang. He sucked in his breath, shifted gears to mentally prepare for an intentionally vague conversation. It was the organization's way to reduce risk while on the phone: never mention specifics, abstract the conversation to a level only the parties on the line could really understand. He exhaled, flipped open his phone, and held it to his ear.

"Rick. Talk," he said. There was a brief pause on the other end of the line, then

You two keeping an eye out?

"Yes sir. Yeah, everything looks fine. Everything's quiet and the product looks good, just like you said."

You keeping to the prescribed pattern and interval?

"As ordered," he lied.

Make sure. No fuck ups.

"Yes sir. Understood," Rick deadpanned. He wasn't sure about Jason, but he'd been doing this sort of thing off and on for years in one form or another. Only with adults. The target may change; the geography may change; but the fundamentals of surveillance always remained the same. So what gave the fuck on the other end of the line the right to tell him how to time his routes? Rick moved the phone slightly away from his ear, allowing the moment to pass; he worked on his breathing, attempting to find his inner Chi. After several seconds, a peacefulness descended on him, pushing the negative energy away.

You still there? The voice bristled, impatient.

"Yes sir," answered Rick.

Okay. Keep to your pattern. Anything happens, you let me know. Immediately. That's the future of our product. Anyone makes a move on it, run interference. Is that clear?

"Understood. What time you want us out of here?" Rick asked.

You two are staying all night, into tomorrow. Get some coffee and stay awake. And don't forget to swap!

Rick once again took a deep breath, focusing on the harmonious connection between his mind and body. Perhaps his boss needed to do that too. He seemed so tense. Maybe someday he would suggest it to him. Or maybe he'd just put a foot up his scrawny ass instead.

"Roger that," he acknowledged.

One more thing ... The Voice changed its tone, lost its edge.

"Yes sir."

How's the new guy Jason working out?

"Okay. Problems here and there, but they may be manageable." Rick kept his voice at an even keel, spoke in a business-like manner to keep the guy sitting next to him from figuring out he was the topic of this new thread in the conversation.

He's got one more week of apprenticeship, then I'm going to need a report from you.

"I'm building it as I go, sir."

How would you rate him on a scale of one to ten right now?

"About a six," he lied. He wasn't exactly sure how he'd rate the guy. He knew he didn't want to work with him because he was a bona-fide moron. But this kind of job didn't exactly require the mind of an Einstein, either. Truthfully, he just didn't know.

Well, if he can't work up to a solid eight, you know what to do.

Before Rick could get another word in, the line went dead.
