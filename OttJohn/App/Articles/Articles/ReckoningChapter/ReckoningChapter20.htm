Chapter 20

 

 

The alarm clock let out a pitched, electronic wail at four in the morning. Clark sat up, then pressed random buttons on its illuminated control panel until the noise stopped. He'd already been awake, had been for almost half an hour. Since he'd been a young boy, he'd never had to depend on any kind of device to wake him. He'd used alarm clocks before, but only as a redundancy measure in case the clock in his head stopped functioning for some reason. But it never had. It always woke him at precisely the time he set it, give or take five minutes. On big days, it woke him a little earlier. And today was a big day, one of the biggest of his life.

He shifted to a sitting position on the edge of the bed, then took a moment to roll his head from side to side, stretching the muscles in his neck, relieving the tension that had built up in him over the past few hours. Already his heart was pumping with the extra force from an adrenalin rush, bringing with it alertness, and a readiness for what lay ahead. He'd been preparing for this day for two years, and finally it had come. In just a matter of hours, he'd be an instrument of revenge; he'd make good on a posthumous promise he'd made to his wife and little girl.

He pushed himself off the bed, walked out of the bedroom, down the hall, and rapped on Tiny's door. He listened carefully after the knock, heard a grumbling acknowledgement, then turned and headed back to the bedroom, almost colliding with Carri as he made his way through the doorway.

"Good morning," he said, smiling.

She looked up at him, her eyes still partially glazed over from the previous night's slumber.

"I'm going to make some coffee." She gave him a peck on the cheek and then moved sluggishly down the hall, as if she were wading through water.

He followed her with his eyes until she disappeared into the living room. Even at four in the morning, in her pajamas, multi-directional bursts of hair cast about her face and draped over her shoulders, she looked like an angel. Beautiful. Exquisite. He returned to the bedroom, quickly pulled on the same clothes he wore the day before, then gathered up his briefcase and laptop and placed both on the bed. He opened the briefcase and did a quick calculation, figured he had about 150 grand left.

He removed just enough money to make room for his computer, stuffing the excess banded, green bricks into one of Carri's black, lacey pillowcases as he did. He guessed he'd probably transferred fifty grand by the time he'd finished. That left him with a hundred, which would be sufficient to start a new life if he were lucky enough to finish what he was doing. He figured he would drive east, perhaps to New York or Chicago, then blend in with the anonymous press of humanity that such cities are known for. But he knew the odds of that were long at best. The police had to know who he was by now. They'd look up the VIN on the engine block of his truck, and a couple of keystrokes later, they'd have his identity and a record of what happened to his family. Dead pedophile in the apartment. His truck in the parking lot. It wasn't calculus. Three kills already, more on the way. A jury might understand his motivation, but they'd be bound by law. That meant life in prison, without parole. At least. But they'd have to catch him first. Clark closed his briefcase, then turned to leave the bedroom.

He paused at the doorway, turned. He took one final look around, telling himself he was only making certain he'd left nothing behind. But he caught himself looking at the bed. He was staring at two depressions, side-by-side, still imprinted on the empty sheets. Images of the previous night's love-making moved through his mind, a lifetime of hope, dreams, and promises all burned up in a single passionate hour. A gust of wind drew his attention to the open window. It shifted the air in the room, washed him with the perfumed scent of an impossible future. He breathed it in, burying the fragrance deep within his lungs. He exhaled slowly, took another deep breath, then turned and left.

 

Clark walked into the kitchen, found Carri and Tiny making small talk between hungry sips of strong, aromatic coffee. Carri moved to the counter as he approached, picked up a large mug of morning brew, and extended it to him. He noticed her hands were shaking, saw Tiny noticed too. Tiny looked at Clark, said,

"I'm gonna wait out by the car, bro. Need to make sure it's ready to go, know what I mean?" He drained the rest of his coffee, placed his mug on the counter, then turned to Carri.

"Thanks for the hospitality."

Carri nodded an acknowledgement, then she and Clark watched the enormous man make his way through the living room and out of the house.

"So where'd you find him?" She asked, gesturing with her thumb toward the living room. "He's as big as a truck."

They both knew it was a strategic question, delivered in a desperate attempt to distract from the elephant crowding the room. Clark searched her eyes, saw behind the fatigue, recognized a look he knew all too well. He'd seen it in the mirror every day for the past two years: a look of emptiness, a reflection of a soul hollowed out by the death of a dream. Suddenly the energy he'd felt earlier, the sense of purpose welling within him, started to drain away. He placed his coffee mug on the counter, closed the distance between them, and caught her up in his arms.

"Thank you," he whispered.

Carri remained motionless, her forehead against his chest, her vision cast down to the floor.

"I want you to understand something: I've been walking down a very bad road for some time now. My life was full darkness until I met you. You allowed me to feel again, to experience emotions I didn't think were possible for me anymore. Hell, I even caught myself smiling yesterday." He paused, kissed the side of her head gently. "Maybe I should've pushed you away. Maybe allowing something to happen between us was wrong. But I'll never regret it. And I'll never forget you."

Carri nodded weakly but otherwise remained motionless.

Clark hesitated, then said, "Can you do one more thing for me?"

Carri nodded.

"I'm sure there's going to be children where I'm headed. I'll give you a call when it's done. I'll stay until you arrive. Someone needs to be with them. Someone they can trust. Can you do that?"

Carri nodded.

"I have to go now. I'll call you as soon as I can." The words moved through his mouth like stones. Heavy ones. There was no doubt in his mind that saying goodbye to her was one of the most difficult things he'd ever have to do. He turned to leave, started walking toward the living room.

"Jack?" Carri's voice cracked just a little, betraying a monumental struggle to keep her part of the goodbye under control. He turned, found her standing in the same spot, head still lowered, shoulders heaving slightly. He put his briefcase on the floor, then walked to her and took her in his arms again. He buried his face in her hair, breathed in her scent, then lowered his mouth to her ear.

"I know," he said. "I feel the same way."

Clark held onto her tightly. He didn't want to let go. He wanted to hold her forever. Because it felt so right. Like home. But he couldn't. He had a promise to keep. The embrace lasted a moment longer, then he kissed her on her forehead, turned to collect his briefcase, and walked out of the house.

 

Clark walked down the driveway and onto the street where he parked his truck. He opened the front passenger-side door, placed his briefcase on the floorboard, then headed toward the Mustang, looking up at the sky as he walked. It was clear, the stars dimmed by the overwhelming presence of a gibbous moon that cast just enough light to make the streetlamps running up and down the neighborhood superfluous. The air had a calm, chilly dryness to it, a signal that told him nothing about the weather would change much but the temperature. As he passed between the vehicles, he caught a glimpse of light falling out of Carri's front window. It was quickly obliterated by a large, dark figure.

"You okay, bro?" Asked Tiny, cocking his massive head to one side.

He paused for a moment. "Yeah, I'm fine." Then he gave Tiny a hard, calculating look. "What about you? You sure you want to go through with this? I appreciate where you're coming from, but you've got to under ..."

Tiny interrupted, "I told you las' night. I want in on this. I need to be. For my little sistah."

Clark wasn't convinced. Up to this point, Tiny had been nothing more than a distraction, something he'd tolerated the previous night as a way to obtain hardware. But distractions have a bad habit of becoming disruptions, and a disruption could slow him down, compromise his ability to complete what he set out to do. On the other hand, if Tiny were up to the task, he could prove to be an invaluable ally. The place in the desert remained an enigma: he had no conception of its layout or how well it was defended. Tiny could provide an extra set of eyes and ears, could shift the odds of a quick, deadly hit in his favor. But he had to be sure.

"You need to understand something, Tiny: the police know who I am. They have my truck. They more than likely know where I'm headed. Hell, they could even be there when I arrive, waiting for me." Tiny looked as though he were going to interrupt again, Clark held up a dismissive hand, and continued. "I've found the operation that killed my little girl, Tiny. I don't care who's there. The police, the FBI, the God Damned National Guard. It doesn't matter to me. I'm going to find a way in, and then I'm going to kill every one of those fuckers. I'm willing risk my life, to die, to get this done. And I'm okay with that. Are you?"

"Don't care who there, neither, bro. This shit been burning in me for ten years. It's time I did something. May not be the niggas who took my little sistah, but it's principle." Tiny paused for a moment, then looked at Clark with fixed determination. "This is principle, man. Ain't no way I'm gonna find that nigga who did that shit. I know that. But I can do this for her and help those kids out there in the desert? I'll lay down for that. Cops or no."

Clark nodded, now convinced. He looked at the rear of the Mustang, said, "Before we leave, I need to see what we have in the way of weapons. Then we're going to have to split it up."

Tiny cocked his head to the side, remained standing in front of him. Clark understood the hesitation, answered his question before Tiny could even ask it.

"I'm okay with you coming along. I have no plans to lose you along the way. In fact, I'm grateful for the help. But if we get separated, or if one of us gets pulled over, we have redundancy this way, okay?"

Tiny said nothing, just nodded in agreement, turned, and walked to the rear of the car.

 

Clark stared into the open bag, took a quick inventory, then shook his head and grunted. Whoever they found themselves up against in the desert, they'd have to be armed to the teeth to match what he was looking at.

Lying lengthwise against the rear of the bag were two Mossberg 500 12-gauge shotguns. Clark removed one and held it up for inspection: black stock with a pistol grip, six-shot pump action, eighteen and a half inch barrel, muzzle break to kill recoil. They were civilian facsimiles of their military counterparts, passed all of the same stringent tests the military threw at their hardware. And they were deadly, could blow six men in half at ten yards in just under ten seconds. In theory. If the shooter was quick enough. It was a matter of turning three actions into two, a matter of converting pump, point, shoot into the more fluid pump and point, shoot.

In the middle of the bag were four Austrian-made combat pistols. Steyrs, 9mm. Clark had field-tested some of them years ago when he was in the military, mostly for comparative analysis against the U.S.-favored Glock. The particular model he was looking at hadn't been in production since 1988. They were a decent gun, however: they were made from carbon steel, had a plastic grip, and a sixteen round. They also featured a gas-retarded blowback design, a mechanism for dispersing the gas released upon firing to minimize vertical muzzle rotation. Which made them deadly accurate at close range.

On the long side of the bag closest to him was a sniper rifle he'd read about but had never seen. It was a Russian-made Vintorez, lovingly called the Vinny. It was the combat weapon of choice for Ukrainian special forces that found themselves in high-risk operations requiring stealth. The rifle was short, only about a yard in length, which diminished its range to about 400 yards. But it wasn't made for long kill shots. It was a medium-range sniper rifle specialized in pushing its 9mm payload through flesh and light body armor. Drops the enemy like flies; keeps the shooter comfortably away from the action. The rifle had a selector for either automatic or single shot, came with a ten-round box clip, and the entire length of the barrel was wrapped with an integrated silencer to ensure its rounds were fired with deadly, muted pops.

Clark shook his head again, then just stared at Tiny in the darkness. "Where the hell did you get all of this?"

"The hood, man. Where you think?"

"There must be some serious fire power running around South Central. Gang warfare must be getting pretty sophisticated, much more so than I read about in the papers, anyway."

"Not really." Tiny answered. "Most is still handguns and knives. All this shit here ..." Tiny motioned to the bag. "All this shit come from some stupid Russian punks thought they's gonna take over the block, bring in a heroin operation. Long story short, we took 'em down one by one, took they house, collected they hardware, burned they shit so it didn't get picked up and sold by some other punks."

"Thank God for stupid Russian punks. Let's split this up. You got any sniper experience?"

Tiny shook his head in the darkness.

"Wasn't my primary job description, but I ribboned in the Army. Kept it up during and even after. Became a hobby." Clark paused, waited for an acknowledgement, got none. "I'll take the Vinny, one shotgun, and two of the Steyr's." Clark moved his hand around the inside of the bag. "Where's the ammo and the scope?"

"Zipped up on the side."

As Tiny opened the side compartment on the long end of the bag, Clark took the Vinny and one of the shotguns and moved them to his truck, placing them together under the rear bench seat. He then took the handguns, placed one under the driver's seat and the other in the center console, which was empty, save for sixteen cents in loose change and a receipt for Chinese take-out. On his final trip to the Mustang, he found Tiny holding the scope and three bags of ammunition. He handed them to Clark, then asked,

"What now?"

"Head out. We have a lot of ground to cover, and I want to be there before the sun's fully up. We need to hit a store on the way, though, so keep your eyes peeled."

"What more we need than what we got, bro?"

"Specifically, three canteens and some powdered laxative. We're also going to need some supplies. I'd like to say we can finish all of this in a day or two, but who knows."

Tiny cocked his head, smiled a big, toothy, Cheshire grin.

"Three canteens? And laxatives? You a little irregular?" He laughed.

"Nope. I'm functioning just fine. Two of the canteens are for us, the third and the laxatives I have other plans for."

"Whatever you say, bro." Tiny closed the trunk, started heading toward the driver's side of the car, then turned back to Clark. "Since you know where you're goin', I'll follow you. Just in case, though, which way we headed?"

"You know how to get to the 10 from here?"

"I live in L.A., man."

"Right. Head to the 10, then go north on the 215. Take that to the 15. In case we get separated,  let's plan on meeting up outside of Victorville. You got your cell?"

Tiny said nothing, just reached in his front pocket with one of his meaty hands and presented a little flip phone. It looked like a postage stamp affixed to the center of his palm. Clark gave him his number, Tiny punched it in. Less than a minute later, they were on their way.

Tobias met Sanchez at a place called Molly Brown's County Cafe, a breakfast establishment right in the heart of Victorville, just off the 15. They sat on opposite sides of a booth at the front of the restaurant, next to a giant plate-glass window that gave them an unimpeded view of the vehicles and foot-traffic moving up and down Mojave Boulevard. Tobias had a map of Baker and the surrounding area spread out in front of him, which he'd strategically placed between two heaping plates of food and two cups of strong, freshly-brewed coffee. He was busy tracing his fingers along the streets of Baker, paying particular attention to the town's northeast corner.

"I think our best bet's right here," he said, pointing to the part of town he'd been considering. "Looks like this area provides the easiest access to the 15, and it appears to be the most industrialized and anonymous part of town."

Sanchez scoffed. "Yeah, if you can call anything about that place industrialized. So you want me to focus my search there?"

"Not necessarily, but let's start there first. Maybe we can save a little time that way."

Sanchez added cream and sugar to his coffee, stirred it, took a sip, then followed up with a shovel full of scrambled eggs and hash browns. He took another sip of coffee and smiled across the table.

"Can't beat a greasy breakfast. This'll probably get me through the rest of the day."

Tobias looked up from the map, said, "Glad you're enjoying your breakfast. What do you think of starting on the east side of town?"

"Sounds like cake to me, boss. Whaddya say we get the show on the road so we can take these creeps down?"

Tobias took a sip of coffee then shook his head. The kid appeared to be a little too comfortable, perhaps still riding high after his identification of Clark the night before. Coolness in a high-stress, potentially dangerous environment is without exception a good thing. Overconfidence, on the other hand, is something to be avoided, something that could get a guy killed in their line of work. He gave Sanchez an appraising look, no different from the look a father might give his son just before his first solo road trip.

"Now, you tell me exactly what you're not going to do when you get there."

Sanchez caught the measured stare, appeared to process the message.

"I'm not going to stay in any one place for long. I'm not even going to get off my bike. If I find the business, I'll memorize the street address; if I find the vehicles, I'll memorize one of the plate numbers. Then I'll immediately ride out of the area, park, and phone the information in to you. Good enough, dad?"

"I hope so. I don't want any cowboy stuff, kid, so don't dive into this like we're going to make any arrests today. We know nothing about these people, have no idea how they might react if they feel threatened. Remember, there are only two of us, and we are in the desert."

Tobias noticed the kid's chest was heaving slightly; a vein protruding from his left temple betrayed an elevated heart rate. He looked a lot like a race horse, foaming at the mouth, pressing against the gate, restless to run.

"Be patient, kid. Let's just stick with the plan. If you find something, we'll play our game on the highway, wait for one of their vehicles to show, and hope it leads us to the main operation."

"In the meantime, wanna find out how fast that geriatric cruiser can go?"

"Don't even think about it. I need you in one piece, not broken up and scattered all up and down the Mojave."

Sanchez shrugged, then looked down and considered his plate of food, which was still about half full. He lifted his fork for one more bite, then thought better of it and abandoned the meal. He took a sip of his coffee, then brought up a concern Tobias was waiting for.

"Using the bike for surveillance is a good idea, but it's still got to be a little suspicious out there in the middle of nowhere. If I run up and down those roads too many times, people are going to wonder what I'm doing. And if it's the wrong people wondering ..."

"Already thought of that. More times than not, the simplest solution to a problem is always the best one. I'll give you the map as soon as we're done here. If you think you've been spotted, take it out. Play if off like you're lost. They see that, they won't think anything of it. You're just a guy out in the desert trying to figure out where the hell he's going. On the outside chance that doesn't convince them, don't give them an opportunity to get close. Just get out. I really doubt it will come to that, though. Baker is small, and you won't be there for long. At least not long enough for anyone to really notice you."

Tobias drained the rest of his coffee, figured Sanchez understood the need for a low-key approach. At least he hoped the he did: the last thing he wanted was to get involved in a war of wills against an opponent he knew nothing about.

"Ready?" He asked.

"Born that way."

"Then let's go."

Clark tried to relax as he drove into the desert, but he found it difficult. The surplus downtime caused his mind to stray from what he should've been focusing on. Instead of thinking about strategy and contingencies, he was inundated with thoughts of his daughter, his wife, and Carri. Individually, and even together, they were certainly topics worthy of contemplation. But not when he needed to focus on his destination, not when he needed to have his emotions completely in check, and definitely not when he needed to be mentally preparing for the most important battle of his life.

As the miles clicked by, he found it easier to concentrate. He eased his mind into a more contemplative gear by first making a mental checklist of the things he and Tiny had brought with them. He interrogated the list until he got bored with it, concluded they were adequately equipped to make it a few days in the desert without having to resupply. As he neared Baker, he was in the middle of running through various surveillance and assault strategies, applying them to a two-man crew, armed to the teeth, while confronted with a harsh, dry climate and an unknown enemy. In his opinion, the best offensive strategy for a two- man crew would be to break it up into two one-man units. Going in together as a paired force didn't make any sense to him, and he hoped Tiny would agree with him on that issue. It halved their odds for success. If the place had security and a sniper who was any good, they could both be dropped in the blink of an eye. Better to split up, then take the place from two different directions. If that were even possible. He wouldn't know until he got there.

He drove past the Baker exit, figured he'd give it fifteen miles before turning around. Based upon what Eduardo had told him, that would be the most probable distance for the turn-off into the desert, where he'd expect dirt roads and hilly terrain. Eight miles later, he found what he was looking for: an exit onto a surface street called Halloran Springs Road. And the probabilities were high, too: hills to his left, a road leading right into them, changing from asphalt to gravel as it moved away from the highway and injected itself into the harsh desert landscape.

He turned on his right blinker, then stepped on his brake pedal lightly several times to get Tiny's attention. He pulled off the 15, took a right on Halloran, then followed it a few hundred yards to a dilapidated assortment of four buildings scattered about on a gravel lot. He parked at the end of a line of ancient metal heaps, rusted-out clunkers that hadn't been modern for four or five decades.  Tiny filed in right after him, parked the Mustang just far enough away from the truck's passenger side to allow the doors of both vehicles to open without touching. He transferred his equipment to the truck, locked the Mustang, then hopped into the passenger seat of the big F-350. Less than five minutes later, they were on an anonymous dirt road, winding their way into the hills east of the 15.

 

Clark drove slowly to ensure he didn't miss any barely-visible crossroads. The terrain was acceptable, presented an occasional challenge, but nothing that required four-wheel drive. As he the road turned around a small, scrubby hill, he decided they were close enough to warrant an elevated level of caution.

"Based on what I was told, we're getting close. I doubt anybody's going to come running toward us with barrels blazing, but just in case, why don't you get out the Mossbergs, load them up, and retrieve a few extra shells for the both of us."

Tiny drawled, "Yas sah! Massah Clark!" He leaned over, inserted his bulk between the captain's chairs. Clark saw he had to twist himself sideways to do so, over-grown lat muscles wedging against both seats, a massive arm appearing and then disappearing as he retrieved the shotguns and the bag of shells one by one. He'd always considered himself a big man, but compared to Tiny, he figured he must have looked like a twig.

As Tiny loaded the shotguns, Clark said, "Didn't intend for that to sound like a command. Just getting a little edgy given the proximity to our target, that's all."

Tiny looked over, a large toothy grin spread across his face. "Prah-sim-uh-tee? Woo-wee! Yo shore does use big words, boss!"

"Stop."

"Just havin' a little fun, bro. Might as well, you know what I'm sayin'? Cause we find this place, it gonna get a whole lot less fun real quick."

Clark didn't answer, just stared out the driver's-side window into the desert. Tiny followed his gaze, then let out a low chuckle.

"And it looks like it's gonna get a lot less fun sooner than I 'spected."

 

Clark was looking down an access road that zigzagged its way to a barely-visible collection of buildings set up against the base of a hill. The road was bordered on both sides by mature cottonwoods. The hill was crowned with six windmills, turbines spinning slowly in the barely-moving morning air, moonlight bouncing off enormous metal blades. He found himself breathing more deeply, a process that had become automatic to him over the past couple of years, an exercise that allowed him to maintain his cool and mentally prepare for an engagement.

He slowed his truck to a crawl, said, "That's it." An image of his daughter suddenly flitted through his mind without warning, his heart rate jumped, and a brief surge of rage coursed through his system, turning his face an angry shade of red. He gripped the steering wheel tightly with both hands, his knuckles turning white under the pressure.

Tiny placed a meaty paw on Clark's shoulder. "You okay, bro?"

"Yeah, I'm good."

"What now? Can't stop here. Too obvious."

"We find a place to park up ahead, stash the truck. Then I'm going to backtrack. I want to get a better look at the compound from the front, see who's moving about and watching the place. Then I'm going to evaluate their defenses and identify any weaknesses." Clark felt the color drain from his face. He took a few deep, silent breaths, did his best to banish the unproductive thoughts from his mind.

Tiny removed his hand. "What you want me to do?"

"You tell me. How do you think you can help best?"

"Yo, man, I'm from the hood, dog." Tiny laughed nervously before continuing. "Gimme a crack house or even a bad residence infested with some even badda niggas, an' I'll move in like a cat. But this? We out in the desert 'n shit." He paused. "I guess I'd wanna move around the rear, use the scrub and the terrain for cover, then look for safe zones and potential assault paths."

Clark said nothing, just gave the man a surprised look. Tiny was holding back.

"You ain't the only one who been in the military. Semper Fi. Grunt, but I paid attention to my training. How the hell you think I beat all them niggas back home, bro?"

Clark nodded his head, felt more than a little relief he wasn't going to have to a explain a split-assault strategy to the big man.

"Take a Mossberg and a Steyr. Right now, let's focus on observation only, get the lay, identify security and weaknesses." Clark reached into the center console, removed the two-way radios he'd purchased earlier in the morning, handed one to Tiny. "Channel Five. Let's keep in contact about every half hour. Two clicks, that's it. You find yourself under duress, get the hell out and meet at the truck. Three clicks for duress, okay?" Tiny nodded his head. Clark reiterated, "No action yet. Just surveillance. Let's try to give it eight hours. After that, if everything goes well we'll regroup at the truck, drive further out into the desert, get some rest, and come back about three or four in the morning and take the place."

They drove in silence for another two miles. The road slowly degraded from fairly maintained to a rutty, sandy mess that required traction on all four tires. Clark drove a little further, to a point where he figured not too many vehicles would venture, then followed it a half mile more to the far side of a squat hill. He parked his truck just off the road, effectively concealing it from any vehicles that might approach from behind. They stepped out of the truck, let the silence continue as they geared up. Canteens freshly filled with water, fanny packs loaded with spare shells and snacks, they headed in separate directions:  Tiny into the desert behind the compound, Clark back down the dirt road toward its front.

Tobias gave Sanchez a quarter mile head start, then followed him north on the 15 through Barstow and then west toward Baker. During the drive, he decided to focus on aspects of the mission that could go wrong. He concluded if they just played it safe, they could accomplish everything they set out to do: identify the facility in question, establish its relationship to Desert Tours, then return to Pasadena to use that information as foundation for a warrant. In principle, it sounded like a good plan. In reality, it was susceptible to annihilation by two wild cards: an unintentional mistake made by an overconfident partner, and an untimely appearance by the man they were after, one Jack Clark. He'd done everything he could to prevent the former; there was nothing at all he could do about the latter.

As his cruiser sliced through the cool, early morning air, he allowed his mind to move from work-related hypotheticals to a more relaxing introspection of the desert. He took in its expanse as he drove, found it amazing it could sustain any life at all given the lack of moisture and the oppressive heat that baked it into oblivion nine months out of the year. He kept his mind busy by trying to identify the various desert plants as he sped by them. That turned out to be a mistake. He caught a glimpse of a Spiny Saltbush, and he was suddenly thrown back into the nightmare he'd had the previous evening. He could see Samantha lying in the desert sun under the frail piece of scrub, her hair flowing away from her body with the wind, ash gray skin marked up here and there by rips and tears, the systematic work of a menagerie of vermin and decomposition. He could smell the putrefaction, hear the steady, overwhelming buzz from the dense cloud of flies feasting on her body. His heart sped up, his skin flushed, and he began to sweat. He wanted to pull over. He gripped the wheel of his cruiser with both hands, took a deep breath and exhaled slowly. Then he took another. He tried to convince himself it was just a dream, nothing more than a random collection of thoughts, built off a foundation of concerns he'd had over the past several days. But he knew better. The dream served a purpose. It raised a question. A very personal one.

What would he do?

How would he react if he found the person responsible for raping and killing his only granddaughter? He tried not to think about it, but he couldn't help himself. He found his mind wandering inexorably toward an honest contemplation of the hypothetical, vectoring in on self-truths he knew were better off left in the more primitive parts of his brain. He was bracing himself for an ugly revelation, a bit of primal self-knowledge, when something caught his attention out of the corner of his eye. A van. Merging onto the freeway in front of him. It was a large, sandstone-colored vehicle, and painted onto the left panel in bold print were the words Desert Tours. He'd been so caught up in introspection he'd almost driven right by Baker.

He scanned both sides of the road for Sanchez but couldn't find him, figured he must have already turned off the highway and was heading into town. He slowed his cruiser just a little, considered turning around, then quickly dismissed the idea. He sped up until he matched the speed of the van, eased his way into a position about a half mile behind it. Using the traffic spread out between them to obscure his presence, he followed it for about eight miles until it exited on Halloran Springs Road. He didn't pull off the road with it. He simply drove on, watching his rear-view mirror as he passed to determine which way it would turn.
