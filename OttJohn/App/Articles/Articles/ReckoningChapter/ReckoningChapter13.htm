Chapter 13

 

 

Tobias surveyed his surroundings as Sanchez parked the cruiser in front of 1452 Warren, the address of a small, older stucco home. While the house itself was representative of many lower-income units populating neighborhoods just south of Santa Ana, he'd never seen one quite so flamboyant: the roof of the house was flat and rocked, its perimeter trimmed by a dark blue fascia, which itself was decorated along its length with painted religious iconography. The exterior walls were painted bright yellow, the high-decibel color mercifully interrupted at regular intervals with small, square windows set within the stucco. The yard was a neatly-kept rock garden made from red and white gravel, arranged carefully to form small crosses on the ground. Various large rocks in the shape of fish were scattered about thoughtfully to give the yard a vertical quality to an otherwise flat presentation. A thin concrete path meandered from the sidewalk to the front door, its entire length impressed with scripture. Tobias looked at Sanchez, noticed his jaw had dropped just a little from the sheer incomprehension of the spectacle. Neither of them made any kind of comment on what they saw, its sum effect stifling any kind of lucid conversation.

"You want to get me up to speed before we go up there brandishing our shields like a couple of big shots?" asked Tobias as he tucked his PDA into his right front pocket.

Sanchez pulled out his notepad, flipped it open, and turned a few pages to scan for the correct content.

"The neighborhood is mostly Hispanic. The woman and her daughter, however, are Russian immigrants; the mother's name is Jana and the daughter's is Natashia. The husband is deceased. He was Hispanic, served in Desert Storm, KIA while on his second tour. Natashia was only five when he died. Three years later, she had the misfortune of meeting Hassenback. Long story short, she was a brave little girl, reported the assault, and he was convicted of two counts of first degree sexual contact with a child under the age of 11. At the time of the molestation, Jana had a boyfriend. His name's Bernito Javier. With respect to a possible match with our guy, he doesn't even come close: about five-ten and hundred and sixty pounds, according to the report."

Tobias nodded, then thought briefly about the conversation they'd had the day before. Sanchez was a young, and sometimes young guys find it difficult to separate personal issues from those that are job-related. The commonality of having lost a loved one to the war in the Middle East had the potential to bias him enough to keep him from conducting an objective interview, and Tobias couldn't allow that. But the kid looked sharp and appeared to be all business. All the same, he figured it wouldn't hurt to quiz him a little. Just in case.

"Any other kids in the house?" Tobias asked.

"Unknown. At the time of the attack, though, Natashia was eight and an only child."

"Friends, relatives?"

"The incident report conveys the family was pretty much an island. No indication of friends besides neighborhood acquaintances, and absolutely no family on Jana's side present. As for Bernito, he was a transplant from the Midwest, fairly new to the area, and, from what the report says, kept pretty much to himself."

"Criminal history?"

"Nothing more than traffic violations for either one of them, boss. They're clean as they come. When the initial complaint was filed, Bernito was working in a Mexican Restaurant in Pasadena, and Jana split time between school and a job at a coffee house."

Tobias reached for the door handle, paused, then turned to his partner. "Okay. You conduct the interview on this one. I'll just observe and take some notes."

"Sounds good to me, boss."

"So, tell me, what are we after?"

"We're looking for evidence of a very large, over-protective significant other with homicidal tendencies and a deep-seeded dislike for older, tech-savvy detectives." He answered, smiling.

Tobias gave Sanchez an uncustomary scowl. "I'm glad to see you're focusing on what's important; now can you be serious for a second?"

Sanchez made a little frown, obviously disappointed Tobias had not laughed at his joke. "Sorry, boss. You're right. Probably should be a little more focused right now." He paused briefly. "We're looking for deception."

"Right." Tobias turned and looked again at the rock yard. He'd have to apologize to Carol when he returned home. What he'd said about her garden had come purely from frustration over the phony investment ad he'd found on Hassenback's thumb drive. He sometimes let things get to him, and if he didn't watch it, they'd impact his personal relationships. And now he'd unleashed some of that damned tetchy disposition on Sanchez. Some days are just better off spent in bed. He released the handle and pressed back into the passenger seat.

"Sorry for snapping, kid. Okay, fill me in on deception."

Sanchez relaxed but carried a more workman-like expression. "We're looking for deception on the part of the principal occupants of the residence within the context of questions we ask about Hassenback. Our key indication for detecting deception will come from body language. We'll look for mechanical, non-fluid body motions, hand movements to the face and ears, eye casts to the upper right, avoidance of eye contact, sweating, defensiveness, and posture."

"Good," replied Tobias. "And how will you check your conclusion of deception against one of simple stress?"

"Change the topic of conversation a couple of times and gauge the response. If the person being questioned is forthcoming, there should be no discernable change in body language, no variation from our metric."

Tobias smiled to himself. The kid was sharp. When he wanted to be. And he was just about ready for the next step. He hadn't told Sanchez, but he was planning to write him a letter of recommendation for an open position for Detective I. To him, there was no doubt the kid was ready. Maybe he'd even write it sooner rather than later.

 

Tobias and Sanchez exited the unmarked and walked the thin concrete path that lead from the sidewalk to the front door of the house.  As Sanchez removed his wallet and readied his badge for display, Tobias gave the door three solid raps, the sound of which barely overcame the roaring din of an adjacent window-mounted air conditioner. After a brief wait, a slender woman answered the door. She had on a short, white skirt and an old, purple tank top that appeared ready to plunge off her shoulders at any moment. Her hair was blonde, her eyes were blue, and  her flip-flops were yellow. Tobias figured her to be somewhere on the wrong side of forty.

"Yes?" The accent was heavy with a nasal inflection, the vowel elongated.

"Miss Jana Milescovitz?" Asked Sanchez.

"Yes. What is this about?"

Sanchez presented his badge. "Afternoon, ma'am. I'm Officer Sanchez and this is Detective Tobias. We'd like to ask you some questions if you don't mind."

Jana briefly glanced at Tobias and then turned directly toward Sanchez. Her brows furrowed and her face flushed.

"This is about him, yes? What he do? Get out? Savage little girl again?" Her voice moved from a quiet tone of disbelief to an agonizing rage. The raw, emotional response mixed with the language perturbations of a non-native speaker made her reaction almost unintelligible.

"No ma'am." Sanchez played with the edge of his notebook, appeared to struggle with distancing himself from the woman's emotional distress. "We're very sorry for the disruption, Miss Milescovitz. I understand that bringing back those memories are difficult for you. But Hassenback has dropped off the radar, and we're just going through ..."

Jana looked at Sanchez quizzically. "Please. What you mean radar?"

"Oh, sorry ma'am. It's just a figure of speech. I should have used a different word. What I meant to say is we haven't been able to locate Hassenback, so we're here to ensure he's had no contact with former victims. Just a precaution, ma'am. We hope you understand."

"Da … yes. Sorry." As looked at Sanchez, her large, blue eyes began to well with tears. One escaped, streaked down her rounded Slavic face, leaving a single, blue streak as it did. "He is disappear? He is loose?"

Sanchez ignored the question, scribbled a note in his binder, then continued. "The chance of a repeat offense on a former victim with respect to a predator like Mr. Hassenback is very small, ma'am. We really don't think you have anything to worry about. However, we feel it's necessary to do the leg work just to make sure. Have you received anything from Mr. Hassenback or perhaps even seen him around, Ma'am?"

"No! You not call animal mister! He is ... how you say … dog shit!" Her lower lip quivered. Tobias handed her a handkerchief so she could wipe her eyes.

She turned to Tobias, said "Spasiba," then turned back to Sanchez. "He is sending nothing. He was not come by. I would know. I watch street and houses. I have many camera. If I see crime, I log in computer. I have site. Keep record."

"So you haven't seen or heard from Hassenback at all?" Asked  Sanchez, couching the question more as a statement.

"No." She replied, beginning to relax.

Sanchez made another note, then asked, "At the time of the incident, Miss Milescovitz, you had a boyfriend. Is he still around?"

"He move to Chicago. He left two years. Move back to family there."

"So you're alone now, ma'am?"

"Yes. Is better. I focus on Natashia. She is still have problem from attack sometimes. It is all I do to work and take care of daughter."

"Sorry to hear that, ma'am. Where do you work?"

"I work in city library. I help with computers and network."

"Ouch! I have no idea what to do with those things. It'd probably take me a week just to learn how to turn one on."

"Is easy. You come to library. I show you."

"Maybe I'll take you up on that. Thank you."

"Puzhalsta."

"Does that mean you're welcome?"

"Yes. Spasiba is for please. Puzhalsta is more like no problem."

Tobias watched Sanchez look down at his notepad then take a deep breath. He was preparing himself for a reaction to his next question, and Tobias figured he'd get a fairly hostile response. But it was human nature and something the kid would have to expect under the circumstances.

"Is your daughter in, ma'am?" Sanchez asked.

Jana's eyes opened wide, the angry flush returning to her face. "No," she said in a shaky whisper. "No. You cannot talk. She has enough trouble. I will not allow!" She trailed off, eyes again filling with tears, voice cracking.

"That's not our intention, ma'am. We don't want to ask her any questions, but we'd like to see she's okay. Can you do that for us?" Sanchez's voice lost its detachment briefly, softness forming around his words, which seemed to re-assure Jana.
"Sorry. Yes. I can do." Jana turned, called back into the house for her daughter. After a moment, a beautiful girl of about fifteen appeared, sporting long brown hair, large eyes like her mother's, but with a face that wasn't entirely Slavic; rather, her features appeared to have incorporated the best her mother and her father's heritage had to offer. She was striking. She stopped tentatively by her mother's side, looked at the two strangers at the door, and then looked up at her mother.

"Who are these people, mother?" She sounded like any other California kid, her language free of the foreign constraints that made her mother's conversation a little hard to understand.

"These men knew your papa," she said. "I wanted you should meet them." She tugged at her earlobe and made a tentative gesture toward Tobias. "This man was captain of papa's squad."

Tobias smiled, then added to the harmless fiction. "He was a good man, sweetheart. I was proud to have served with him. You have a lot to be proud of, too, having had a father like him."

"Thank you," She said it brightly and honestly but with the lack of emotion that made it clear she never had the opportunity to know her father. She let her gaze linger on Tobias for a brief moment, then shifted her attention again to her mother. "I'm working on a project with Suzy, mom. Can she come over tonight?"

"Yes." Jana said. She smiled as she watched her daughter disappear back into the house. Then she turned back to Sanchez.

"This is satisfactory?"

"Yes, Ma'am."

Tobias grabbed Sanchez by the shoulder, then interjected himself into the conversation."Ma'am," he said, softly.

"Yes. Are we done?"

"Yes, ma'am. Just one more thing about Hassenback."

"What? Tell me please."

"You don't have to worry about him anymore. We've been checking former victims to build a suspect list in his murder. Sorry for the trouble and the false pretence, but under the circumstances, it couldn't be helped." 

There was shock, then a little anger, then a look on her face that could only be bitter satisfaction.

"Good!" She spat out. "Bastard should be dead after what he do to my Natashia. This is best news!"

"Yes ma'am!" Sanchez smiled, making no attempt to hide his agreement. "We won't take any more of your time. Thanks again for your cooperation."

As they turned to go, Tobias stopped and turned back.

"You mind if I ask you one more question, ma'am?"

"Not at all."

"You said you posted info about neighborhood observations you make?"

"Yes. I post. I have website." She moved her right hand in front of her, gesturing  to the street from left to right. "This is not good place. We have prostitute, drug dealer, suspicious person. I see, I post. I get picture, I post."

"What do you do with the information after it's posted?"

"There is police substation nearby. I give them address. They look at post. They tell me I do good job. I might even get position to set up same in other places." She added the last with a prideful smile.

Tobias returned the smile, wondered briefly if it was just a lack of opportunity that kept her from moving to a better neighborhood. She certainly seemed to have skills. "That's a wonderful thing you're doing, ma'am. And congratulations on the possible job opportunity. If you don't mind, I'd like to take a look at what you've created. Could you give me the address to your site?"

"Yes. I have no domain name yet. Just IP address. It is 209, 45, 137, 168."

Tobias took out his PDA and began punching in the address. As he entered the last of the four numbers, the digits shattered into his mind like a brick exploding into a plate glass window. He quickly returned his PDA to his pocket, then looked at the woman in front of him, a big smile stretched across his face.

"Thank you, Miss Milescovitz. You have been very helpful!" He then turned to Sanchez and said, "Let's go."

Clark sat alone in his weekly, a hazy cone of yellow light enveloping him like a bright, airy blanket. Instead of staving off the cold, though, it held against a darkness that surrounded him, that encroached on his space, threatening to blacken everything around him and within him. He was sitting at the table, chewing on a protein bar, drinking coffee, and reviewing a checklist he had long ago created to help him prepare for engaging targets.

Normally, he would wait a few weeks to make personal contact, using the intervening time to observe his subjects, understand their habits, their schedules, and their social interactions. It was their social-physical characteristics that were especially important to him, as their pairing implicated his strategy for approaching them. For those who were primarily introverts, he'd always elected for a home visit. Small or large, those falling under that category had never been a problem and had always given him the information he'd asked of them. He'd elected to employ the same strategy for small to medium-sized extroverts as well. They were typically much less cooperative, but he found that well-placed threats and a little bodily harm got them to talk every time. Those who were large and extroverted, obnoxious really, were better suited for abduction.

He'd worked with only one, in Atlanta. And he was a handful. He was big, probably just short of 300 pounds, and he was mouthy. But the outward disposition of a man, the way he interacts with others, doesn't ultimately get in the way of acquiring information from him. It all boils down to will. Most men aren't going to engage in a fight over information, especially when it's apparent the altercation is going to find one of the participants in the hospital. Or worse. Rather, when facing bodily harm, most reasonable men are going to simply do what is asked of them; and in Clark's experience, asking for information was a request most men found reasonable enough. It was mostly a matter of picking a place and a time that maximized the odds of accomplishing his goal. His chosen place; his chosen time. And that had always been enough to demoralize his targets to a point they'd give him the information he'd asked of them. He was simply a hunter. They were his prey. And any well-prepared hunter has a supreme advantage over prey that has no idea it is being stalked, notwithstanding its size or intelligence.

Most of Clark's checklist was simply categories of information he gathered while watching his targets over the course of weeks: physical description, particulars of transportation, place of employment, shopping habits, forms of entertainment, sleep patterns, social bearing and relationships, and living arrangements. Unfortunately, in Eduardo's case, there were many points he wouldn't be able to consider with the thoroughness he was accustomed to. In fact, all of his planning for this engagement would have to come from just a single observation. Clark flipped through his notebook to catalog what he'd seen at the coffee house, hoping the little bit of Eduardo he had observed would give him the edge he needed.

At 6:30 in the evening, he'd made his way to the Gypsy Den. As he suspected, the coffee shop was basically empty when he arrived twenty minutes later. That had made Eduardo easy to identify. At around 6:55 in the evening, he spotted a 1974 Chevy Vega rumble down the street, spewing out a trilogy of sound as it did: low, throaty roar of exhaust; bass vibration from an expensive stereo system rattling tinted windows, the opacity of which was legally questionable; and the intervallic rumble of a small-block V8 engine. Clark noted the car was lowered, decked out in low-profile wheels, and painted a repugnant metallic orange. About a half a minute after it parked, a small, Hispanic man of about 35 stepped out. Clark estimated he could be no more than five foot six and 150 pounds. He sported a shaggy ponytail stretched tightly back, which caused his thin visage to appear even narrower than it actually was. He had closely-set eyes, a large nose, and a face that bore the wounds of a teenage war with acne.

At around 7 PM, Eduardo entered the coffee shop; Clark watched him walk to the counter, order a beverage, and take a seat at the front of the store next to a large plate glass window near the door. During the first ten minutes, Eduardo split his time between taking sips of his beverage and batting the container between his hands.  During the next five minutes, he spent his time pacing the length of the coffee shop with a nervous energy, the same energy exhibited by most people who find themselves struggling with impatience while longing to be somewhere else. After about 15 minutes of waiting, Eduardo took a final sip of his beverage, meandered to the counter, and engaged the clerk in an animated conversation that lasted about four  minutes. Clark was fairly certain most of the conversation was filled with a short description of Hassenback and whether the clerk had seen a man fitting that description in or around the store. Of course, the clerk had not, so Eduardo exited the coffee house, and spent his the next eight minutes waiting outside smoking a cigarette.

Once Eduardo finished his smoke, he took one last look in the coffee shop, then looked up and down the street, lingering his gaze on corners and entryways to other shops. Seemingly satisfied Hasseback wasn't going to show, he returned to his vehicle, started it up, and drove away. As he vibrated down the street, Clark started his pickup, waited for two cars to fill the void between them, and followed. Fifteen minutes later, he found himself on Parton Street, driving into a collection of low-end apartment buildings. Clark watched as Eduardo parked his car, then drove past and eased his truck into a space four stalls away. He then adjusted his rear-view mirror, took out his pay-as-you-go cell phone, and pantomimed a phone conversation as he watched Eduardo walk from his car to a second story unit right in front of the complex. Clark held his position for another hour to ensure Eduardo had indeed returned home for the evening.

As he sat in front of this computer, Clark thought about what he'd seen. Eduardo was definitely small, but he was no shrinking violet. He had a loud car, and he had no trouble at all engaging the clerk at the coffee shop in a conversation he had initiated. Certainly, then, Eduardo should be classified as an extrovert. He played some scenarios out in his mind and decided a home visit would be the best strategy. Given Eduardo's spare stature, any kind of struggle would be a non-issue. Further, his extroverted nature could be easily subdued with an early morning visit, when he was asleep and most vulnerable.

Clark leaned back in his chair and thought about his objective. Initially, it had been to simply gather enough information from Eduardo to determine the location of the facility his daughter had been taken to. After some thought, however, he decided he should probably leave nothing to chance, conduct the interview in exactly the same way he had all of the others, and gather every bit of useful information he could. It seemed to be the most prudent route to take. After all, what if for some reason he was not able get the location of the facility from him? What if the facility had drivers that were sent to pick up its patrons, conveying them in the darkened back of a windowless van? Better to conduct a solid interview to keep his investigative continuity strong. If Eduardo came through and gave him the location, great; if he could not, then Clark would at least have some information to work with to build more leads.

Satisfied he had all the information he was realistically going to collect about Eduardo, Clark closed his laptop, went to his bed, and opened the bags of supplies he had bought on his return trip to the weekly. He'd patronized four different businesses to purchase what he required for his interview: a hardware store to pick up duct-tape, cotton gloves, a three-pack of painting masks, two gallons of white flat paint, and a rolling kit; a medical supply outfit to pick up disposable paper outerwear and booties; a used clothing store to pick up a pair of slacks, a white shirt, a blue tie, a worn brown jacket, and pair of leather boots; and a toy store to pick up a metal police badge, a realistic-looking pair of plastic handcuffs, a cowboy hat that might fit a nine year old boy, a toy gun, and a supply of caps.

Of course, the paint, rollers, hat, gun, and caps were not anything Clark planned to employ in his interrogation. Rather, they were simply diversions. Going into a hardware store and buying duct tape, gloves, and a mask might look a little suspicious; buying paint and a roller kit as well made the purchase look altogether ordinary. The same could be said about the badge and handcuffs. Without the gun and caps and hat, the purchase might look somewhat incongruous, adding to the purchase lowered the risk for suspicion. Certainly, the additional purchases were a waste of money, and throwing the supplies into an anonymous dumpster on his way home only added to the squander, but a little lost cash was much more preferable than grabbing the attention of a paranoid store clerk.

Clark moved the bags of supplies from the bed to the floor, turned off the lonely incandescent bulb that hung above the table, and crawled into bed, his only remaining preparation to ensure his body was reasonably well-rested. In two and a half hours, he would drive to Eduardo's and present himself as a detective. It would be three in the morning, and more than likely, Eduardo wouldn't have any idea what hit him.

Windows rolled down, Dean Gerkowitcz breathed in the morning desert air as he drove the last leg of his journey toward his property. It was located just off the Mojave Highway, at about the midway point between Vegas and L.A., deep in the desert and literally in the middle of nowhere. The closest establishment was an old mining facility on Halloran Summit Road, which was located about 170 miles from the front door of his home in L.A. The drive itself should have taken about three hours, but he liked to cover the distance a little more slowly. In his fast-paced, work-a-day world, an easy drive into the unforgiving, desolate landscape of the southwest was just what the doctor ordered. The desert air seemed to clear his mind, purge the debris that collected there as a result of his investment work in the city; the drive itself allowed him to switch mental gears and concentrate on the goals he'd laid out for the next phase of his new venture.

He exited Highway 15 and turned left on Halloran Summit road, which he followed for a good quarter mile until it terminated at a county dirt road. The road was composed of an unforgiving mixture of moderate grades, small rocks, and a lot of sand, so he flipped his SUV into four-wheel drive and followed it as it meandered through a forest of cactus, Yucca, and Joshua trees. As he drove the last half mile to his turn, he marveled once more at the immensity of the property he owned. It was over 1500 acres, stretching from the center of the dirt road on which he was now driving to just over the ridge of the hill to the southwest.

He took a left onto his access road, then breathed a sigh of relief as the suspension on his BMW stopped squeaking. Compared to the last three and a half miles of dirt he'd just driven, his driveway felt more like the autobahn. Topped with a solid surface of crushed gravel, it stretched about a quarter mile to a circular parking area that fronted his buildings, all of which were situated at the base of the hill behind his property. The length of the road itself was not an accurate indication of the true distance from the buildings on the land, though. In fact, it had two very exaggerated switchbacks that served to disorient uninvited guests who happened to chance their way onto the property, something a straight driveway of a few hundred feet simply couldn't do. Either side of the driveway was lined with cottonwood trees, a staple of desert farmland. They were thick, purposefully unkempt, with lots of low-lying branches, and planted close enough together to form an almost uninterrupted wall of vegetation along the entire length of the road. The overall effect of the wall was to efficiently obscure the buildings behind them while offering a striking contrast to the native foliage surrounding the property.

Gerkowitcz pulled into the looping driveway and parked, the crackling of the  gravel under his tires giving way to a peaceful silence that might sound strange and perhaps even a little unnerving to someone who'd spent his whole life in the city. He got out of his car and looked around. The property came with three buildings: a main house, a large, red wooden barn, and a guest house. The main house was roughly 5500 square feet and decked out in a rustic opulence that would rival many of the newer homes parked the hills above Hollywood. The barn housed a couple of working horses that were used  by a small crew of part-time ranch hands he employed to tend to the chores in and around the property, which included keeping the 15 head of cattle he owned fed, watered, and out of trouble. The barn came complete with a hay loft and two water troughs, both of which were fed with their own direct line to an underground aquifer. As for the guest house, Gerkowitcz took that for himself when he visited, as the main building was used for another purpose directly related to his business. The guest house was a newer, wooden structure probably built in the late 90's. It was about 2400 square feet, which was small by his standards. But with three bedrooms, two baths, walk-in closets, a living room with a large screen TV, and a gourmet kitchen, it suited his needs adequately.

He'd first looked into purchasing the property five years back. When he'd been caught up in all the paranoia about the end of civilization due to political instability, the financial crisis, the healthcare crisis, the technological dehydration of the American workforce, the wars in the Middle East, and the health of the planet in general. From where he stood, the whole world was going to Hell in a hand basket, America in particular. At the time, he'd read voraciously about the shifting of global power, the dumbing down of American children, and the impact that was going to have in the near future: innovation had been responsible for keeping America ahead of the rest of the world for the last two centuries. After so many years of leading in every conceivable category, from science to agriculture to military might, he watched as Americans sat on their collective dead asses and allowed the rest of the world to catch up. He figured in another fifty years there wouldn't even be an America anymore, not as he knew it anyway.

He was especially worried American affluence was coming to a quick end, the death throes of which would present a sickening land and asset grab by minorities, mostly Mexicans, Chinamen, Japs, and towel heads, none of whom did anything to help make this country great. Just a bunch of fucking vultures hastening the death of a great nation so they could feed on its rotting carcass. And he didn't want to be anywhere near a metro area when the hammer fell. He'd convinced himself he needed a shelter from the shit storm that was coming. A place he could take his family and wait out the inevitable invasion of America's infrastructure by the very parasites it had worked so hard to sustain during the past century.

What he needed was a fortress, a place so far out of the way no one would ever want to take the time to bother him. When he first found the property, it was listed as a severely-reduced, somewhat distressed ranch off from highway 15. He'd been looking for a place for about a year then. Having found nothing worth a damn, he decided to take a drive out to the desert to check it out. When he arrived, he was skeptical. The guest house was in great shape, but the main building needed a lot of work: the wiring needed to be upgraded from what was considered state-of-the-art in the 1930's, the interior walls were old and damaged, the roof needed to be replaced, the flooring was rotting, and the windows were cracked, some broken. And that was the good news. The real downside, in his opinion, was that the place was in the middle of nowhere, the roads that lead to it were an unmaintained disaster, and most of the land itself looked so dry he may as well have been on the fucking moon. The listing, too, was right at seven million. He figured the place needed at least a half a million in building rehabilitation and another quarter million in upgrades, and that was just about enough to make him want to drive away and not look back.

As he was about to decline any further consideration toward purchasing the property, the realtor, a fairly nice-looking buxom blonde who broadcasted a slightly slutty cowgirl look, had insisted in a rather touchy-feely way he first listen to what came with it. As she itemized the features for him, three things stood out: one, the top of the hill behind the property was populated with six windmills, which powered the entire facility in addition to making about a thousand a month from the sale of surplus energy to the local power company; two, the land came with water and mineral rights. The wells on the property were sound and certified to last at least another hundred years; and three, the northwest part of the land contained a spring-fed pond the previous owner had stocked with trout. Not that bottom-feeding carp bull crap you couldn't feed to a hungry pig, but the kind of fish fit for human consumption. The bottom line of the whole delivery was that the entire place was off the grid and self-sustainable for many years if managed properly.

When she'd finished, his interest had been rekindled, not enough to run out and buy the place right away, but he could definitely feel his evangelical survivalist spirit soaring, just about high enough to touch the goddamned face of Jesus H. Christ himself. He'd swallowed the information from the realtor like a dehydrated, half-dead man crawling through a hot desert would take to a canteen full of ice-cold water. The business-savvy side of him, though, still had considerable trouble with the seven million dollar price tag. That and the extra million in upgrades put the total cost just outside of his budget. He'd been interested enough, though,  to return to the property five times, each visit resulting in the two sides of his personality waging war: the survivalist in him declaring the roads, in their terrible state of disrepair, added a measure of defensibility; the business-savvy side of him arguing the property was over-valued and that only a fool would buy it.

In the end, during his fifth visit, he'd asked the realtor to leave him with the property alone overnight. He figured he shouldn't have her around. Instead of concentrating on the property, which was what he should've been doing all along, he'd been enjoying the guest house and partaking in the carnal pleasures of what he'd come to think of as a sexy and accomplished professional woman, certainly a hell of a lot more sexy and accomplished than the pathetic, good-for-nothing sorry excuse for a woman taking up space and air at his house in L.A. There was another reason he didn't want the realtor lady to stay with him, too: he had it in his mind he wasn't going to put an offer in, and he didn't want to have to say goodbye to her. He just wanted to have one last night of peace and quiet in the desert, drink a little beer, watch a little television, and then leave the next morning, never to return.

Rather than watch television and relax, though, he spent a good chunk of the evening walking around the grounds. He returned to the main building and looked at the valuations of the house over the years. The numbers didn't add up. The previous owner, who'd died several years earlier, obviously had the same survivalist bug: he'd put in the windmills, upgrading them even, and carved out a  pond and stocked it with trout. He wanted the entire property off the grid, and he'd succeeded. For the life of him, though, Gerkowitcz couldn't understand how the property had laddered up to 25 million in 2007. Certainly, all of California was in the midst of an orgy of unmerited real-estate appreciation, but in his best judgment he couldn't see it being valued at any more than 15-17 million at even its most obscene markup.

After a few hours of reviewing the documents, he'd finally had enough. He filed the papers in the main building's large kitchen, grabbed his beer from the dining room table, and started toward the rear door. As he reached for the handle, he remembered he'd overlooked the basement. Curious, he took a swig of his beer, opened the door to the basement, flipped on the light to illuminate his way down its narrow staircase, and descended. When he got to the bottom, what he saw looked more like a hollowed-out rock than a basement, sort of like a man-made cave. Besides the open space and debris scattered about, mostly appliances and tools from an age long gone by, there was nothing. He walked across the floor to the far end of the basement and touched the damp, rock wall. As he moved about, he noticed his footfalls hollowed out somewhat. The effect was minor, and would completely escape detection unless considerable attention was given to the sound. He walked over the area several more times, listening closely, leaning into the floor and testing it for weakness and pliability. After several minutes, he was convinced the floor below the corner of the basement was hollow.

He kneeled on the floor and used his hands to scrape the loose dirt from around the spot. After digging a hole about four inches deep, he reached a square slab of plywood, which he figured to be about five feet on a side. The plywood was nestled snugly into a concrete lip, the seam too tight to allow him to use his fingers to pry it up. Remembering the tools he saw on the far wall, he stood and walked to the other side of the basement. After a minute of searching, he found what he was looking for: an old, standard screwdriver with a long shaft and a head thin enough to fit between the plywood and the concrete. He returned to the hole, knelt in the dirt, and worked to lever the plywood out of the lip. After a few minutes, his effort was rewarded. He held the screwdriver in place, put his hand beneath the exposed edge of wood, lifted the sheet up, and flipped it over to the side. What he found under the plywood took his breath away.

Gerkowitcz shook off his reminisce, stepped out of his Beamer, and took a big gulp of desert air. Everything he'd suspected during that final visit had been right on the money. What he found in the basement of the main building that evening had overwhelmingly changed his mind about purchasing the property. What he'd found, too, had more than made up for the obscene discrepancy in its valuation. Upon walking back up the steps of the basement that night, he knew he had to buy the property. He also knew what he had to use it for.
