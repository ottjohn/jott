Chapter 18

 

 

Clark drove in no particular direction, his only objective being to get as far away from Eduardo's apartment as possible. He had his left hand on the wheel of the Mustang, his right held his open, pay-as-you-go cell. He punched in a seven-digit exchange and pressed send. To his surprise, the phone picked up after the first ring.

"This is Joe."

The number may have been local, but the voice on the other end betrayed a heavy Louisiana accent, sent Clark back the better part of a decade. His mind flooded with memories of a young black man who'd come to him during his freshman year in college looking for advice about which classes he should take to get him up to speed for the computer-related coursework Clark taught at the college. Joe was about 21 at the time, he and his brother fresh from New Orleans, sent to LA by their mother in a desperate attempt to remove them from the gang activity both had fallen into. They had been sent to live with an uncle who resided just outside of Orange County. The uncle was not rich by any means, but he was in a good neighborhood, and he understood the plight of his sister all too well. He'd taken them in, no questions asked. In the end, Joe had made it, picked himself up by his bootstraps and made himself a better life. His brother Jerry, on the other hand, didn't. He quickly fell in with a local branch of the Crips, picked up right where he'd left off in New Orleans. It was Jerry he wanted to talk to.

"Hey, Joe, this is Clark. Remember me?"

"Clark?" Pause. "How are you doing these days?"

He could hear the concern in Joe's voice. He could hear an inflection of hope there, too. Hope delivered by a former student and a friendship borne out of the collegiate bond that sometimes forms between teacher and student. Joe had been to both funerals, had even offered him a room after he sold his house. It was a bad time, though. And he really didn't want to spread his misery around. He needed to deal with the loss in his own way, so he'd declined the offer. Joe had tried to call several times after, but Clark never picked up, never returned the calls. The reason was simple, in his mind, anyway: the second he'd walked out of the cemetery, the second after he'd put his wife in the ground, he'd vowed revenge. He knew, too, the path he chose to take would be a bloody one. He couldn't bring someone like Joe into that, not after the kid had worked so damn hard to get out of trouble. He checked his mental wandering, snapped himself back to the conversation.

"I'm doing okay."

"Still going to the DoJo?"

It was a sideways question, a backdoor interrogative regarding his mental health. Joe had always understood Clark as a guy who needed to engage in heavy physical activity to be level and happy. He knew this because he'd trained Clark for the better part of five years.

"Training at home.. I'm keeping up on it." The reply was unintentionally terse, the conversation awkward and a little mechanical, the way they always get after a couple of years of silence. There's really no grease to make the words flow more steadily, no rehearsal for what he had to ask of his friend. He wanted to find a way to ask the favor without getting the kid caught up in what he was doing, to hide his intention behind a mask that would somehow keep him completely uninvolved. Safe. But there was not solution to this one, so he decided on a semi-direct route:

"How's your brother doing these days?"

Another pause. This time rooted in shock.

"Jerry? He's doing okay, I guess. Why do you ask?"

"Listen, Joe, I need to see him. I really need you to get me in touch with him as soon as you can."

"Whoa. Wait. Why? The guy's nothing but trouble, Clark. Hell, he's so bad I barely talk to him. What do you need from a guy like that?"

"I just need to see him, Joe. It's very important." Clark let the phone drop off his ear for a moment. He knew there'd be another pause. A long one. One of the things he really admired about Joe was his innate ability to assess, calculate, and conclude. His last two statements would have moved right around anyone else, drifted past a careless listener like an apparition wafting through the air. But Joe looks for motivation in everything. Given the circumstances, a bizarre request borne out of the only obvious motivation on Clark's part, Joe made the correct conclusion in less than a minute.

"You can't do that, man. It's crazy. If you know where the sonofabitch is, I'm begging you, get the police involved. Let them take the bastard down. You can't ..."

Clark cut him off. "Can't do that, Joe. Look, I don't want to get you involved any more than getting me in touch with Jerry. I don't really want to get him involved, either. I just need him to point me in a direction. That's all."

"Clark."

"Joe." Slight pause. "This one's not God's. It's not going to be for the justice system, either. I made a promise. To both of them. This one's mine."

Another pause, then a tone of resignation.

"Yeah, I guess it is. Hell, I'd probably do the same thing myself. Call me in an hour. I'll give you a place where we can meet. We can go from there."

"I don't want you involved, Joe."

"It's the only way it's going to happen, bro. My brother doesn't know you, so you're definitely going to need me there. One hour. Okay?"

Clark reluctantly agreed, then slapped his phone shut. During the conversation, he'd somehow managed to drive his way up toward Glendale. The air in the mustang was on, but the car was stifling hot. And he was thirsty. He pulled into a gas station, picked up a liter of water for about the same price he could buy a gallon in a supermarket, paid the attendant, and returned to the car. The seatbacks seared his shoulders as he leaned back into the hot leather. He opened his water and took down almost half the bottle with three deep gulps. He stared out the front of the car, found himself fiddling with the radio receiver. He looked at the phone, then back at the water bottle, then back at the phone again. He started the car and flipped the air onto full blast. He took another gulp of water, then looked at the phone once again. He had one more call to make, and he knew he was stalling. He really didn't want to make the call, but he had to. The scene at Eduardo's apartment complex demanded it. He flipped open his phone and dialed another local exchange.

Tobias and Sanchez had worked quickly to sort through Eduardo's mail, keeping only items that looked like bills or statements. They then allowed themselves a quick search of the apartment for any further evidence of additional bank accounts or credit cards. After twenty minutes of looking, Tobias decided they'd probably find nothing else and called off the search.

"There's nothing else here," he said.

Sanchez gave Tobias a puzzled look. "Doesn't look like it. But we haven't searched the whole place yet, either."

"No time. We need to call this in. We're starting to get into a murky area here: manager's got an arrival time for us, and the difference between that and our call time is getting a little long. Probably already too long, but not enough to cause too many raised eyebrows."

"Okay, want me to notify the manager he's gonna have a crime scene on his hands?"

"Yeah. Probably a good idea. You do that and I'll call this in. Don't forget to tell him we're going to need the names of the tenants in the adjacent units and the parking out front is going to be cramped for a while."

Tobias followed Sanchez down the flight of stairs to ground level. As Sanchez turned the corner toward the apartment office, Tobias walked straight to his car. The first thing he did was get on the radio to call in the 187; he then dialed Lieutenant Dursk, hoping he was still searching the files on The Orange Network servers.

"Dursk." The gravelly voice rasped through the phone.

"Tobias. How are things coming?"

"We're wrapping up. Not a whole lot here besides what we found initially. I hope it helps. Did you find your guy?"

Tobias sighed. "Our unknown got to him first. He seems to be one step ahead of us, but I think we may be closing the gap. Problem is, we only have one reasonable route left, and if we exhaust that without results, we're probably done for a while."

"Yeah, I was afraid of that. After you left, I thought about what you said. It all sunk in, so I'm really not surprised at what you found. I do have something for you, though: the email exchanges after Hassenback's death were about a banking opportunity. So if you're right about that being a front of the child peddlers, then that's what most of  their conversation focused on. Bottom line: since Eduardo's dead, we can assume this guy's got the information he needs, knows where this place is."

"Yep. And now it's a foot race, and we haven't even started running yet. But you told me exactly what I needed to hear. I need you to do me a favor: fax all of the correspondences to Robert Michaels up at the Pasadena Superior Court. I'm about to make a call to the guy and ask for a warrant to gain access to Eduardo's transactional history at his bank."

"Not a problem. I'll get that out right now. I'm not giving you good odds on that angle, though, Tobias. A facility like that is most definitely cash-only."

"Understood. But he had to drive to get there. I'm going to bet it's a fair distance from LA. With any luck, maybe he needed gas or picked up a meal somewhere nearby. Anyhow, while it's definitely a long shot, it's all I got right now."

Tobias ended the call, then dialed the number for the Superior Court of Pasadena. After a brief exchange with the secretary, he got through to Michaels.

"Michaels." The falsetto voice sounded tinny, like an AM radio over the cell.

"Sir, this is Detective Tobias. I was your 7:30 this morning."

"Yes, detective. Search warrant net you anything?"

"Yeah, as a matter of fact, it did."

"Well, very good. How else may I be of service?"

"I need another warrant."

"Did this ..." Michaels paused. Tobias heard the slight sound of paper being shuffled around. "Eduardo. Did this Eduardo fellow give you enough information for me to issue an additional warrant?"

"No, sir. The condition in which we found him precluded such an elaboration." He said it with a little irritation, brought about by fatigue and the feeling a runner gets when he comes in second during a big race. The pause in the conversation made him regret his tone.

"What do you mean, detective?"

"He's dead. We have no ..."

Michaels interrupted, incredulous. "He's dead? Same perp?"

"No doubt. Identical M.O."

Michaels let out a low, wet whistle. "No shit?"

"There's more. Turns out Eduardo had an exchange of emails with Hassenback when Hassenback was being processed by the morgue. The content of the emails specifically centered on interest in the facility behind the phony marketing literature sent to Hassenback by Eduardo. Ergo, our unknown now has information about the location of this facility. The particulars of the exchange itself should either be on their way or at your office right now. I just had them faxed."

"And this is going to be the basis for a warrant against whom?"

"First National Bank of California. I want to take a look at Eduardo's transactional history. It's a long shot, but it might give us an indication as to where this facility is. Right now, it's the only trail we have."

This time, Michaels surprised Tobias, offered no resistance or skepticism.

"Well, you were right on the money about Eduardo, detective, so who am I to deny you access to a transactional history? Drop by in about an hour to pick it up." Michaels paused, then said, "Oh, and by the way, can you do me a favor?"

"Sure," said Tobias, a little perplexed at what he might be able to do for Michaels.

"Next time you get a case like this, could you spread things out a little bit so I'm not typing up all of your affidavits and your search warrants?"

Tobias shared tired chuckle with Michaels, then disconnected. As he placed his phone in his pocket, he noticed Sanchez walking back toward him.

"Everythink okay?" He asked Sanchez.

"Obviously, he's not happy. He's looking up the names of the adjacent tenants now, said he'll bring them to us in a few minutes."

"He'll bring them to you. You're going to stay here and help control the scene and make certain we didn't miss anything. I need to go back downtown to the courthouse and pick up another search warrant. From there, I'm going straight to the bank."

Tobias's voice trailed at the end, and Sanchez gave him a look. Neither of them had much sleep over the past couple of days, Tobias especially, and the young officer's expression obviously questioned how much longer the detective was going to be able to operate.

"You going to be okay to drive? If you don't want to do the running around, I can go pick up the warrant and run to the bank."

"That's okay, kid. I'm fine. Warrant's in my name, anyway. "

Tobias got in his cruiser, closed the door, started the engine, and then rolled down the driver's-side window. He waved Sanchez over, then said, "One more thing."

"Yes, Detective Columbo?"

"See if Mr. Ruez has a manifest of the vehicles allowed to park here."

"You think our guy still might be here?"

"I really doubt it. Better to be thorough, though. On the outside chance you rolled up right as he was walking out."

The phone picked up on the second ring, a pleasant-sounding female voice answering. A familiar voice. The voice of an angel. Or the nearest thing to it as far as Clark could tell.

"Hello?"

Clark paused, tried to collect his thoughts. How many people should he get involved with his problem? He figured only two. That was the upper bound for the possible answers he had to that question. He really only knew two people in the area. Two people who were important to him, anyway. Sure, he'd get others involved, but they really didn't count. Those were people who committed heinous crimes on a daily basis. He couldn't get them in any more trouble than they had already gotten themselves into. But adding innocent people into his plan didn't sit well with him, placed a dull ache in the pit of his stomach.

As much as he didn't want to get her involved, though, he knew he had to talk to her. He had, after all, just killed her ex-husband; and as soon as the body was found, the police would be called, they'd build their interview list, and she'd be on it. So she had to be ready for that. And he needed her assistance, too. If his hunch was correct, the police probably already knew who he was and what he was doing. In other words, this was now a race to the desert, and he had to prepare for it, beat them to it. To accomplish that, he'd have to be at different locations simultaneously over the next several hours, and last time he checked that was impossible. Whether he liked it or not, he needed help, just a little, to provide him just enough inertia to push through to the end.

"Hello?" She asked again.

"Hey, Carri, it's Clark."

"Jack?" She asked, not attempting to hide either her surprise or delight.

"Yeah. I really need to talk to you. It's important. It's also imperative we talk somewhere where we'll not be overheard." He didn't mean to, but he spoke each sentence deliberately, placing a significant pause between each.

"You can come to my place. You still have the address?" The excitement in her voice leveled off, diminished by the concern brought about by his request.

"Yes. When are you off work?"

"Actually, I took the day off to take care of some things around the house. You want to come over now?"

Clark did a quick calculation. He expected Joe to call in a little under an hour. With a ten minute drive to Carri's, that gave him about 45 minutes to talk to her. Probably ten minutes to tell her what he'd done to Chuck, followed by another half hour describing what he needed from her. He had no doubt she would take the news about her ex with equanimity. For years, she'd been forced to go out of her way to adjust her life to avoid the man. He'd put her in the hospital several times, and Clark knew if he hadn't killed Chuck, the man would've probably eventually killed her. No doubt he would have killed her. After what Clark heard him say, it was only a matter of time. Now she'd have the freedom to move about as she pleased. She wouldn't have to worry about Chuck anymore. The rationale made sense to him, but he wondered if he was fooling himself. Understanding and anticipating the behavior of a man had always come easy for him, but he'd never fully understood how women thought, figured the differences in thinking and motivation had to be rooted at an atomic level, a place he just couldn't get to.

"You still there?" Her voice was soft, velvety in his ear.

"Yeah, sorry. I'll come right over. Can you do me a favor, though?"

"Sure."

"You promise? This is really important."

"Yes. I promise." Her voice pitched up a notch, betraying a slight amount of stress.

"I want you to stay in an interior room. Don't stand at a window or out front waiting for me to arrive. Come to the door only when I knock. Can you do that?"

"Yeah. I guess. What's this all about, Jack?" The slight tinge in her voice spilled out with a nervousness Clark caught with a little trepidation of his own. He couldn't have her waiting for him only to see Chuck's car roll up. She'd definitely panic, maybe even call the police.

"Can you trust me on this? I promise you I'll explain when I get there." He did his best to say it softly, reassuringly.

Carri sighed. "I can do that. But it's a weird request all the same."

"I know. I apologize. I'll see you in ten minutes."

Clark hung up the phone, started the Mustang, and headed down the road, wondering the whole way whether he'd made the right decision to involve Carri.

The trip to the courthouse had taken less than a half hour, but Tobias was more than a little concerned about having to wait another thirty minutes for the paperwork. The thought of making small talk with Michaels' anachronistic secretary didn't bother him too much. Actually, he figured a short conversation might help him fight what he was really worried about: dozing off in the reception area. He knew if he fell asleep there, they'd have to call someone to pick him up. His body felt like lead, and every movement he made felt as it were through Jell-O rather than air. To his surprise, the warrant was waiting for him when he arrived, delivered by the codgy secretary herself, who was wearing something Abe Lincoln's wife might have gone bananas over. He told her to thank Michaels for the quick work; she responded with a curt nod that told him she'd relay the message. Twenty minutes later he was at the main branch of the First National Bank of California.

He entered the bank, found Eduardo's transaction history was already waiting for him, all tucked neatly into a large, yellow envelope. The clerk who'd handed it over told him in a hushed, confidential voice some heavy from the courthouse named Michaels had phoned in ahead, said a warrant was on the way, and the information was time-sensitive. So the bank had put a priority on running the report, feeling it was their duty to help the police expedite their mission. Tobias just gave the guy a tired smile, handed him the a copy of the warrant, thanked him, and left, grateful for the additional surprise granted by his friend at the courthouse.

He beat a baked path back to his cruiser, the hot sun pushing down on him as he walked, bathing him in a sleepy warmth that decimated his focus, threatening to lull him to sleep. He opened the door to his car, was pleased to find the interior had kept a remnant of the coolness the air conditioner had blasted out on his way to the bank. He closed the door quickly to keep the heat out, started the car, then flipped open his phone to call Sanchez.

"Hey, boss."

"How's going out there?"

"Got a D-II here who thinks he's God's gift. Didn't really want me around until I told him who I was working with. That changed his attitude slightly."

"What's the detective's name?"

"Guy named Blevens. Looks kind of like Mr. Belvedere on Steroids. Why do people dress like that?"

Tobias chuckled. "Yeah, he can be an ass at times, but he's pretty good. He waiting for me?"

"He's started the preliminaries, but, yeah, he said he wanted to talk to you."

"Scene's no different from Hassenback's, really. Tell him I said so. In fact, tell him I'll have someone from the precinct deliver the Hassenback file I worked so he can use it for background, cross reference it with what he finds to definitively link the two crimes as being the work of the same actor. Unless we caught this guy in the middle of something, though, he's not going to find squat in that apartment. Just a db, some missing computer equipment, and a clean floor."

There was a pause in the conversation, Tobias understood Sanchez was using it to write everything he said in his notebook.

"Anything else, boss?"

"Yeah. Tell Blevens this one's his. I got the transactional history from the bank. He doesn't need to know about that yet. I'm going to take it home, get a glass of wine, and work my way through it. I had no idea a report detailing a year's worth of deposits and withdraws could generate so much damn paper."

"Got it. By the way, the apartment manager does make the tenants register the vehicles they park on the grounds with the office here. I have a guy checking all the plates against the list now."

"Good work. See that part though. Let me know if you come up with anything. When you're done, come on out to the house. You can help me work though all this bullshit."

"Roger that. Any chance for some of those world-famous burgers tonight? Nobody cooks that stuff up better than Carol."

"I think there's a distinct possibility. Listen, I need to go take a cold shower. Maybe that'll wake me up enough to get through Eduardo's banking history. I bet the whole state of California didn't have this many transactions last year. How much longer do you have with respect to vehicle elimination?"

"At our current rate? Probably about an hour."

"Okay. See you then, kid."

Tobias slapped his phone shut, worked hard to keep his eyes open and focused on the road. He knew a cold shower would do him good, would revitalize him, give him the energy he needed for a couple of more hours of work. As he drove north away from LA, he knew for certain the day couldn't get much longer. Another few hours, and he'd be asleep. It wouldn't matter what he was doing. He'd just nod off and be done with the exhaustion.

Clark exited the gas station parking lot, followed West Woodbury Road to the 210, and merged into its comparatively light north-LA traffic. As he made his way toward the Foothills Blvd exit, he hoped Carri had listened to him and kept to an interior room. He knew the request on its surface was ridiculous, but it was still the right thing to ask: no doubt she wouldn't be able to make him out behind the tinted windows of the Mustang when he rolled up, no doubt she would panic, no doubt she would call the police out of fear for her safety. As he headed up Foothill Blvd, he tried to rehearse what he was going to say to her. As with Joe, however, there was simply no easy way, no shortcut for delivering the news. He took at a crumpled piece of paper from his shirt pocket that detailed where she lived. The instructions told him to take a left on Santa Carlotta Street. A minute later, he was parked in front of light yellow, stucco home, the top of which was roofed with an expensive Spanish tile. It had a three car garage, a high fence surrounding the backyard, mature trees and shrubs decorated the front yard and both sides of the driveway. He exited the vehicle, then gave his shirt a good tug to ensure the gun in the small of his back wasn't protruding. He made his way up the driveway, followed a tiled path to the front door of the house, and rang the buzzer. Carri answered the door a moment later.

"Jack! Good to see you again!" She smiled, eyes plainly displaying pleasure. "I kept to a back room like you said. I still don't understand the request, though."

Clark said nothing, just stood patiently in front of the entryway.

Carri smiled and blushed. "Oh, sorry! Come in please."

She stepped back from the door while holding it open. Clark entered the home, walked through a conservative foyer with closets on the left and right, a tasteful hanging chandelier, and white sandstone flooring. The Foyer opened into a quaint, old-fashioned living room that sported solid oak floors and an assortment of older, clearly-adopted furniture. While the furnishings hadn't been purchased as a set, the eclectic collection produced a harmony that reminded him of his parent's farmhouse. The whole orchestration gave him an instant feeling of country living with a highly Spartan inflection. As he stood in the middle of the living room, Carri closed the door, then walked up to him, stood so close he could smell the perfume on her, feel her breath.

"Hi." She said, not bothering to hide the obvious flirtation. Her mouth was curled at the edges, her eyes beaming with brightness. Clark once gain found himself captivated by her, found himself more comfortable than he'd been in years. But he knew he couldn't allow himself the luxury of comfort. Not yet. Maybe not ever. He took a deep, inconspicuous breath, held it, then let it out.

"I think you should probably have a seat, Carri." He said it as gently as he could, knew the weird requests on his part were beginning to stack up like dirty dishes.

Carri gave Clark a quizzical look, the half smile and the dance in her eyes all but gone, supplanted by a more sober countenance. She backed to her sofa, sat, then said,

"What's wrong?"

Clark didn't answer immediately, just took out Chuck's keys and threw them to her. She caught them one-handed and looked at them.

"You recognize those?" He asked.

"No. Should I?" She looked again at the keys, then set them on the stone coffee table in front of her. She tucked her feet under her legs, and continued to stare up at the big man in front of her.

"They're Chuck's." His words hung in the air for a moment. She looked even more confused. After a long pause, she said,

"Jack, what are you doing with Chuck's keys?" She appeared to be thinking about the mixture of possibilities, which changed her look from confusion to concern to fear.

"I don't know of any other way to say this to you, Carri. He's dead. I killed him." The final two sentences hit the air like a shot, the concussion of which knocked Carri off center.

"What do you mean?" Her voice changed, moved up in pitch but markedly quieter. "What do you mean you killed Chuck?" This time barely above a throaty whisper.

Clark knew he cut an imposing figure, especially when standing in front of a seated, petite woman who was clearly frightened. So he crouched down in front of her, made sure his eye level was the same as hers. He softened his voice, then said,

"He tried to kill me, Carri. With this." He took the gun from the small of his back. Carri flinched at the sight, brought her knees up to her chest to make herself small.

"Oh my God, Jack. No! Why? Oh my God. Why? What happened?" The words just spilled out, then stopped. She looked straight at Clark, her eyes got wide, and then she let out a choked, plaintive yell.

"No!"

Clark remained where he was, motionless, frozen by the reaction.

Carri took her arms from around her knees and placed both hands to her face, employing them to wipe away the torrent of tears streaming out of the corners of her eyes and down her cheeks. Her breathing became ragged, interrupted irregularly by a soft choking sound. They  maintained their positions for a good five minutes, Clark soldered to the floor, Carri on the couch crying.

"I'm sorry," he said. "He wanted to kill me. I really had no choice."

"You think I'm crying for Chuck?" She let out a choked laugh and then cried even harder.

"I guess I don't really know," he said softly. "The last thing I wanted to do was hurt you." He meant it too. He'd killed Chuck so the bastard wouldn't have any avenue to hurt her after his business in the desert was complete. She had done something very special for him that night in his room: she had stayed with him, held him, supplied emotional footings when his were about to give. For that he owed her a lot, perhaps even his life. For that he'd never forget her, always be in debt to her.

Carri got up from the couch and again walked right up to him. He stood. As she approached, he could see the despondence spilling out of her, pressing her down and wreaking havoc on her every movement. She looked down at the gun in his hand.

"Could you please put that down." The words came out spasmodically, hacked to the verge of incomprehension by receding sobs.

Clark looked at this left hand, noticed the gun.

"Sorry." He placed the Colt on the coffee table, then returned his attention to her.

Carri stepped closer, wrapping her arms around his midsection, placing her head just above his sternum.

"Oh, Jack." She took a deep breath, then spoke quickly. "I couldn't care less about Chuck. Really, I couldn't. After all he's put me through? He deserved what he got, the bastard. This is the last thing you need, though. When I saw you last ... those things you told me. Those horrible, horrible things. I would have stayed. I wanted to stay. After holding you all night, all I wanted to do was hold you more. But I thought you needed space. So I left, and I didn't want to leave. But I did. I wanted you to call. I kept thinking about you. I couldn't work I thought about you so much. And then you called. And I was so happy. And you tell me this. I don't care about Chuck. You see? I care about you! And what if you're caught? You might go to jail and I might not ever see you ..."

"Shhh." Clark whispered. He lowered his head and gently kissed the nape of her neck. She turned her face to meet his and gave him a soft kiss. He wanted to kiss her more. He wanted to kiss her harder. He wanted her. But he couldn't. It wouldn't be fair. Not with the business at hand. So he brought his hands to her shoulders, massive paws that eclipsed any hint of what was under them. He pushed her slightly away and looked at her.

"I feel the same way. I do. But I can't."

"I understand. I'm sorry." She bent her head down, tears once again beginning to build and flow.

"No. You don't understand."

She looked up at him quickly, her ponytail bobbing behind her as she did.

"I don't?"

"No. I think you better sit down again, Carri. There's something else about me you need to know."

Tobias was seated at his kitchen table. He could definitely say he felt refreshed after having taken a cold shower, but he didn't feel reinvigorated. He'd been up for more than thirty hours, still felt exhausted, felt he probably had another few hours in him before he simply involuntarily shut down. He stared at the paperwork in front of him. There were two piles. The pile on the left held Eduardo's transactional history. It was a good inch thick, most of it populated with trivial purchases of less than five dollars. He felt a little acid move up his esophagus, where it pooled and burned. He shook his head at none one, figured if Eduardo were still alive, he'd kill him himself for being such a sloppy spender and putting him through such a monumental inconvenience. Who puts 89 cents on a debit card for a pack of gum? Really?

The pile on the right was a much smaller stack. It contained keys for the storefronts on the other side of Eduardo's transaction history. It was full of business names, addresses, and phone numbers. The bank had been thorough in giving him all they could, but he thought it was too bad they couldn't combine the reports somehow so he wouldn't have to do all that cross-referencing. Just as he was about to think of another way he could posthumously kill Eduardo, Carol walked to the table holding two glasses of wine.

"You read my mind!" He said.

"You're tired, dear. You should just go upstairs to bed. You can drink your wine while I give you a nice massage. You can do all this tomorrow, right?"
