Chapter 17

 

 

Clark walked toward his truck, his second-guessing about eliminating Eduardo dissipating with each step. He hadn't really wanted to kill the man, but it didn't take him long to realize it was the only thing he could do. He had priceless information in hand, information he knew would likely allow him to bring closure to the single most important objective of his life: to exact revenge on the man who'd destroyed his family. Alive, Eduardo would've presented too many risks, presented too many unknowns and too many distractions. Eduardo breathing was simply a luxury he couldn't afford. Not with the end so near, so close. Eduardo, therefore, had to die, just as Hassenback did.

Clark heard the sound of a single footfall behind him, a slight scuff on the uneven pavement. The warning he'd felt earlier in Eduardo's apartment returned, shot through his body like fire. He slowed but kept his direction, focused on listening to determine distance and location. The sound came to him over the still morning air. Soft steps. Slow steps. Careful steps. About ten feet behind him. Then he heard the voice. It was hushed, barely above a whisper, but laced with animosity.

"Well, whaddya know. If it ain't lover boy from the bar. You know, you should be a little more careful about where you park, asshole. I mean, what're the odds? Me just driving by, and there's your truck."

Clark recognized the voice immediately. It belonged to Carri's ex-husband, Chuck. He figured after getting his ass kicked in front of her, he was probably still a little pissed off. But he also figured the guy would be smart enough to avoid him for fear of another beating. Wrong on one count. Of course, two circumstances could give Chuck the courage he needed to face him now: he brought a friend or he was armed. Maybe both. There was only one way to find out. Clark took a single, deep breath, then slowly turned around.

Chuck was alone, filled with courage borrowed from the rounds punched into the clip of the Colt 45 Automatic he held at the end of his outstretched right arm. Understanding the odds for what they were, Clark slowly put down his briefcase and bag, then raised his hands until the tips of his fingers were even with the tops of his ears.

"Nice gun. Love the no-slip grip on those things. Could you do me a favor and not point that at me?" Clark spoke slowly, wondering if Chuck would actually risk firing the gun in a congested area full of potential witnesses. Sleeping or not, a round fired from a 45 in the early morning hours would certainly wake up a lot of people, definitely enough to provide a reliable description of the moron who was now standing in front of him.

Chuck just leered at him. "No way, asshole. I'm not stupid. Now pick up your shit; we're going for a ride." He motioned with his free hand at the briefcase and plastic bag at Clark's feet, the lit end of his cigarette creating a fiery zigzag in the air in front of him as he did.

Clark didn't move, simply stared at Chuck. He was a big guy, probably had quite a bit of stamina. But an outstretched arm wasn't meant to hold onto a three pound weight for an extended amount of time. If he could stall for even a few minutes, Chuck would have no choice but to lower his weapon, which might shift the odds his way, even if just a little.

"Hey, you hold all the cards, boss. But you need to know the thing in the bar was just a misunderstanding, a case of being at the wrong place at the wrong time." Clark kept his hands up, continued with the slow, soft tone.

Chuck said nothing. Clark gave him his best concerned look.

"You don't want to do this, man. Think about it. You have your whole life in front of you. You're gonna get caught, and then you're gonna spend the rest of your days in prison, or worse. It's just not worth it. Just put the gun down. I'll walk my way, you'll walk yours, and we'll forget this ever happened. Promise."

Chuck's facial expression slackened a bit. For a moment, he seemed to be considering what Clark had said. Then something flashed in his demeanor, an emotional hollowness tinged with a giddy anger. The face of a sociopath caught up in the ecstasy of feeling real emotion. And power.

"Fuck you. There ain't nothing you gonna say that's gonna make me change my mind. But it might be fun to watch you grovel before I splatter your brains all over the place. Maybe I'll make you beg for your life, too. If you're lucky."

Clark understood Chuck's reaction for what it was, pulled the sensibility card. He'd get nowhere with that. There's no talking sense into a sociopath. He'd have to take another route, a much more dangerous one, but one that might just buy him the time he needed. He motioned with his head to the cigarette slowly burning to a stub in Chuck's right hand.

"Mind if I bum one of those?"

"Why? You and my wife share one of 'em after she spread her slutty legs for you? I bet that's what you want to be thinking about when the back of your head gets blown off, isn't it? The both of you lying there in bed, passing a smoke back and forth after you've done your dirty business. Well, fuck you! You ain't gettin' shit."

Adding accelerant to a fire can be a dangerous thing. You have to pick the right one to make the fire bend to your will. Clark needed Chuck's to smolder for a while, choke him with heavy smoke before erupting into a consuming conflagration. He thought about what he was going to say, hoping it would be more like adding an armload of dry leaves to a open flame, rather than a gallon of gasoline.

"I'll trade you."

"Trade me what, bitch? I don't want nothing but your stinking, rotting corpse on the ground in front of me. What the hell do you have to trade?"

"I can tell you the truth about Carri. You're right about her. Don't you want to know what we did? Don't you want to hear about the thing's she's done to you?"

The man's reaction changed quickly, transformed from a giddy rage to the look of an addict about to get another needle full of heroine. The expression lasted for only a moment, then reverted back to its former complexion.

"Fuck you."

More fuel.

"You don't want to know? To be honest, I don't want to be the only person who gets fucked over by what she did. Hell, I even have other names for you if you want them." Clark paused for effect. "Lots of them."

The junkie was back. It was as easy as flipping a light switch. Right fuel. Clark hoped he'd get the right outcome.

"Fucking bitch!" Chuck said it mostly under his breath, basically spat it out between grinding teeth.

"Give me the smoke, Chuck. I need one if I'm gonna tell you everything."

"Fuck it. Who am I to deny a man his last smoke?" Chuck reached in his shirt pocket with his left hand, removed his pack, and tossed it to Clark, who caught with his right hand. Clark took out a cigarette, removed the lighter from the plastic lining of the pack, and lit up. It had been years since he'd last smoked. As he inhaled, he could feel the nicotine pour into his body, course through his veins like a heavy poison. He had to focus not to cough. He put the lighter back in the plastic lining and started to toss the pack back to Chuck.

"Stop! No way I'm falling for that, asshole! You think I'm stupid? Just throw them on the ground."

Clark did what Chuck asked, threw the pack to the side. He took another deep drag from his cigarette and exhaled.

"You just gonna sit there looking like a fucking idiot, or are you going to tell me what the fucking bitch did to me?"

"Where do you want me to start?" Clark took another deep drag, exhaled.

"Don't fuck with me. Just tell me what you two did. All the sleazy details so I can go shove that up her ass after I'm done here with you!"

"Okay." Clark lowered his hands, placed them at his sides. "We met at some dive bar. Can't remember the name of it. She was sitting alone on a stool, wearing one of those really tight miniskirts, legs crossed, almost falling out of a pink tank top. I couldn't help myself. I walked up. I didn't need to say anything. She just asked if I wanted a good time, said she had some coke, wanted to get high ..."

"So she's not just a fucking whore, she a fucking coke whore?" Chuck sounded incredulous.

"Yeah. She did a line in the bathroom before we left. Invited me in, but the bar was starting to get busy, so I didn't risk it. I waited ..."

"Just get to the fucking details, fuckhead. I don't need the scenery."

Clark remained cool but knew he had to resolve this quickly: the sky to the east was turning pink, which would soon be followed by a bright morning sun. He needed the cover of darkness, couldn't risk a witnesses. His cigarette had burned most of the way down. A couple of more drags and he'd have it just about where he'd need it.

"We came back here to my place." Clark motioned with his head toward Eduardo's apartment. He took one more drag of his cigarette, then lowered it to his side, rolling it between his thumb and forefinger.

"She did you up there, didn't she?" Chuck rotated his head slightly, taking in the apartment complex. "Just goes to show, a slut'll fuck anyone, anywhere. Now tell me what you and the bitch did. Consider it your last confession."

Chuck erupted with a short, hysterical laugh, his face etched with a perverse satisfaction. He was nothing more than a junky greedily taking a hit, feeding off the emotion running through his body. Instead of elaborating, Clark just stood there and said nothing, watched the man simmer impatiently.

"Tell me what you and the whore did, asshole! I wanna whisper it in her ear while she's dying, watch the life run out of her while I'm telling her she's nothing but a dirty fucking slut."

"Chuck, that's private, man."

Chuck keeled back slightly in disbelief, dumbstruck at Clark's flat response. The complexion in his face changed to the nervous look of an addict who'd lost his needle.

"Fuck you! You fucked her, and now you're fucked!" He tightened his grip on the Colt, lowered it so it was pointing right at Clark's groin.

"No, Chuck. I just told you what you wanted to hear." Clark was sure Chuck's self-control was beyond the man's reach, inextricably lodged somewhere between anger and anticipation. Not good. Pushed the odds of the moron firing his weapon to likely. Which meant he needed to end this quickly. And Chuck was just about where he needed him to be to do that. He smiled, risked a laugh of his own, then said, "She's not a bad person, Chuck. You are. You're the fuck."

"You think you're pretty smart, don't you, big man? I wonder how smart you're gonna feel with a big fucking hole in your head! Fuck this. Get your shit. We're going for a ride. One way trip for you, though, asshole."

Clark noticed the gun was beginning to quake, a result of fatigue, confusion, and a copious amount of adrenaline. He said nothing, just locked eyes with the man and visualized what he needed to do. As he rolled his cigarette until it was pinched between the pad of his thumb and the nail of his forefinger, Chuck slowly elevated his gun for a head shot, the muzzle tracing out tiny circles in front of him. No more time. Clark exhaled, then flicked his cigarette toward Chuck's face. Just by dumb luck, the butt connected with the man's left eye. He would've been happy with a neck shot or even a shot to the cheek; either way, the reaction was unavoidable: Chuck instinctively lowered his head and thrust his hands to his face. Clark quickly closed the distance between them, grabbed the gun with his left hand and delivered a vicious uppercut to the bottom of Chuck's chin with his right. The momentum of the blow sent Chuck's head skyward. Clark met it on its way up with a crushing head-butt, ripped the gun away, and grabbed Chuck's throat and squeezed it until he was sure he'd crushed his airway. He could feel his fingernails dig into the man's neck, could feel blood erupting around his digits and trickling up his wrist. Chuck's legs buckled, and Clark eased him to the ground, looking into his bulging eyes as he did.

"Never give your opponent a weapon, asshole."

Clark checked the gun, switched on the safety, and stowed it in the beltline in the small of his back. Then he assessed his surroundings: Chuck's crumpled body was about ten feet away from the right rear bumper of his truck, sprawled on the asphalt, convulsing in a futile attempt to take in oxygen. A few lights had come on in some of the apartments in the complex, early morning risers preparing for another day of work. Daylight was coming. He had a dilemma, figured he probably had about ten minutes to decide what to do about it.

He couldn't leave Chuck's body where it lay. And there was no obvious place to hide it. He thought briefly about lugging it up to Eduardo's apartment, but he didn't want to risk a commotion, wasn't even sure if he had the energy for that anyway. He couldn't put him in his truck, either. It was cluttered, full of possessions he used for day-to-day living. If he had the time, he could clear it out, but the whole process would take him at least a half hour, which was probably 20 minutes more than he could spare. Under the circumstances, there was really only one option: he'd have to find Chuck's car, conceal the body there, drive it somewhere remote, dump the body, and return to the apartment complex later in the day to swap vehicles.

It took about a minute for Clark to drag Chuck's body so it was hidden in the shadows beneath the bumper of his truck. It took less than a minute to find the keys to Chuck's car and another two frantic minutes to locate the vehicle itself. Clark unlocked it, started it, and drove it back to his truck, parking the car so the vehicles were almost bumper-to-bumper, the trunk of the car about three feet from the bed of his truck.

Lifting a body is awkward, even to the relatively low height presented by the trunk of Chuck's Mustang. Clark figured it was roughly two and a half feet. It'd been some time since he'd lifted anything of considerable weight from the ground to that height. He briefly wished rigor was something that set in a matter of minutes rather than hours. Lifting a dead weight is difficult enough; lifting a weight that can unexpectedly rotate or shift is something else entirely. Clark squatted at the direct center of the body, used his right arm to move Chuck's leg so his knee bent off from the ground. Clamping the base of the man's skull with his left hand and hooking his right forearm under the crook of the man's right knee, he carefully hefted Chuck's body off the ground and threw him into the trunk of the car and closed the lid.

He collected his briefcase and the plastic bag containing the items he'd confiscated from Eduardo's apartment, placed both on the driver's side floor board of the Mustang. He then returned to his truck, retrieved what little he kept that could identify him, his toolbox, his pay-as-you-go phone, and placed those in the back seat of the car. As he walked to the driver's side door of the Mustang, he took one last quick look around to ensure he'd cleaned the area sufficiently. The lot basically looked as it had when he'd walked across it a few hours before. He climbed into the car, fired it up, and drove out of the parking lot. He figured transferring his possessions would likely turn out to be unnecessary, but he didn't want to risk leaving anything that could be connected to him in a truck that could be vandalized, towed, or even discovered by the police. As he pulled out of the lot, he peered into the rearview mirror, saw the first of the early-morning risers making his way to a vehicle. He'd just made it.

The staccato bursts of gunfire reverberated in Tobias's ears, emanating from a single gunman several yards away. The combination of adrenaline and a hyper awareness amplified the sound just enough to cause a persistent ringing in his ears. He was behind his cruiser, squatting next to the front, driver's-side wheel well for protection. The gunman in front of him was walking slowly toward him, wearing full body armor, brandishing an older model, Russian-made, AK-47. The man let go with another controlled burst of three quick shots: pop-pop-pop! Tobias felt the air around him stir slightly as one bullet slammed into the body of his cruiser and another ricocheted off the asphalt and careened by him. He was basically trapped. The only thing working in his favor was the weather: a heavy rain had descended on the Los Angeles area, blocking the sun, making daylight dark. The blurry atmospheric mess protected him, like a heavy blanket of confusion.

He figured it had been less than four minutes since he'd called for backup. It felt like hours. He felt compelled to jump up and lay fire toward the gunman, but his training contradicted his instinct, told him to hold his position, gun in hand, and wait for support, support he hoped he'd get before he was discovered. Even in the face of how badly outmatched he was, he was surprised at how calm he felt: out in the open, he knew he stood little to no chance. His department-issue nine millimeter offered him absolutely no reassurance over a madman with a machine gun. His only salvation might come from a quick shot over the hood of his car. A lucky shot. Until that opportunity insinuated itself into his reality, he'd hold fast and hope the madman would move away from him, toward the rows of cars in the parking lot behind the bank, to chase shadows in the rain.

But the gunman had other plans, continued advancing. Like he knew where he was. Like he could sense his position behind the cruiser. He let go another three quick rounds. pop-pop-pop! A windshield to the left of him exploded, showering him with fine shards of glass. He risked a glance around the front tire of his car, rainwater streaming over his head, wrapping around his eyes, and blurring his vision slightly. He saw two boots and the muzzle of an AK-47 approximately eight feet from his position. The man's gun was down to his side, hanging there, momentarily disengaged from its deadly business. Tobias made a split-second decision, knew it might be now or never. He took three short, quick breaths, gripped the handle of his gun. He figured the man to be about six foot two, figured he should draw and point at a 30 degree level of inclination above the horizontal, decrementing the angle gradually as his lifted himself fully off the ground.

He slowly moved his feet so they were positioned directly beneath him, so his legs tensed like coiled springs. He made a silent three-count, then pushed up. From there, everything happened in slow motion. As his arms and head thrust up from behind the hood of his cruiser, he fired his first shot. It was low but centered. It hit the gunman in the chest, pushing him back slightly, the bullet falling ineffectively as it bounced off the man's Kevlar vest. As he moved to a half-upright stance, he lowered his aim a little and let off another round. This one found its mark. Still a little low, it hit the man in the side of the neck. The wound spurted a small quantity of arterial blood. The gunman seemed surprised for a moment, appeared to not completely understand what was rising off the hood of the car in front of him. But he appeared to understand the apparition was a threat, so he quickly raised his left arm, leveling the AK-47 at Tobias. The last shot Tobias got off was in a full upright stance. He squeezed the trigger. The shot moved from the barrel of his gun to the center of the man's forehead in less than 1/1000 of a second. A split second later, the bullet and a pink mist raced from the back of the gunman's head. A second after that the gunman was on the ground, dead.

Clark had been thinking about where to dispose of Chuck's body before it had even hit the ground. The encroaching daylight and the growing congestion on the roadways prohibited dumping it locally. Instead, he'd have to find some place remote, a place that would provide the cover the receding darkness no longer could. By the time he had Chuck loaded in the trunk, he'd decided where to take him. He headed east on Edinger Avenue, doing his best to blend into the light, early morning traffic, paying particular attention to posted speed limits. He was driving a stolen car with a dead body in the trunk. Not a good combination if he got pulled over. After several minutes on Edinger, he headed north on the 405, then got on the 10 until he reached the Pacific Coast highway. He took the PCH to Topanga Canyon Boulevard, then drove up the windy, coastal mountain road until he found a suitable dirt road careening off to the right. He drove about 100 yards off the main road and stopped the car. From beginning to end, the whole trip had taken him just over an hour and a half.

He shut down the engine, killed the lights, and exited the vehicle. He took several deep breaths of the fresh, rural air. It smelled like a salty farm. It smelled like home. It filled him with a healthy, morning medicine that helped purge the smoke from his lungs, burn the nicotine out of his system. He stood next to the car, bathed in a dark, morning shadow. In LA, the sun would be shining with its glorious morning warmth, a warmth that would in a few hours turn into an afternoon sizzle. In the hills above Topanga Canyon, however, the shadows continued to obscure the landscape, adding a muddy confusion to help cover his movements. He hurried around to the rear of the car, popped the trunk, lifted Chuck, and shouldered him. He carried him to the nearest tree, propped his body against its trunk, and headed back to the car. There was no sense in trying to hide the body. Even out in the relative wilderness of the hills to the east of Malibu, Chuck would be found. It really didn't matter to Clark when that happened, just so long as he wasn't there when it was discovered.

He returned to the Mustang, decided on a more leisurely ride back. If he timed it right, he'd arrive at the apartment complex at the midpoint between the morning rush and lunchtime. He turned on the radio, adjusted the tuner until he found a news station, then got caught up in a story about a recent quake in the Caribbean, the length of the program taking him all the way back into the heart of LA. As he headed back up Edinger Avenue, he turned the radio off and thought about his next move. He decided that after retrieving his truck, he'd return to his room, collect what little possessions he had there, then immediately head out to the desert. He needed to find a way into the hills north of the 15 that would allow him to do reconnaissance, find the facility, assess its security, determine its weaknesses. As he was deciding whether to camp or stay in Baker, he saw a patrol car in his rearview mirror. It had its lights on. And it was closing fast.

He was about a block from where he needed to be. He had no options. He couldn't ditch the car and run. Too much stuff, too little time. Besides, grabbing him would be as easy as bagging a dog with two legs. One man against the LAPD was no match, not out in the open in broad daylight anyway. He grasped the steering wheel tightly, sweat from his palms slowly eroding his grip. He forced himself to look ahead, but the lights in his mirror told him he needed to get to the right. Just as he was about the pull to the side of the road, the patrol car screamed past.

His panic eased. He realized he was holding his breath and let it out. He dried his hands on his pants, continued to roll slowly up the street. As he turned on Parton, he got his first clear view of the apartment complex. What he saw brought the panic back in spades: just under Eduardo's unit, he counted no less than three unmarked cars, six patrol cars, and a crime scene unit. He continued driving. He could forget about his truck. He could forget about his perceived lead on the authorities, too. All that was done. And all he'd worked for over the past two years now seemed to be hanging in the balance.

Situation? Grim. He'd killed three men. He was still driving a stolen car. And he was driving it right past an apartment that contained the body of one of his victims. He was too close to the crime scene, and the car was definitely too hot to be driving. He needed another vehicle. He needed cash. He needed to get out of town. For the first time he could remember, he needed help. He picked up his phone, flipped it open, and dialed the number of the only person he figured might deliver.

Rick got the call at 4:30 in the morning. The guy he'd received most of his instructions from, Manheim, told him the ranch needed another asset, their shitty euphemism for telling him he needed to abduct another little girl to serve up to the slimy-ass bastards who visited the boss's spread outside of Baker. He really didn't like what he'd been doing, could barely tolerate it. He'd done abductions before, just not children. He'd worked for a mob boss in Las Vegas for a couple of years, and most of the people he'd picked up were slime-balls who owed a lot of cash to the Vegas Mafia. Those abductions, however, didn't pay that well. Not like these. Not even close.

Jason Dungston was another aspect of the job he barely tolerated. Jason was his new partner, the guy he was supposed to break in. But the man was a moron, and he'd demonstrated on various occasions that his diminished mental capacity was going to either get them killed or jailed. Eventually. With Jason, it was just a matter of time. No, he didn't like what he was doing, and he didn't like who he was doing it with. At this point, he was only staying because he was broke. That and he liked money. A lot. And the cash for a patrol was good. The cash for a pick-up was even better. One more pickup, and he'd have enough money in the bank to last him a year. Enough to get out of the sleazy operation he'd somehow gotten himself into. After that: fuck Jason, and fuck the organization too.

Rick drove east on the 91, enjoying the silence afforded him by the task at hand. He was in charge of the abduction, and those were always executed solo. That meant no Jason, at least for a while. And that translated into a less risky morning, with the added bonus of not having to listen to the man's drool-laced, low-brow blather that seemed to always be erupting from his stupid mouth. He forced the thoughts of his partner out of his mind. Instead, he concentrated on the overall handling of the car, which was a late-model Honda Civic, a vehicle that saturated the streets in Southern California. He chose it because it matched almost exactly the car driven by the parents of the girl. He'd boosted it just after 7 AM, right after its owner had stepped out of it and into a low-rise building full of low-paid government workers.

In his experience, abductions had always been carried out with stolen vehicles, even when he'd worked in Vegas. Under both organizations, the stolen vehicle is used long enough to make the abduction, after which he would immediately rendezvous with a company-owned vehicle. This keeps the organization's assets clean during the period of highest risk. Once the swap is made, the stolen vehicle is scrubbed and abandoned. If it's found,  the police typically chalk the theft up to kids joy riding, in which case they don't even bother with printing.

 Once he hit Riverside, Rick took the University Avenue exit, heading east. He followed University for about a mile, took a left on Franklin Avenue, followed by another left on 5th Street. The girl lived on 5th Street, which was exactly one block north of Longfellow Elementary, the school she attended, probably as a third-grader. Rick made a mental note of the girl's address, then spent the next two hours driving through the surrounding streets. Driving streets allowed him to gauge traffic levels and avoid unforeseen obstacles such as road work and dead-ends, which allowed him to choose an optimal exit route. After running through his options, he decided his post-abduction route would take him north to Third Street. From there, he'd head east, then take the 215 up to San Bernardino, where he'd meet Jason at the Central City Mall for the hand-off. Afterward, his part would be done. He'd get the car cleaned, abandon it at the mall, and then get a bus ticket back to LA.

Rick looked at the clock on the console of the Civic. It was 10:30 in the morning. He had about five hours to kill until the girl got out of school. Five hours until boredom turned into a torturous, hour-long adrenaline rush. He could do without the risk-induced stress, but at forty grand a snatch, he figured it was worth it. He also figured he'd do this one last job and get the hell out of LA forever. Maybe move to South America where the living is cheap. Maybe even try to get clean. He looked at the clock on the dash again. It still said 10:30. It was going to be a long day. He worked his way once more along his planned exit route, deciding this time he'd take it to San Bernardino and check out the drop-off point at the mall. After that, he'd head to Victorville, get some lunch, and then head back to Riverside. The trip would likely burn at least a few hours, which would keep him out of Riverside and minimize his exposure in the neighborhoods surrounding the school.

 

Rick returned from Victorville at three in the afternoon. He drifted by Longfellow Elementary, circled around the block, and parked on the corner of Eucalyptus and 5th, betting the girl would choose the shortest, most direct route to her house. While he waited, he pulled out her photo, studying it for what felt like the hundredth time, firming up his best guess regarding her height and weight, length of hair, facial features, and style of clothing. Ten minutes later, school let out, and children began filing past him on their way home, walking, talking, and playing, oblivious to the world around them. It took about 20 minutes for all of them to march past, not one of them matching the photograph. He waited another 15 minutes just be sure she hadn't had a late start home, then switched strategies and began randomly driving one of three pre-selected loops that would take him past the girl's house every 15 minutes. At 4:30 in the afternoon, he rolled down 5th Street for a final pass. The girl was nowhere to be found, and the daily business of the neighborhood was picking up with returning parents. Too dangerous to continue surveillance, and certainly too risky to even think about an abduction, he followed his exit route to the 215 and headed toward Victorville. As he merged into the right-hand lane, he flipped his phone open and dialed Frank Malone, his immediate supervisor, but a guy he rarely spoke to. After several rings, Frank answered.

"You got the package?"

"No."

"What happened?"

"Inventory problem. Out of stock."

"Shit. Okay. Let's check again tomorrow. Let Jason know."

Rick pressed the end button on his phone, then dialed Jason. He could think of hundreds of more appropriate names for the guy, most of them ending in the word shit. Jason answered immediately.

"Hey, you got her?" Jason sounded annoyed, perhaps even a little manic.

"Who?" Rick played coy, hoping the dumbshit on the other end of the line would understand they shouldn't be broadcasting the details of their business to the rest of the world on an unsecured cellular signal. The thought flashed again through his mind: one of these days, that fuck is going to get us jailed or killed.

"Who the fuck do you think I'm talking about, bozo? The girl you were supposed to pick up today."

"I have no idea what you're talking about. Is this Mark?" Rick rattled off a number that didn't match the one he'd dialed, hoping Jason would remember protocol.

"Quit fucking around, Rick." And then, "Oh, shit. Right. I gotcha."

Rick said nothing. After a moment, Jason said,

"Did you pick up the merchandise?"

Rick pulled his attention away from the phone, caught himself starting to drift onto the shoulder. He shook his head at no one and just laughed. The stupid fucker thought the pause in the conversation would be sufficient to remove any mix-up regarding its underlying meaning, nullify what had been said before it. As he worked to move past his incredulity at his Jason's boundless stupidity, he looked ahead and noticed a gas station looming in the distance. One of those large, trucker-oriented facilities that also catered to weary car-bound travelers. Rick slowed, pulled into the station, and parked.

"You still there?" Jason asked. Impatience again, this time salted with more than a little arrogance.

"Yeah."

"You're gonna hafta go again tomorrow. You ready for that?"

"Of course. Why wouldn't I be?"

"Just wanna make sure you can stomach it, smalls."

Rick looked out the windshield of the car. A family of three was just exiting the store. A small kid, about four, toddled next to his dad, one hand clutching the man's pant-leg, the other tightly grasping a candy bar. They looked happy. Normal, middle-class family out for an afternoon. He remembered how horribly boring that lifestyle had seemed to him when he was a young man. He now caught himself at times longing for it. Longing for the stability. One more job. He'd get the money, split, and start over. He gave the family one more look, then returned his attention to the phone.

"Hey, where you staying tonight?"

"Laying low. Motel Six. Out of town a bit. Why?"

"Wanna have a little fun?"

"Whatcha got in mind?" Jason seemed to perk up, dropped the attitude.

"Was thinking about picking up some beers, grabbing some girls, and heading out to the desert. How's that sound?"

"Hell yeah!"

"Good. You in the Six north of town?"

"Yep."

"What room?"

"303."

"Pick you up a little after eight."

Rick slapped his phone shut and placed it in the car's center console. He then reached under his seat and removed a 9mm Beretta he'd stowed there earlier. He wasn't supposed to pack during a pick-up, but that was one rule he couldn't go along with. No way he was going to boost a car, troll a seedy neighborhood full of paranoid illegals, and execute an abduction of a little girl unless he had protection. To do otherwise would be crazy. He ejected the clip and inspected it. It was full. Of course he knew that because he'd topped it earlier. But there's a rule you follow regarding guns and life-threatening situations: check your shit two times to make sure. Once you're sure, check it again. He slapped the clip back in, chambered a round, and clicked the safety on.

He started the car, pulled back onto the 215, heading toward San Bernardino. The plan was simple: he'd pick up Jason, take him to the desert, shoot the fucker between the eyes, then head up to Victorville. The next day, with any luck, he'd grab the girl, make the transfer himself, and complete the delivery. He'd tell the boss Jason had issues with the operation, was complaining about the money. He'd tell him Jason was going to go to the cops unless he got a better deal. He'd tell him he'd had no choice but to kill the man. If everything worked out all right, he'd get Jason's share for the delivery, maybe even something for the kill.
