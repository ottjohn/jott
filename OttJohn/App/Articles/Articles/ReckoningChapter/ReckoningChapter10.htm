Chapter 10

 

 

Clickity clack, clack, clack! Snap, snap! Clickity clack, clack! Tobias busied himself entering notes into his PDA, notes he felt warranted further scrutiny than a single meeting could provide, notwithstanding its almost intolerable length. He turned, faced the whiteboard he used to sketch out the partial profile of Hassenback's assailant, and snapped a couple of pictures. He hammered in a few more notes and then turned to Sanchez, who was sitting in the same chair he'd occupied for the entire duration of the meeting.

"It would appear LAPD HQ has a different take on the meaning of an hour than we do, what do you think?"

Sanchez gave him a droopy nod, said, "Yep. Most definitely more than sixty minutes anyway. So what's next?"

"Well, I suppose we're going to continue with the profiling piece; then I'm hoping we'll move on to the contents of the thumb drive."

"So what's all this business with breaking the profiling into three pieces?" He asked. "I don't get it. Why not just get everything out and be done with it?"

"It's all about scope and probabilities, kid. We start with what we're confident of first, to get a good, high probability foundational picture of our perp. And as you could tell from the discussions we had, there's not a whole lot of agreement among us about what actually is a certainty with respect to describing the guy we're after."

Sanchez though about that for a moment, then said, "Well, none of this stuff's for certain anyway, right?"

"No, none of it is," said Tobias. He rummaged through the bits and pieces he'd remembered from what he'd read years ago, then said,

"We break up the profile into three pieces because there are three classes of assumptions we formulate when profiling a suspect. The first class has the highest confidence, the third the lowest. Each class is defined by assumptions that have comparable accuracies, or probabilities. So, for example, in our first class, we have to be highly confident of each of the assumptions we make about the suspect; in the third class, the confidence of the assumptions diminish."

"Okay," said Sanchez, "then why do the probabilities diminish? It seems to me that as we build out our description of our suspect, the probability we're correct and therefore going to find him should increase as well, right?"

"Not necessarily, but that is a common misconception. Consider this example: let's say we start with a profile of a suspect that simply states the assailant is a man. In that case, the probability we're correct in our assumption is very high, right?"

"Yeah, I mean, women engage in this type of crime, but it's rare."

"Right. But even though our simple profile has an extremely high probability of being correct, it's not very helpful. A profile like that narrows the scope of the search to probably three million men in the LA area. Now let's add to our assumption list. Let's assume our assailant is six foot four and 240 pounds. We now have a more useful profile, but what do you think that does to its accuracy?"

"It's going to diminish it?"

"You're right. Why?"

Sanchez looked up to the ceiling, rubbed the stubble on his chin and thought for a moment. "Okay, I'll use that damn statistics class you made me take last year to illustrate: if I flip a single coin, the probability it will land on heads is fifty percent, same for tails. So if we're asked to guess the outcome of a single flip of a coin, we'll be fifty percent certain it'll  be heads. This is kind of like the first assumption we applied to the suspect, that the sex is male. Having only this attribute gives us very high confidence that we're right. But it doesn't help us much."

Tobias nodded; Sanchez continued.

Now, if I decide to flip two coins, and I'm asked to guess the outcome of both flips, then I'll be twenty five percent sure both will wind up being heads."

"Why's that?" Asked Tobias.

Sanchez looked up at the ceiling again, reached back in his mind, looked at Tobias, satisfied with what he found. "Because there're only four outcomes for such a scenario: tails-tails, tails-heads, heads-tails, and heads-heads. Each of those events has the same probability of occurring, and if we're looking for the heads-heads outcome, the probability is one in four. So to extend the analogy, when we add an attribute to the suspect's profile, we have a more accurate picture of him, but the probability our model is correct has to diminish."

Tobias nodded, then said, "In a purely random world, yes. But we're also talking about categorical probability distributions, and while the probability seems small, compared to another category based on a different set of descriptions, it's actually relatively high."

Sanchez said, "So compared to the category that contains nothing but midgets with club feet, our category is most likely the correct one."

Tobias nodded, said, "Yes. We're looking for the most likely category of potential offenders. That's the simplest and least useful explanation of profiling you're going to get. It's simply the application of statistical and psychological analysis to a crime to shed light on an unknown perp. It's far from a perfect science, but sometimes the margin of error can be staggeringly small."

Sanchez paused, appeared to be deep in thought, then said, "So there's another reason for keeping the three classes of assumptions separate, right?"

"Yep. And you're going to tell me what that is."

"My guess is so we have three different sets of search criteria, working from the most specific to the least. That way, if we miss our suspect with our most specific search, we can fall back on the middle class and leverage it."

Just as Tobias was going to give Sanchez another thumbs up, Jacobsen and Kabinski walked back in the meeting room.

 

As everyone settled in, Jacobsen leaned over the table and pressed a green button on the console in front of him to activate the camera, and just that quickly the agreeable Agent Rathman was back in the room as well. Jacobsen then removed a black marker from his briefcase, threw it to Kabinski, and said,

"Your turn."

Kabinski smiled, got up from his chair, his slacks showing no sign of wrinkles or creases that would indicate he'd been wearing them for the last ten hours.

"Okay," he said, "I think we've made a very good start." He rested his fingertips on the table, looked around the room, and then continued. "While we were at dinner, I tossed some ideas at Jacobsen here, and I think we came up with some assumptions that might help us to quickly flesh out the final part of our suspect profile. Any objections to me going over those first?"

The room remained silent.

"Good. Well, I've been thinking about our assumption the assailant is completely on his own. I agree with that assessment, but I also think there's more to it, and I think the crime scene bears that out. Specifically, it could be a primary driver in his behavior. We already know the assailant is highly organized and smart. Obviously, the nature of the crime and our surrounding assumptions regarding his motives means he once had a family."

"He has no family now," said Tobias. "What you're getting at is this guy really is alone in the world, which means there are no survivors at all, nothing left to keep him grounded in the present so he can plan for the future."

Kabiniski regarded Tobias with a smile, said. "you've been thinking about this too. Yes, based on the nature of the attack on Hassenback, I think we can conclude either the wife is deceased, along with any other family members who matter, or she left him, completely disappearing from his life."

"Very good, gentleman," said Rathman. "Such a condition would free up any uncertainty about his actions, no counter-balance to his need for revenge. An otherwise lucid man would still have doubts about taking such action, would care about what surviving family members would think of what he's doing. Without any survivors, though, he's free of those constraints. In other words, he's got nothing to lose and no one to disappoint."

Kabinski nodded his head in agreement, then said, "In this case, I'm leaning toward a deceased spouse. If she were alive, I think the scene would've had more contamination, formed more out of aggression than the surgical strike it was."

Rathman nodded his head aggressively, betraying an enthusiasm that showed he was definitely now in his element. He continued,

"Right. There was only one indication he lost his cool at the scene, that being the body of Hassenback itself. As you pointed out, his remaining activities reflected a methodical purpose that was definitely lacking the spillover rage that would have more than likely been evident had this crime been driven partially as an effort to send a message to his wife."

"Okay, so we have agreement about this point?" Kabinski asked.

There were nods of agreement all around the table and on the wall. Kabinski turned and wrote,

Wife deceased. Singularity a driver in activity. Nothing to lose.

Rathman looked into the room like animated wall art, carrying a demeanor of a person who was just bursting to talk. He pressed a meaty finger onto the center of his glasses to push them back up his nose and said, "Okay, so what else you got, Kabinski?"

"Smart guy. Family man. Smart family men are generally fairly successful. Fairly successful family men typically have houses. I think we can assume, then, that our man was a homeowner. The big question, though, is does his still own his house? I think we get much better than even odds this guy sold his house and is now using the equity to finance his mission."

"I keep hearing the word mission," scoffed Rathman. "You people are making it sound as though this guy is conducting some king of renegade military operation!"

Sanchez gave the wall a look of irritation, then said, "It fits the criteria, Rathman. This guy has a strategy: he plans, he executes, and he will continue to cycle through planning and executing until he gets what he's after. Hell, he's even leaving a wake of collateral damage, though some might argue that to be more of a public service."

Everyone at the table stared at Sanchez, Tobias in particular giving him a disappointed shake of his head.

"What? Can't a lowly sergeant have an opinion?" Sanchez blurted, then said, "Look, I didn't really mean the public service thing. I'm sure repeat offender pedophiles have every right to live productive …"

"What Kabinski said makes sense," interrupted Tobias, returning the conversation to a more productive peal while preventing Sanchez from digging a hole he wouldn't be able to get out of. "If he's engaged in serial activity to collect information, then that activity could take him all over the country, perhaps even the world. Someone that dedicated to a mission isn't going to have time to work, and he's going to need money from somewhere. Equity cashed out of a house is a really good way to bankroll something like that."

Kabinski nodded, continued. "The next obvious question, then, is how he's spending his money. He's already demonstrated his skill for not contaminating crime scenes, so it probably makes sense he's not going to leave a money trail, either. I highly doubt, then, he's living a life of luxury on the road, staying in high-end hotels. My bet is he's staying in very cheap motels, the types that take cash and don't ask questions. My bet is our suspect probably operates exclusively on a cash-only basis."

Kabinski again looked around the room, then said, "So do we have any objections to adding the assumption this guy has money but only spends cash?"

Again, there were no objections, so again Kabinski turned to the board and wrote.

Has money. Works with cash only. Stays in inexpensive motels.

"That's about all Jacobsen and I got to over dinner," said Kabinski. "Anyone got anything else to add?"

"I have a bit more," offered Rathman. "Let's talk about the intelligence factor a little more, because I think it might uncover other things. For instance, highly organized persons who commit violent crimes, especially those who are as efficient as our guy, tend to have some education. I would bet our guy has at least a four year degree. In fact, I would bet his degree is in a hard science."

Rathman peered into the room, said, "Any problem with that assessment? It builds into something else."

No reply, just nods all around.

"To use the word we've been bandying about," Continued Rathman, his image appearing to look directly at Sanchez, "this appears to be a very mission-driven man. This isn't an attribute that spilled out of him overnight. I would suggest our perp was a head-to-book sort of guy when he was younger, mission driven all the way through college. I think it's probably a characteristic he's carried around with him for his whole life. He probably hangs on to things like a Pit Bull. In fact, I'd bet he spent the first couple of years after college focusing on getting a good job, then perhaps even another couple of years more to acclimate to it. Only after that would a guy like this even consider injecting himself into the social scene to find a good woman, get married, have kids, yada, yada. Given he's a meticulous guy, though, I would also wager he waited at least a couple of years to have his first and only child. Now, taking into consideration the average age range for victims of sexual crimes is between four and 12 years old, I think we can safely assume the guy we're looking for was somewhere between the ages of  32 and 40 at the time of his child's abduction."

Rathman paused, presumably to allow everyone else to catch up with his slick thinking, then continued. "This is all very shaky, and there's one more likely scenario to add to the confusion: there's also a good chance the guy we're after did a stint in the military, four years, honorable discharge. Therefore, the likely age of our suspect could be somewhere in the neighborhood of forty-four years old."

Rathman looked around the room, appeared content his synopsis had topped of the profile of the perp.

"Bump it up at least a year," said sanchez.

"And why's that?" Asked Rathman, his face suddenly appearing large and flushed on the wall.

"Stands to reason," answered Sanchez. "The wife more than likely didn't die on the day of the abduction, and he's not going to engage in his mission on the day of her death. He needs a cooling off period. To say otherwise would contradict much of what we've already assumed about him. To strike right after these events would demand he be driven by rage, and this was not the case. Bump it up at least a year to accommodate for the deceased wife and the cooling off period."

Tobias gave Sanchez a nod and a look that conveyed the message he'd made up for his earlier transgression. Kabinski verbalized what Tobias seemed to be silently communicating,

"Damn good observation, Sanchez." He then went to the board and wrote,

College educated. Probably Hard science. Probable stint in the military. Age between 33 and 45.

"I'm not done," said Rathman, a note of irritation still ringing in his voice. "Let's go back to the size of this fellow. He's a big guy, right? And he's definitely on the road a lot. He spends a whole lot of time in his vehicle. Big guy like that's not going to be comfortable in a small or even medium-sized car. In fact, I would bet he uses his vehicle as his base of operations occasionally, so he needs something that will accommodate him, give him some room. Given his size and his road-based activities, I would put him in either a large SUV or a truck. Further, it'll be a very low-key vehicle. If he's a smart guy, he's not going to want a lot of attention, so my money's on a vehicle that's at least six or seven years old. Nothing fancy."

"Good point," said Kabinski. There appeared to be no argument, so he went to the board and wrote,

Owns an SUV or a truck. At least six or seven years old.

Kabinski turned back toward the camera that fed his image to Rathman, asked, "are you done now?"

"If I had a couple of days, I could come up with more I'm sure. But for now, that's about all I got," he replied.

"Tobias?"

Tobias looked around the table and said, "everybody's so cooperative this evening. What's going on?"

"I think we're all a bit tired," offered Jacobsen.

Everyone turned to look at the lieutenant, as if each was contemplating what sort of brain processing went behind a decision to answer a rhetorical question.

"Well, I'll make this quick," said Tobias. "There's only one more thing I can think of: he went out of his way to take the media. This tells me he knows what to do with it. I'm certainly no expert in this, but I get the feeling if a guy takes storage media for the purpose of interrogation, then he has to be at the very least connected to an IT field in some way. This just isn't something your average schmuck is capable of. I'm not sure what his degree was in, but I'm pretty confident we'll find he actually works in the field in some capacity."

Rathman arched his eyebrows, his glasses using the opportunity to once again slip down his nose. "Very good, Detective. Write that one up on the board, Kabinski."

Kabinski turned and wrote,

Connected to the IT field.

"I think that's probably good enough to start a three-level search," said Rathman. "What do we have left?"

Jacobsen motioned Kabinski to take his seat, leaned over the side of his chair, picked up manila envelope, and said,

"We have some relevant material from the thumb drive."

Everyone groaned.

Clark stormed into his sleazy weekly, turned to close and lock the door, then faced the table on which he'd left his laptop. He'd left the restaurant overcome by a mixture of sorrow and rage. By the time he'd reached the motel, most of that was replaced by regret over the abruptness of his departure. At the beginning of the hour, all he'd wanted was to get away, to get back to work. But as Carri continued to talk, to share her ideas and perspectives, she started to transform from some stranger for whom he'd done a favor, to a very pretty, smart woman who was beginning to reel him into her world. He thought her passion was remarkable, and she had, throughout the entire hour, made him feel as though he was the only person in her world. Carri deserved better than the exit he gave her, for sure. He would have to find a way to make it up to her somehow. But his departure was unavoidable, no way he could have changed that. She took out that damn credit card, and embossed on its surface was a family-friendly image of a teddy bear.

It looked a lot like the one he'd given his daughter years earlier. That by itself would have been enough to peel back the scab that had barely begun to form around the wound of an obliterated family. But he could have endured that. He could have swallowed it whole and made an impression of a friendly goodbye, a final nice-to-have-met-you he knew Carri deserved. The image, however, had caused some synapses in his brain to fire, to reach for associations and build context around what he saw; and as the branching electrochemical circuitry went to work, it quickly assembled three things in his mind: daughter's teddy bear, family-oriented images, and bulleted list. The strength and relevance of the association was so shockingly powerful, it was as if Clark had been cold-cocked right there in the restaurant by Mike Tyson himself.

Clark sat at the table, flipped open his laptop, and fired it up. He launched his web browser and loaded the website he'd been looking at only a couple of hours earlier. He quickly scanned the bottom of the page, noticed there were only two teddy bears bulleting its various marketing points. The bear he was looking for was in the first column, second row. He right-clicked on the image and saved it to a working folder, then opened it up with a free Microsoft imaging software package and enlarged it to 400% of its original size. A grossly pixilated image stared back at him, nothing more than a blurry presentation of colored squares. He scrutinized every detail: the left eye appeared to have a shadow underneath it, betraying damage done by a feisty little terrier. The right breast expressed a blurry yellow ribbon, put there by a deceased aunt who had lost her only son in Iraq. The neck presented a barely perceptible, thin gold chain. Below it dangled a small, red heart. The necklace had been put there by a loving father.

He continued to stare at the image, once again overcome by the mixture of anger and sorrow that had assaulted him in the restaurant. His emotional state now, however, was seasoned with a prodigious amount of disbelief and the kind of satisfaction that can only be delivered by pure dumb luck. Or was it luck? For the past couple of years, he'd had worked tirelessly searching for a vector, a direction toward resolution. Until now, he hadn't had much relief. He could always find a direction, but it was always just a bit off, taking him on unproductive tangents until he found himself circling around to try again.

But this was different. He allowed himself the briefest of smiles. This was different. What he'd found, beyond any shadow of doubt, would provide him with a true direction, the vector he'd been looking for. There could be no other conclusion: the left eye of the teddy bear had been partially torn off by the family dog, a terrier named Baxter; the aunt who had placed the yellow ribbon on its right breast was his dead wife's sister; and he'd placed the necklace around the bear's neck himself. Any one of observations alone would have given him pause, would have brought up doubt about the veracity of the find. But together, the probability he was wrong was staggeringly small.

Clark closed his browser window, closed the image of the teddy bear he'd given to his daughter years before, its grossly pixilated image disappearing from his sight but not from his mind. He shut down his machine and closed the lid. He felt sick. The light from the lone incandescent bulb that hung above him cast its meager brightness over the table, which dutifully reflected most of it back as a hazy glare that seemed to dig into his head and press against the back of his eyes. Besides the little cocoon of light and glare in which he was working, the room held no other illumination. It was as if the environment surrounding him was really the gaping maw of a hungry black hole, greedily devouring any light that dared moved too close to its well-defined boundary. His head throbbed. He got up from his rickety chair, took the three steps to the dresser, found pain relief in a bottle, and took three. Headache or no, he had work to do.

He stepped to a white mini-fridge parked on the floor next to the dresser, positioned to double as makeshift counter space. He opened it and removed a bottle of beer. He twisted off its top, took another three steps to the room's ratty bed, propped up his pillows, and sat down with his legs outstretched in front of him. He had no problem sleeping under or on top of sheets that had been washed and then used by an almost countless number of strangers. Pillows, on the other hand, were an entirely different matter to him: too many holes in the human head out of which fluid could drip or secrete, and a pillow soiled and polluted by strangers was the last thing he wanted to lay his head on at night. So the pillows were his.

Clark took a swig of beer; it spilled pleasantly into his mostly empty stomach. Then he stared at the ceiling, trying to banish the image of the bear, attempting to focus on his next best steps. The combination of the alcohol and the pain reliever were beginning to take effect; his head was no longer throbbing. But the bear persisted. Whoever had built the site had been smart. But not smart enough. Whoever had built the site had decided it would be clever to incorporate thumbnail images of trophies taken from their young victims into their confabulated marketing piece, a little shared secret for their members to marvel over, a list they could aspire to contribute to. Whoever had built the site knew the odds of anyone surfing to it and recognizing any of the thumbnails were small to say the least, infinitesimal even. After all, the site had no domain name, so how could anyone just accidentally surf to it? Clark lifted his beer to his lips and took a long pull. The alcohol washed through his system, gave him the feeling of happy isolation and heightened focus. Time to get back to work. As he swung his feet off the bed to head toward the table, he heard three tentative raps at his door.

An electric shock of surprise flew through his body, the voltage of which spiked his heart rate and temporarily made his breathing ragged. His mind raced, millions of thoughts seemingly racing about all at once, each demanding scrutiny. Clark burst out of bed, yelled "Just a moment!" He crossed the tiny room to his laptop, frantically stuffed it and the rest of the paperwork lying on the table into his briefcase, returned to the bed, and slid it underneath. How could the police have caught up with him so fast? Who else could it be? He'd told no one where he was staying, really had no one to tell.

Then he forced himself to slow down, took control of his heartbeat and breathing. He thought about it. Even in his pessimistic mind, the odds the police were at his door were very low: huge city, very little evidence left behind, perhaps a rough physical description based on the condition of the body. It didn't add up. He thought a little more, concentrated on the knock. It had been tentative. There had been no declaration from behind the door that it had in fact been the police. And the police would not give three soft raps on the door in the first place, right? More than likely, this was nothing more than a visit from one of his neighbors or the management. Satisfied he was in no immediate peril, he stepped to the door, unlocked it, and opened it just a crack, instinctively placing a foot behind it to keep the visitor out.

 

Clark looked through the opening in the door, a small vertical crack about an inch and a half wide, saw sparkling azure eyes beam back at him.

"How ..."

"Did I find you?" She finished.

"Yeah," Clark stammered. How had she found him? He'd left her at the restaurant over two hours ago.

"I followed you, Joe! How else would I know where you live?" The final word was inflected, not intentionally, with little repugnance.

"Oh," Clark answered, his head awash with that far away feeling from having been caught off guard, his speech slowed and almost slurred. He looked at her. She smiled. Then he noticed she had two large paper bags in her arms. The molasses in his head thinned.

"Forgive me," he said, smiled. "You caught me a bit off guard." That was the understatement of the century. He'd let his preoccupation, his revelry in shock and anger soak into him so much he'd not been careful at all on his return trip to the motel. Dangerous, stupid, and he couldn't let it happen again.

"Please, Carri, come in." Clark stepped back and let Carri pass through the door. She walked directly to the small table and placed both paper bags in a spot that had held his laptop and some paperwork just a minute before. She then turned to face him.

He said, "you shouldn't have followed me. This isn't exactly a safe neighborhood, you know."

"I .." She paused, looked down, staring at her feet. Her blue and white dress clung to her body in all the right places, her hair tousled around her shoulders, her hands crossed in front of her, fingers interlaced.

"I'm so sorry, Joe," she looked up, her face now wearing a worn look, her eyes just beginning to brim with wetness. "You're right. I have no business here, no right to follow you. I am so sorry for the intrusion, really. I just …" She looked around, taking in the depravity, shocked by it. "The bags on the table are groceries. They're for you, Joe. Please forgive me." The last sentence was barely choked out as Carri rushed by Clark and reached for the door.

"Wait," Clark said as he grabbed her arm. Carri stopped, said nothing.

"You bought me groceries?" He almost laughed out. "Why?" But he already knew the answer. He let go of her arm, and she turned to face him again, eyes now brimming over.

"Oh, Joe, you left in such a hurry." She used both hands to smooth back her hair, which was beginning to clutter about her face. "Obviously, something happened at the restaurant, something went wrong. I had to find out what, so I immediately hatched a plan to follow you home. About 45 minutes later, you parked here. I thought you were perhaps visiting someone, but when you didn't come out, I concluded you lived here." Again the unintentional inflection of disgust.

"And you figured me for some displaced soul in need of food and supplies," he deadpanned. She looked at him, then away, her face flushed with shame.

"I'm sorry, Joe!" The tears began streaming down her face now, leaving delicate paths of purplish blue. "It's not that I think you're a bum, Joe. You lost everything, though, didn't you? You lost your job, your possessions, and from the looks of it, your family too! Oh, Joe, I'm so sorry! I didn't mean to make you feel like a charity case; I was only trying to help!" She looked down again, trying to choke back sobs.

Clark burst out laughing. He couldn't think of anything else to do. She was so damn close to the truth! What was his other option? Join her in a tear fest?

Carri stood back, let out with a partial laugh choked back by a sob. She raised her right hand and slapped it on Clark's left shoulder.

"What's so funny?" She asked, wiping her eyes with her free hand.

"In this dark, cold ocean populated with so many self-centered, uncompassionate creatures, how the hell did I get so lucky as to meet Mother Teresa herself?"

She stifled another sob and joined in his laughter. "I could say the same thing about you, Joe!"

Clark cringed at the name, then made a decision he hoped he wouldn't later regret.

"Jack," he said.

"What?"

"My name, Carri, isn't Joe. It's Jack. I'm sorry for the lie. But I didn't think it would hurt at the time because I didn't think I'd ever see you again. And I didn't really want my real name given in case the police showed up at the Rusty Pelican."

"That's okay," she said, removing her hand from his chest and employing it to finish wiping her face clean. "I completely understand." She averted her eyes slightly, indicating that perhaps she didn't. She looked around, took in the entire room for the first time. She looked again at Clark, at his eyes.

"What happed tonight? What made you leave so quickly?"

Clark gave the question a moment's consideration, decided a half-truth couldn't hurt much.

"The image on your credit card, the bear; I gave my daughter a bear a lot like it when she was four years old." He stopped, aware the memory alone was enough push him toward a place he didn't want to go. He looked down, averted his eyes from hers.

"Oh," she said. "How long has it been since you've seen her?"

"Two years," he replied. His voice involuntarily cracked, memories began flooding into his consciousness, taking him back to a time when he was a solid man and not the jagged, vengeful shell of what he'd once been. He felt himself slipping, falling into the past. He fought it.

"Jack?" She asked softly, taking one of his big paws in both of her hands. "What's her name?"

"Was," he replied slowly, painfully. "What was her name. Jessica. Her name was Jessica."

Suddenly Clark was flooded with a memory of the day he'd given his daughter the bear. It had been Easter, and she was four years old. He and his wife, Stacy, had taken her to the park for an Easter egg hunt. The weather was cooperative, the sun casting copious amounts of sunshine into the crisp spring air. The other children at the hunt, however, seemed to have no notion of fair play. As it turned out, Jessica was much smaller than all of the other kids. She had scrambled toward the obvious eggs cast about all over the park, but, each time, before she could get to them, a larger, faster child would swoop in, retrieve the egg, and move on. After about four incidents like that, Jessica had given up, wandering back to Stacy, crying and just wanting to go home.

At the time, they had lived only about a block south of the park, so Clark had Stacy and Jessica return home. He remembered giving his daughter a hug and watching her and her mother walk toward the house. He then turned and walked to a small mall that serviced the town in which they lived, a community of about 40,000. He found an open specialty shop, bought the stuffed bear and a basket full of the best candy he could buy. He then walked home, stashing both bear and basket behind a large acorn tree in the back yard. The presents hidden, Clark stepped in through the rear sliding glass door of his house, crept up behind Jessica, and wrapped his arms around her with a big hug that only dads can give. She squealed with shock and delight.

"Sometimes the Easter Bunny visits houses, too, you know," he whispered to her.

"Really?" She asked. He remembered how her eyes had flown open wide at the news. "But what if the other kids come and get it before I get there?" She looked down, as if she were about to cry again.

"Won't happen, sweetie. Why don't you and mommy take a walk around the backyard and see if he's been here?"

"Ok! Mom!" She yelled. Stacy had run to her, grabbed her hand, and walked out the sliding glass door. Clark turned to pour himself a cup of coffee, a broad smile appearing on his face as the shrill squeal from the backyard announced Jessica had found her treasure. They both returned to the house running, Stacy holding the basket and Jessica the bear. Clark remembered his daughter had continued to run through the rear glass door, arms spread wide in her pick-me-up approach, her new bear bouncing and flailing in her tight little grasp. Clark bent over and lifted Jessica high into the air, settling her off-center from his body so her head rested right in the side of his neck.

"I love you, daddy!" She said.

Stacy came in from the other side and wrapped her hands around them both.

"I love you, too, Jack." She then nudged in, close to his ear. "You're a hell of a father, you know that?" She choked.

"Jack?" Carri watched Clark, eyes cast to the floor, shoulders beginning to heave slightly. He said nothing.

"Oh God, Jack. I'm so sorry."

Carri took his hand and led him to the bed. Clark didn't resist. He let her take his shoes off, then his shirt. He then lay down, facing away from her in a fetal position. Carri returned to the door and locked it. She then turned the light off, removed her shoes, and planted herself right behind him, wrapping her arms around him. For the next couple of hours, she held him, feeling his body heave, the ragged breathing of a tortured soul letting go a torrent of pain.
