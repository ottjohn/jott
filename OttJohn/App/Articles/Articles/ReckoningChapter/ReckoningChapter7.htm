Chapter 7

 

 

Tobias and Sanchez pulled up to the Hassenback residence, a now forbidding-looking structure on the 1100 block of East Wilshire Avenue. Police tape still decorated the front lawn, marking now dilapidated stay-away zones. Flapping and twirling in a light breeze, the tape whistled as the wind caught it. Under most other circumstances, it wouldn't have been an altogether unpleasant sound, but under the present context, it drew moribund attention to the scene and served as a reminder of the recent residential mayhem with its restless, plastic cacophony. Tobias checked his watch, it was 7:15. He really wanted to make this quick, so he could get back to his little party at home. He wanted another burger. And another glass of wine. He wanted to visit with his family. He wanted to visit with his granddaughter. All he had to do was try to remember what it was about a light switch that was so important to him when taken in context with the crime scene.

"So are you going to tell me why we left perfectly good burgers and booze behind so we can go swimming around a crime scene, or am I going to have to guess?" Sanchez asked, only half jokingly.

Tobias looked at Sanchez, who was wearing an expectant expression on his face. The kid had started tagging along with him during his free time a couple of years ago. Not because Tobias was a really great guy, but because of his solve rate and his interrogation methodologies. Truth be told, Tobias had no idea how things came to him at times. They just did. He figured the main reason for his success was that he didn't force himself on crime scenes. He let them come to him. And that removed the frustration that can sometimes snag investigations. If you're not frustrated, you work with a clear head. This time, he had only a partial answer. He knew what he needed to look for, but he didn't know why. Yet.

"Tell the truth, kid, I'm not really sure myself. All I know is we need to look at all the light switches in the house." Tobias pointed at his head with his right index finger. "It's right there, you know, like the name of someone you're meeting again for like the second time. You're trying to remember the name through some stupid association game, but you can't. It's back there in my head like that. So all I know is light switches. That's the link. Hell, it may not even be a light switch, but I think looking at them will take us where we need to go."

Tobias took his official copy of the key to the residence, opened the door, and stepped in.

"Hey, boss?" Sanchez lagged behind a little.

Tobias turned. Sanchez's face had contorted just a little. His eyebrows leveled over his eyes under a scrunched forehead, as if he were thinking about something but not sure he wanted to share. He saw the same expression on his daughter's face probably a million or so times, in his most conservative estimation.

"What's bothering you, kid?"

"Do you really want this guy to go down? I mean, in a perfect world, would we even care what this guy did?"

"Well, one, it's not a perfect world. Two, if there's evidence we overlooked, we need to do everything we can to find it, notwithstanding how things might be in your hypothetically perfect world."

"Yeah, I know." Sanchez's voice trailed off. He went silent, looked a little deflated. Tobias knew what was on his mind. He'd been there dozens of times before.

"Look, son, we all have to do things in life we really don't want to. In this case, the job description is getting in the way of an ideology. But this is what we do. To tell you the truth, I don't like it any more than you do. But whether this guy walks isn't our decision. Our job is to find him and let the lawyers, judges, and juries ferret out justice. You understand?"

"Yeah. It's our job. And I love my job, don't get me wrong. I love it a hell of a lot more than the pleasure I get out of seeing a guy like Hassenback go down."

Tobias was unfazed. He understood. All new cops go through the same process of ideological reconciliation with their job description. Sanchez was a good cop, and he knew he would make the adjustment and continue on to make a fine detective one day. But he still felt compelled to bring Sanchez back to the task at hand, to snap him back with an obvious argument.

"Hey, if we let this guy go, we would have two major problems: one, we'd be operating within an ad hoc justice system, and justice that operates on whimsy is not a place we want to be for obvious reasons; two, it opens the door for vigilantism. That's just as bad as ad hoc justice. In fact, it is ad hoc justice. That would lead to anarchy and chaos. There would be a breakdown in the social system we worked so hard to forge. Then before you know it, really bad things would start to happen: dogs might become vegetarians, Paris Hilton might be elected president, that sort of thing."

Sanchez smiled. "Vegetarian dogs. You're a mad man."

Tobias said nothing.

"I believe in what I do, boss, but sometimes ... What kind of monster watches that shit?"

"Don't remind me. And, yeah, it keeps me up at night too. And you'll never get used to that part of it. At least I hope you don't. It's just something you have to try to work through. Now, you gonna be okay?"

"Affirmative." Sanchez relaxed, gave Tobias a sheepish grin.

"You take the back part of the house and I'll take the front. We'll meet in the middle," Tobias directed.

Sanchez said nothing, just turned and worked his way toward the rear of the house, where the bedrooms, office, and bathroom were placed around the short hallway. Tobias loitered in the living room and began inspecting the wall switches there. They were nestled snugly in their plates, which themselves were screwed tight into the walls. He flipped a switch. A light came on. He flipped it again. The light went off. It couldn't have been any of the fixtures or any of the ceiling-mounted lights. All of those had been searched by the assailant. So what was it about the light switches? Tobias again looked at a plate. Then he looked at the screws that mounted the plate to the wall. Then he got it. He was about to call out to Sanchez when he heard,

"Think I found what you're looking for!"

Tobias walked into the hallway and took an immediate left into the sparsely-equipped office. It was the closest room to the front of the house, and it had been the very same room in which he'd had found discovery oversights in his first walkthrough. He noted to himself he'd need to schedule a little pep talk with the tech who worked the room.

"I can't believe I didn't see this," Sanchez said.

"Wouldn't worry about it too much, kid. Looks like the techs missed it too. And me."

Tobias looked carefully at the wall-mounted plate. It didn't provide a face for light switches at all, and that's what was giving him problems. It was just a plate, a rectangular piece of plastic used to cover a spot where a light switch had been. In Tobias's experience, such plates were put in place and then left alone, either until the old switch was resurrected or until the wall and plate were painted over. Neither of these things had happened to this one. Instead, unlike any of the other plates in the house, it bore the scars of a few scratches made by what had to have been a standard screw driver. The tops of the screws used to mount the plate to the wall showed heavy wear: the protective coating over them was almost completely gone and the grooves in the tops of the screws were worn. Apparently, Hassenback had some reason for removing and replacing this particular plate a lot.

"Odds?" Tobias queried Sanchez.

"Well," Sanchez replied, "I wouldn't wager an entire month's pay on it, but I bet there's something back there."

"Yeah, unless Hassenback's final guest beat us to it."

Sanchez said nothing.

"Well, let's get our gear and find out."

Tobias and Sanchez exited the house, retrieved a small standard screwdriver, gloves, and an evidence bag from the car. They returned to the room, put on their gloves, and removed the screws that mounted the little vanilla-colored plate. Using the flat end of the screw driver, they carefully levered the plate from the wall, then stared into the partially-lighted cavity it had covered. There, inside the wall, tucked within a plastic sandwich bag, was a metallic thumb drive.

Clark worked deep into the early morning hours reviewing returned files from his first set of search parameters. He'd learned through trial and error that his most effective searches were not a product of what one might expect: yes, using key words that were raw and described accurately what went through the minds of sexual predators yielded prolific results, but they were also mostly bound up in twisted fantasy and rarely provided directional vectors to real information. Further, many pedos who employ salacious verbiage wrought with graphic sexual descriptions tend to broadcast that behavior into the online world, which puts them at high risk for electronic surveillance by state and federal authorities; so targeting that demographic fell outside the bounds of what Clark considered safe. The smarter demographic, on the other hand, skirts such phrasing in favor of safer communication invariably planted within exchanges and even careful marketing blasts about anything from financial opportunities to reminders about bake sales.

At a very basic level, there are two types of predators that systematically hunt for children: individuals who operate alone, prowling favorite chat rooms and social web sites to groom victims for eventual meetings; and highly-organized groups that use tactics ranging from abduction to drug addiction to meet the needs of the world's ravenous sex trade industry. Clark didn't have a lot of use for the loners. He was after bigger game. It was the organized manifestation of the industry that had obliterated his family, so he'd keyed on that from the start: every target he'd selected over the previous two years had a past connected in some way to organized sex trade involving preteen adolescents. While his last target, Hassenback, had been incarcerated for the molestation of a minor under the age of 12, and mostly fit the profile of a lone pedophile who utilized grooming and confidence to prey on his victims, the evidence brought against him included allegations of trips to Thailand, a country notorious for sex crimes against children.

During her short life, his daughter at no time went anywhere near Thailand. In the end, she was found outside of San Bernardino. Alone. In the hot desert sun. Picked apart by animals and partially mummified by the dry, unforgiving air. The autopsy had confirmed she'd been a victim of repeated rapes over the span of six months. The blood work had been at once brutal and revealing: she'd contracted several sexually transmitted diseases, the number and diversity of which pointed to one sickening conclusion: she'd been the sexual object of numerous, unknown male subjects. She was used up, and then she was dumped in the desert. Alive. So what do you do? Find a pedo who knows how to operate within one organization, such as any of those running in Thailand, and that might lead you to another that operates locally. That's all he needed. That's all he thought about.

It was 2:30 in the morning. Clark had been looking over Hassenback's documents for several hours and he was getting tired. He stood and stretched. He looked down at the small, round table at which he'd been working. His laptop marked an item in a list indicating the next document he'd read. The blue highlight stood in stark contrast to the black text and white background. The keyboard glistened under the harshness of the white light bursting from a bare incandescent bulb burning above him. It presented a study of an ordered landscape set in shadows and reflected light. Beside his computer were a pen and a yellow tablet, half the pages of which were tucked behind it, concealing a proliferation of notes taken from what he considered data of mostly dubious utility. Behind his tablet was a Styrofoam cup, the top of which was stained with brown, caffeinated sludge. The rest of the table was just glare, its flat surface reflecting light into the periphery, where it was quickly absorbed into the darkness.

Clark picked up the distressed Styrofoam cup, walked a pace-and-a-half to the diminutive coffeemaker that sat on the dresser, and poured himself what was left of the coffee. He then returned to his computer and double-clicked the next file in the list. The file opened in a new window, and the cursor blinked patiently in its upper left-hand corner. The document appeared to be an email copied into a text file for safe-keeping.

Clark read the document. And then he read it again. He allowed himself a faint smile. He realized immediately it could be a very important clue, something that had the potential to bring this anguished phase of his life to a quick and bloody end. Then again, even if the document were exactly what he thought it to be, it could amount to nothing at all, meaning it might not lead him to the organization that had obliterated his life, killed his little girl. He studied the numbers, the phrasing. He tried to focus and think, but he realized he was just too tired. He felt a mixture of hope, anguish, excitement, and unmitigated pain all at once. He wanted to continue on, but exhaustion flooded his body, tugged at him like a lead weight. He thought about pressing on anyway, but decided against it. No sense in starting work he'd have to do over again. Fatigue causes errors. Better to put what little energy he had left to some other use.

So instead of working his find, he turned to the thoughtless machinations of copying the important files ferreted from the previous day's search to an external storage device: a heavy duty, encrypted, password-protected thumb drive. He then adjusted the parameters of his search utility to focus on a systematic exploration of images. He always let this part of his forensic interrogation of storage media run overnight while he rested. The prodigious amount of porn kept by pedophiles required the protracted search time. Just locating it all and organizing it sometimes took hours. What took hours more, though, was the way he searched the files.

All files on computers contain something called metadata. Metadata is typically not visible within the file in which it's contained, and its sole purpose is to convey information about its parent document. Metadata, for instance, can hold information such as origination date, original author, caption, description, version, and, in the case of images, a thumbnail. A thumbnail is a very small, low resolution version of the image in which it resides. They're utilized mostly as a view option within folders, allowing users to select images by recognition rather than by name. But a thumbnail does not have to be representative of its parent file. In fact, it can be any image at all. Open a folder, double click a thumbnail image of the San Francisco Bridge, get the Brooklyn Bridge instead. There's lots of software out there that can manage that. Swapping out thumbnail images on pornographic files is part of a well-prepared pedophile's arsenal for keeping snooping eyes out of a highly-prized stash. In his experience, Clark had learned thumbnail masking was effective at deflecting the untrained eye, but fell way short guarding against forensic scrutiny. His image searches, therefore, were always sorted by thumbnail and general metadata content. Typically, pedophiles aren't too creative when they use thumbnail masking. In fact, he'd found most of his targets had used a single low resolution image to mask all of their porn, most likely to allow them to find it more easily. Further, because metadata in general is mostly invisible, Clark included the more salacious verbiage he formerly eschewed as parameters for his image search.

Clark set the search in motion, drained the rest of his coffee, then collapsed onto his bed. He would start working the important information in the morning. At the moment, all he could really think of was sleep.
