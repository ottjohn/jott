Chapter 9

 

 

The scrutiny of the physical evidence was complete. Even with a pending re-test of some of the material at headquarters, there was a resigned collective acknowledgement that its quantity and quality lacked sufficient measure to lead to anything conclusive about Hassenback's assailant. As a shift in strategy hung in the air, Tobias, Sanchez, Jacobsen, and Kabinski stepped away for coffee and to answer one of the few calls of nature that just can't be ignored. Rathman turned away from the camera mounted on his computer, made a non-verbal production of having other things to do, and then set about making himself look busy. It was 1:20 in the afternoon. Ten minutes later, everyone was once again seated around the conference table ready to begin. This time, Kabinski took the lead.

"Okay, let's do a little rehashing. What do we actually know about the assailant? What can we be certain of?" He said gently.

Tobias was perplexed by Kabinski. The man didn't fit his preconceived notion of what he thought a psychologist should look like; in other words, he looked nothing like the legendary Dr. Freud. Instead of a short, crusty old German man sporting a rumpled beard and wrinkled clothes, Kabinski looked like a cover model for one of those magazines targeting preppy, metro sexual males. He couldn't have been anywhere near forty. He had short, blonde hair, green eyes, and a square jaw framing the bottom of a rough but handsome face. He wore a pair of grey Khakis decorated with a thin, brown belt, and a white shirt striped in light blue. An expensive pair of Doc Martins adorned his feet, sans socks. He was the only person in the room who didn't wear a tie. He exuded relaxation, both mentally and physically. Tobias considered his question, which was couched in a voice conflicted with the playboy look he presented. Life is full of paradoxes and inconsistencies.

"We know the assailant is probably between six foot two and six foot five, weighs anywhere between 220 and 240 pounds, handy with computers and computer hardware ..."

Kabinski motioned his head to an adjacent wall, giving Tobias a non-verbal queue to take the floor and itemize his descriptions on one of the room's two white boards. Tobias understood, got up from his chair, walked to the nearest board, picked up a marker, and bulleted the three points of certain knowledge he'd just communicated. Then he continued.

"Further," Tobias said, "we can surmise the assailant or someone close to him has, or had, a child who was impacted in some way by sexual violence. Given the nature of the crime, however, I think it's probably safe to say the child was his and is probably deceased."

"Your hypothesis doesn't go without considerable academic support," chimed in Rathman. "While murder resulting from vigilantism is not a big problem in the states, it still happens. Further, these types of crimes against sexual offenders are much less likely to occur in cases in which the victim of sexual violence survives. While the data does agree with your conclusion, however, its associated probability does not warrant its placement in this list."

Kabinski said, "I agree with Agent Rathman's assessment here, Tobias. Let's leave this off the board for now and continue with what we know for sure."

Tobias turned to face the room, then said, "the rest is academic. From the condition of the crime scene and the absence of useful physical evidence, we can infer the assailant is smart careful, and motivated. And that's about it."

"We can assume one more thing," added Kabinski.

"Yes, we can," added Rathman, not about to be left out.

"What?" Asked Sanchez.

"When considering domestic demographics by race, non-whites have the largest proportion of sexual crimes committed against their children. But when we talk about sheer numbers, it's no contest. When we look at the numbers, the victim is almost always white." Kabinski finished, laced his fingers on the table in front of him, his dark brown tan almost blending into its surface.

"Okay, so we can safely assume the assailant is white," Tobias stated. He turned, added to growing list on the board.

"Most likely," acknowledged Rathman. "And that wraps up assumptions we can be  confident about. Anything else we come up with from this point forward will be speculation grounded with much less certainty."

Jacobsen leaned forward to ensure his voice dominated the background noise collected in the mic in front of him, then said, "In summary, then, we're certain we're looking for a white male, between six foot two and six foot five, weighing between 220 and 240 pounds. Further, the assailant knows his way around computer equipment, and he probably has above average intelligence."

Tobias smiled, pointed his index finger at Jacobsen, and said, "Okay, I'll send out the all-points! With that kind of information, we should have this all wrapped up before morning. Good work, boys!"

Sanchez chuckled, but the rest of the room looked more or less impatient. So much for a little ad hoc humor. Tobias uncapped his pen, turned back to the board, and drew a horizontal line above his list and wrote What We Know above it. He followed that with another horizontal line and the text What We Think We Know just to the right. He then turned to face the room again.

"Okay guys, my partner and I have been thinking about this for the past few days. I admit we didn't come up with the race angle, but that's part of why we're here, right? I do believe, however, we've put enough hours into this case to fill in some of the blanks under our new heading, which might give us a little more to work with. If you disagree with any of what I am about to say, stop me, and we can discuss."

"Please continue. I'm very interested in hearing what you've come up with," said Kabinski. He leaned forward, both elbows on the table, green eyes focused on Tobias as if he were the only person in the room. Jacobsen sat at the end of the table, interested but not in a position to contribute. Rathman fiddled with his tie, repositioned himself in his seat, and did his best to look aloof.

"First, I don't believe for a second we're looking for a third-party vigilante out to avenge a family he knows. I've been a cop for a long time, and I may not know the exact numbers, but I would bet my last paycheck vigilantism of that sort is rare. Not enough stake in the victim. With that in mind, here's who I think he is." Tobias went to the board and wrote.

A father of a victim of sexual violence

Tobias continued, "I don't think we can assume anything else here. Nothing else really makes sense. You can love your niece to death, you can love your god daughter, but you're not going to engage in this type of behavior to avenge the death of a child who's not yours. So let's assume the assailant is the father of a child murdered in the commission of a sex act. What of it? Well, we're in America, right? If you have one kid, then you probably have 2.4. Acting out in this way is very risky, could take you out of the lives of your remaining children, not to mention your wife. My bet is that under almost every circumstance a father with a remainder household is going to choose to take care of business at home rather than launch a vigilante mission fraught with danger and a low probability of success." Tobias returned to the board. This time he wrote,

The victim was an only child

"Now let's return to the crime scene for a moment. The plate glass was knocked out of the window of the door at the back of the Hassenback residence. The assailant entered the home there and then lay in wait until Hassenback returned home. The guy knew where Hassenback lived, he knew what day to lay in wait, and he seems to have known about what time Hassenback would be home. This tells me the assailant watched the victim, long enough to catch on to his day-to-day patterns. All of us in here know this could take weeks, if not longer." Again Tobias wrote on the board.

He conducted surveillance

"I'd wager he spent a few weeks watching Hassenback." Tobias let that float out into the air, allowed it to be breathed in by the others in the room. Then he continued. "That takes preparation. And lots of patience. It takes dedication, as proper surveillance has to be done at all hours of the day and night. It interferes with the rest of your life. Ask any cop's wife about it: surveillance is incompatible with harmonious matrimony." Tobias went to the board one more time, and wrote,

He is alone in the world

Tobias capped his marker, returned it to the white board, then took his seat at the table.

Kabinski leaned back in his chair, laced his fingers together to support the back of his head and considered what Tobias had written. After a moment, he brought his arms down to his sides, looked at Tobias, and said,

"Pretty well considered, detective. And I really don't have a problem with any of it. I think you're right, most acts of vigilantism by mainstream middle class people are kept in check by the nest." He motioned to the board with his head, said, "No reason to sit now. You seem to have a lot of this figured out."

"Thought I would give you guys an opportunity to contribute," Tobias replied, a grin forming at the corners of his mouth, its reflection bouncing out of his eyes.

"All pretty obvious." Quipped Rathman. "Nice guess on the vigilante loner angle, too. I hate to be the bearer of bad news, but a man with a remainder family is not statistically less likely to commit a revenge killing than a father who lost everything. You need to widen your scope a little. But I guess that shows the difference between Quantico and the Police Academy, right?"

"Quit being an ass, Rathman," Kabinski retorted. "Besides, if you would pay more attention to the details, you would've realized this wasn't a revenge killing at all." He then turned to Tobias and said, "Sorry, it would appear Special Agent Rathman is a little crabby right now and in great need of a nap."

Rathman just stared blankly through the wall, said nothing.

Jacobsen appeared to be looking off into the distance, concentrating on something. A confused look was painted on his face. "This is n­ot a revenge killing?" He asked.

"No, most likely it's not," answered Sanchez.

"Okay, I'll bite", said Rathman. "Enlighten me."
Sanchez continued, "the scene itself tells us."

"What do you mean?" Demanded Rathman. He turned in his chair, placed a forefinger at the center of his glasses and pushed them up his nose.

Sanchez said, "the house was trashed. Almost all of the storage media was taken. The whole place underwent a systematic search. If the assailant had been there for a revenge killing, if he'd actually found the person who'd killed his child, then why search at all? What's the point?"

"Perhaps he was looking for images of his child. Maybe he wanted to destroy them," answered Rathman.

Sanchez gave Tobias a look. Deja Vu. Tobias nodded to the kid, let him continue.

"There wasn't any need to remove the media from the house. If that's all he was after, he could have simply destroyed the hard drive and whatever else he found right there. No need to take it. And besides, what would be the point? Hassenback's dead. No one left to spread it. Next visit to the house is from us, and all we're going to do is run it through forensics and eventually destroy it anyway. Further, if Hassenback actually did have images of the guy's kid, odds are he shared those with a friend long ago, and that friend passed them on as well, so what good would it do to destroy the media at all?"

"You're missing something, Sanchez," replied Rathman. A smile formed on his ruddy face, appeared to drip with arrogance. "If the assailant left the media at the house and did not destroy it, we could scrutinize it, perhaps find images of his child, and that would lead directly to him. See, he's not removing the media to conduct some amateur investigation; he's not removing the media to keep more images of his child from spreading around the internet. He's removing the media to cover his ass. And even if he did try to destroy the media at the house, you'd be surprised at what modern computer forensics can do with a fried hard drive. He accomplished what he set out to do by killing Hassenback. The search was about mitigating any chance of connecting him to the crime. Simple as that."

Sanchez blanched, said nothing.

"Good point," said Tobias. "I wish I would've thought of that. But I was all caught up in how the murder went down and what took place afterward. See, Hassenback was killed first, and then his assailant spent the next few hours in his house, systematically searching it for media. We figure he was in there for a good three or four hours based on its thoroughness. He found the computer immediately. He took the hard drive out. But he didn't put it in the sink. He didn't put it in the oven, either. As far as we know he didn't do anything at all to it. He just put it to the side and spent the next three and a half hours continuing his search. He must not have been very smart, that guy. Because if it had been me, and all I was after was the destruction of shots my kid in this pervert's private stash, I think I would have broiled the drive while I conducted the rest of my search. Yeah, computer forensics is hot these days, but it would come up with nothing on a drive that's been broiled in an oven for a couple of hours. But he didn't do that. He made sure he took the drive with him. It was an additional risk, taking that thing out of the house, along with whatever else he found. But why would he take that risk, sir? Unless he wanted something else from the media? And that's what I thought. See, I figured he took the hardware because he thought he might find something important on it."

Rathman laced his tie around his stubby fingers, then said, "like what?"

Tobias continued, "A clue, sir. Something that might lead him to the person who actually did harm his kid. Because when you get down to it, if Hassenback had been the guy, then what would be the point of searching the house at all? The assailant actually seems to be a pretty bright guy. Lots of planning, surveillance, and no evidence. I wonder what he'd think about the odds of our being able to find him by scrutinizing the images on the media? Pedophiles have serious stashes. Which department is going to systematically cross-reference every single one of them with the recorded victims of sexual crimes, and then research the hits they get to look for a large, white man? I'm not a believer in that. He killed Hassenback, he searched the house, and he took the media, all with one goal in mind: to help him with his own search. It's the only thing that makes sense to me."

Tobias leaned back in his chair, looked around at the others as he let them absorb what he'd said. Jacobson had a blank look on his face, Kabinski sported a crooked smirk, Sanchez had an unapologetic shit-eating grin plastered on his face, and Rathman simply looked defeated. The room remained silent for a moment, then Jacobsen turned to Tobias, said,

"I agree with your line of reasoning, Detective Tobias. It seems more probable than taking the hardware to simply destroy evidence elsewhere. Could you write that up on the board?"

Tobias got up, retrieved the marker, and wrote,

Not a revenge killing. Serial action to collect information.

Jacobsen looked around, said "Okay, let's take a break for dinner. None of us have eaten all day. Given what we've come up with so far, though, I think we should get back at this tonight, while the iron is hot. Personally," he looked directly at Tobias, "I think we've made quite a bit of headway."

Tobias and Sanchez got up from their seats, walked out of the conference room, entered the elevator, and took it to the lobby of the new LAPD building. As they headed out the big glass doors of the facility, Sanchez broke the silence.

"Whaddya think, boss?" He asked, a smile beginning to peal around the corners of his mouth.

"I agree with Jacobsen. I think we've made some headway. And what are you smiling about?"

"The way you handed Rathman's ass to him on a platter." Sanchez let out a chuckle and continued, "Honestly, boss, I thought he had a good point. Then you go all Zeus and thunderbolts on him, like he's some kind of kid needing a good lecture. It was great!"

"I think he means well, Sanchez," Tobias lied. "A couple of decades in this business can give a guy insight. That's all it is."

"Okay. If you say so. Where're we going to eat? I'm starving."

"Well, they're probably expecting us back in an hour, so it's going to have to be someplace quick. I saw a Chinese place down the road on the way in. You up for some of that?"

"Yeah. Sure. I just need to eat. I was about to die in there."

"Me too. Let's go."

Carri Parker flipped through the pages of material her friend Lisa had left with her. The amount of information was immense, almost overwhelming; the content was striking. There was a definite conclusion she could leverage in the story she was beginning to formulate: children are under-served in the medical industry. In fact, children are more like a subclass of people afforded only grudging support, and for some unknown and tragic reason, it appeared that children with cancer had the least support of all. It seemed to her the reality of the system was just the opposite of what it should be. As she began re-reading what she had highlighted the night before, her phone rang.

She shifted the material from her lap to her new coffee table, which she had recently picked up from one of those discount furniture markets. The table had an aluminum body, painted black, and its top was constructed from several rectangular stone segments of various sizes.  When buying furniture, Carri followed two self-imposed rules: it had to be inexpensive, and it had to roughly agree in style with the other, mostly-used furniture she had collected over the years. Some of her furniture was modern, some rustic, but together it presented a warm, country feel, a reactionary theme purposefully set against the hustle and bustle of the city in which she worked. She got up from her soft, burgundy couch, tramped barefoot to her kitchen, grabbed the phone from her purse, and answered.

"This is Carri."

Nothing.

Carri waited, her ear pressed to the phone, the sound of her heart beginning to assert itself as the loudest noise in the room. She heard some rustling in the background, wondered if it was Chuck, wondered if he had once again bullied her number out of one of her friends. She hoped not, but given the continuing silence, who else could it be? The jackass was apparently playing one of his stupid, childish mind games, doing whatever he could to make her life miserable. Anger and resentment welled up within her, trumped the fear that generally plagued her when confronting him. She felt blood rush to her face and her pulse quicken.

"Look, asshole, you know this is a violation of your restraining order, and so was that shit you pulled the other day. You're lucky I didn't call the cops on you. I should have." She paused for a reaction. When she got none, she continued, "you know what? I'm going to call them right now, so fuck off, Chuck!" Carri started to move the phone away from her ear and slap it shut, when she heard a tinny, distant

"Wait!"

Carri returned the phone to her ear. Not Chuck. Shit. What if it was a business call? The anger and resentment she'd been feeling were quickly replaced by a wave of embarrassment and nausea. How could something like this even happen? Chuck. He was making her life miserable, almost non-functional.

"You okay?" The voice asked.

Recognition swept over her. She smiled to herself. She'd wondered if he would call, thought he probably wouldn't. Over the past few days, she kept returning to those eyes, quietly contemplating what had been behind them. She remembered how fierce they looked when she first saw him, how they remained that way when he was dealing with Chuck, and how, when he'd talked to her outside the bar, they had transitioned from icy cold to nearly warm.

"Joe! Oh my God, I'm so sorry. I thought you were Chuck." She paused for a moment, then said, "I honestly didn't think you'd call. How are you?" She immediately felt a little embarrassed for asking such a stupid question. She didn't even know the guy. How are you? Compared to what?

"Good," he replied.  "Just thought I'd call and check up on you, make sure you're OK."

"Yeah, I'm fine thanks to you. I still owe you a drink, you know." It wasn't phrased as a statement but as an invitation. She hoped she wasn't being too forward.

There was a pause on the line, then, "You don't owe me anything, Carri. I didn't do anything anyone else wouldn't do. Besides, what could be better than helping a pretty woman in a jam?"

"Flattery will get you everywhere! And, no, what you said is simply not true: I doubt too many guys would have stood up to that bully the way you did."

"You never know. I just happened to be the first guy there."

"Well, no matter," she replied. "But I still want to buy you a drink. You know, for doing nothing special in particular."

Another pause on the line.

"Unfortunately, I'm going to have to take a rain check, Carri. I'm in the middle of something right now, and it's really consuming my time."

Carri disassembled the sentence, started reading between the lines, then caught herself. She remembered thinking how it appeared something was weighing him down when he was in the bar, something really heavy. She wondered if her perception of his state of mind had something to do with whatever was consuming all of his time. She wanted to find out, but she realized she probably wouldn't get the chance: it was clear he wanted to get off the phone. But then why did he call? Just to check up on her? She realized, too, that half of her was in denial about why she really wanted to see this man again, while the other half was warning her she was about to tread some uncertain, maybe dangerous, waters. The briefest of skirmishes ensued, denial won, and she snapped herself back into the conversation to try one more time.

"Hey, I believe you're busy, but I really very much want to show you my appreciation, Joe. Just give me an hour, okay? We can go get a drink and some appetizers somewhere, I can get a proper thank you out of the way, and then we can go our own directions. But I really feel like I need to do this. Please don't make me beg."

Another pause, although this time she thought he might actually be considering her invitation. She offered a little more.

"Hey, I'll even make it someplace close to you so it won't take too long for you to get back to what you're doing."

"Okay, but I can only stay an hour. Is that all right?"

The short conversation over, a quick dinner meeting was set for a place far from Clark's pathetic, temporary hovel on the fringes of LA. He was on the road less than five minutes after the call. Truth be told, he wanted to see her, but he knew he really shouldn't risk the exposure. Lay low. Stay out of trouble. He knew the police had found Hassenback, and they certainly had time to process the scene and study the body by now. Clark was no expert in forensics, but he was confident he'd left very little in the way of material evidence behind. In spite of the probable lack of evidence at the site, however, he figured they had more than likely come up with some kind of general description of him. He knew he shouldn't be bothered by that, though; after all, how many people in the LA area fit his description? But it still made him nervous, and it was better to simply be careful rather than to take unnecessary risks.

On the other hand, he'd been working all day, and he was tired. He felt as though his mind were swimming through Jell-O. And a beer sounded awfully good. It might even help him relax a little and allow him to focus when he got back to work. The website crept back onto the foreground as he drove, pushed aside the idle thoughts. It was very different from anything else he'd seen before. It was corporate. It was sophisticated. It was believable. It had to be a gateway for a large, well-funded organization. He ran its content through his mind, tried to think of something he missed, but nothing came to him. He knew he would have to gain access to the site, so the next obvious step would require a quick surveillance of Hassenback's friend Eduardo, followed by some persuasive conversation.

As Clark made a mental list of things to do, he followed the 110 toward Pasadena, exited on North Avenue, and swung onto West Colorado Boulevard. The place Carri suggested was called Stoney Point Restaurant. He'd heard of it before but had never been there. In his former life, his friends had recommended it, telling him it had an excellent range in cuisine, specializing in French and Italian seafood dishes. He got lucky, found curb-side parking right out front. He killed his ignition, stepped out of his truck, stretched, and took a quick look around. Satisfied the place was anonymous enough, he crossed the sidewalk and walked into the restaurant.

The place was surprisingly small. Along the left wall, there was a bar and serving station, nothing fancy. Immediately in front of the bar was a line of stools, which accommodated several customers, most of whom were probably imbibing in stress-reducing libation before continuing on their way home. Just to the right of the barstools were five, small rectangular tables, done up in white cotton table clothes. White napkins shaped into small pyramids stood atop of leather-bound placemats that doubled as menus. Just to the right of the tables was a wall, within which was set a large picture frame window. The place was cozy, to say the least. Carri sat at the table against the far end of the restaurant, sipping a glass of red wine. She recognized him, smiled, and waved him over.

"Thank you for coming," she said, still smiling.

Carri sat comfortably and confidently in her chair, watching him with sparkling azure eyes as he took a seat across from her. Clark couldn't help but notice how beautiful she looked. She had thick black hair that was pinned up to expose a long, slender neck, which was adorned with a conservative pearl necklace. Her smile revealed a set of pearly-white teeth, which were in perfect contrast with ruby red lips. She wore a simple blue and white cotton dress that came down just below her knee. Her legs were crossed; on her feet she wore simple black flats. She looked like she stepped right out of the fifties. As much as he didn't want to admit it, she looked really good. A waitress came by, and he ordered a beer. As he looked across the table at her, an incongruous thought formulated within his mind and tapped him on the shoulder. He knew he shouldn't ask, but he did anyway.

"So ..." He paused. "How'd you wind up with a guy like Chuck?"

Carri's brow furrowed a bit and she frowned. Nope. Not a good question to ask. But, honestly, he really didn't care. He had a choice: he could stay here for an hour and probably torture himself with conversation that would be painful and evasive, at least on his part; or he could come off as an insensitive asshole, bring this whole thing to an end, and get back to work. She remained quiet for a moment and then answered the question.

"Just one mistake in a long procession of bad decisions." She sighed, her brow relinquishing its message of moderate resentment in favor of a more playful arch. "You see, I've always had big problems finding men who're really worth a damn. In fact, I haven't met one yet!" A slight smile curved at the corners of her mouth. Her eyes, though, signaled her statement was probably closer to the truth than she would've liked to admit.

"Sorry you've had so much trouble," he said. Her eyes softened, but she said nothing.

"What are you going to do about him?"

"I don't know."

"You know he's not going to stop, and I'm sure you know how these things turn out, right?"

"Yeah, I know." A chilly resignation melted over her voice. She was clearly a little uncomfortable.

"Have you thought about moving away?"

"Honestly, Joe, I have. But I have a good thing here. It's taken me years to build up to where I am now at the paper. If I left now, it would be like pressing a giant reset button on my life. I'm not sure I'm willing to do that."

"Fair enough."

Carri said nothing. Clark looked at her. She seemed to be getting a little impatient, perhaps upset, with his line of questioning. He figured he'd probably be back on the road in less than five minutes. But he had to get one more thing out, felt it necessary to motion to the elephant right there in front of both of them. He knew it, and she more than likely did too.

"Look, I know you don't like thinking about things like this. Who does? But if you aren't going anywhere and he's not going anywhere, then you should think about getting a gun and learning how to use it."

"I've considered it. I just don't like them, Joe."

Clark felt a twinge of guilt pass through him. The day he'd helped her, he'd given her that name in case the police had been called. If they had shown up, a fake name would've given them just enough motivation to file away the complaint and then forget about it. There had been no damage, both parties to the disturbance had fled, and the police had much better things to do than look for a couple of guys guilty of nothing more than a bar fight. Wrong name, but what to do about it now, though? It wasn't like he was even remotely thinking of starting anything with this woman.  A little misinformation couldn't hurt things in that case, right? Clark remained silent.

"So ... what do you do?" She asked, like it was a loaded question. Maybe it was.

"This and that," he answered.

"Oh, c'mon. You can give me a little more detail!"

Clark sensed a feigned playfulness in her admonishment, but he also saw something like recognition sweep across her face, as if his answer had clarified something for her. Clark thought about it, then replied,

"I work with children."

Carri's eyes flew open. "Really? What a coincidence! I'm doing a story on children right now. Children and healthcare. In fact, the woman you saw at the bar with me, Lisa, she's my source. She works at a metro hospital. She gave me some information you would not believe."

Clark said a silent prayer of thanks for the coincidence. While it was obvious he failed at his attempt to make a quick exit, given her enthusiasm about her story, he knew if he played his cards right he might get away with not having to talk about himself at all. He put on his most interested face, then said,

"Sounds intriguing. Tell me about what you're writing."

"It's sad, really. I don't want to bring you down."

"Oh please." He smiled. "You've piqued my curiosity. If you leave me hanging now, I won't be able to think of anything else for the rest of the night."

"Well … okay. It's about kids with cancer." She frowned. "Did you know almost twelve thousand kids die from cancer each year?"

Clark shook his head. Negative.

"It's true. And don't get me wrong, research has progressed tremendously over the past couple of decades." Carri made an exaggerated wave of her hands as she said the word tremendously. She continued, "In fact, a couple of decades ago, the survival rate for pediatric cancer was just ten percent. Now it's around eighty. But there are still way too many kids dying from it, and the way funding is channeled to pediatric cancer research and treatment is terrible. Why, it's almost criminal in my opinion!"

Carri was animated, pretty, and passionate. She was very articulate. And she believed in what she was doing, that was clear. He could see the dedication spilling out of her, a deluge resulting from the hard work and tenacity she put into her vocation. He thought she probably had worked very hard to get to her current position at the paper. Why reset that? He made a mental note that he'd need to find Chuck and have a little talk with him before he left town. Clark simply nodded his head in agreement, now drawn into the conversation.

"So what's screwing up the funding?"

Carri took a quick sip of her wine. Clark reciprocated with a sip of beer, which he had been completely ignoring. She continued.

"Well, take the National Cancer Institute, for example. Their budget last year was 4.6 billion dollars." Again she pantomimed with her hands as if she could use them to make Clark understand just how much money that was. "Of that money, breast cancer received twelve percent while prostate cancer received seven. You know how much all childhood cancers combined got?"

Clark again shook his head. Negative.

"Less than three percent, Joe!" Her eyes opened a little wider, she looked upward, and her voice cracked just slightly. Clark briefly wondered if this was more than just a story to her.

"That's a crime," Clark agreed, meaning it.

He was drawn in, found her charm and enthusiasm hard to resist. The rest of the hour was consumed deep in conversation about pediatric cancer, a blur of animated facts and stories about what she'd researched, and the children she'd visited. He'd honestly shared her shock and contempt when she told him that for every one child diagnosed with AIDS, 15 are diagnosed with cancer; yet the amount spent on the child with AIDS is fully 30 times more than that spent on the child with cancer. He'd as well shared her grief when she told him about a particular little girl she'd visited who had succumbed only a week ago after three remissions from leukemia. It hit home, just in a slightly different way.

While he was now actually enjoying himself, Clark found it more and more difficult to follow the remainder of the conversation. His mind kept returning to his work and what he had to do. He had to admit, though, that some of his attention was drifting in another direction, going down a path he couldn't let it. There could be no distractions. Clark found an entry point into the conversation and said,

"You're really passionate about what you do, and I can't even begin to tell you how much I enjoyed our conversation. But ..."

"But you have to get back to work," she finished.

"Yeah. I really am sorry." He reached in his back pocket and began taking out his wallet.

"No!" Carri said, forcefully. "I asked you out; therefore, I'm going to pay. Okay?"

Clark considered the challenge. He was still a little old fashioned and would've rather picked up the tab. But he had to get back to work, so he decided not to haggle about it.

"Okay. I'm fine with that. But you have to let me get the tip."

Carri agreed and Clark tossed a twenty on the table. Carri retrieved her purse from the floor, removed her wallet, opened it, and retrieved her credit card. Clark saw it edge on first. Then, as Carri turned it over to place it flat on the table, asickening feeling dropped into his stomach like a bomb. His breathing became jagged, and his face flushed. The website flashed back into his mind. He had overlooked something, something that could break his search wide open.

"Are you okay?" Asked Carri.

Clark did his best to nod his head and tell her not to worry. He felt dizzy. He put his hands on the table and pushed himself up.

"I really have to go, Carri. Thank you. I had a wonderful time."

Before she could reply, Clark had turned and hurried toward the door.
