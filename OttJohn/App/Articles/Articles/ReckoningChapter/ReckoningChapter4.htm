Chapter 4

 

 

Clark's Friday had started with the purchase of two hard drives that were a little larger than the one he'd taken from Hassenback's computer. He needed them to image it, which would allow him to work with an exact copy of the drive rather than use it directly. That's the first rule of computer forensics. Make a copy. Don't use the original. Bad things happen when you do that. He'd preached that to his students over and over, in his former life. Using the original is risky, opens the door to equipment failure, inadvertent writing, and the activation of data-munching trojans, things that really don't matter much with a copy. It can be replaced. The original can't.

It was his practice to make two copies of everything. He expected failures and experienced them often enough. Imaging one drive is time consuming; imaging two is almost unbearable.  It would take the better part of the day just to get the copies made and ready for interrogation, roughly sixteen hours. Downtime. He couldn't do any work while his laptop managed the process, so he spent most of the time catching up on sleep. He hadn't realized how tired he'd been, though. The previous two weeks of surveillance and planning had him working eighteen hour days the whole way, so sleep came easy. So easy, he'd slept through all of Friday and into Saturday morning, waking only occasionally to check on the progress of the drives. 

 

At five in the afternoon on Saturday, Clark sat at the table in his weekly staring at a bag of tacos he'd picked up at a local fast food place. He'd spent the better part of the day organizing the data on the imaged drives, preparing them for a systematic search that would consume at least another two days. The smell of grease wafted the air, momentarily taking his mind from the retrospective of his latest operation. It was another thing he'd always done after an interrogation: review everything that had transpired during the operation, giving himself scores for its different components, then writing a synopsis of what could have gone better, and what he'd executed well.

He really didn't want to think about this one, didn't want to consider the mistakes he'd made. Of all the interrogations he'd conducted, the retrospective from the last one promised to teach him more about operational protocol than all the others combined. He removed one of the tacos from its wrapper and took a bite. Not very good. High in fat, low quality beef, probably no milk at all in the cheese. He drowned it in hot sauce, hoping the condiment would cure the flavor.

It hadn't gone very well. He gave himself high marks for data acquisition, but he failed miserably on the interrogation. He'd let his emotions get the better of him and blown it. Over the past couple of years, He'd viewed prodigious amounts of child pornography, images he was sure would haunt him for the rest of his life. But he had to, had no choice in the matter. He had to find a clue, a connection, something that would give him direction. And he thought he'd seen it all, too. But he was wrong. He closed his eyes, thought about what had burned into his mind that evening, wondered if he could have done things a little differently.

Hope for a productive interrogation had died with the images that flickered on Hassenback's television, images that seeped into his thoughts like a toxic sludge. He'd only glimpsed some of it, but what he'd seen had been enough. There was a girl, perhaps seven. She was alone on a bed in a dimly-lit room that looked like a dungeon. She had short brown hair and a soft, oval face that was pale with blotchy purple marks that undoubtedly came from beatings. Her large, brown eyes stared up toward the ceiling. They looked hollow, the kind you see in drug addicts at the apex of their highs. She twisted slowly, her naked body rolling over a pool of blood puddled on the sheets between her legs. He remembered looking away then. It was not the image of the girl that had caused him to momentarily lose focus, to seek shelter in the dullness of wood flooring. It was the sheets. They were plastic. And he knew what was coming next. He forced himself to look again, tried to deny what he was seeing, rationalize it somehow. As his eyes returned to the screen, he noticed the girl's head had changed slightly: a small, red dot decorated the middle of her forehead. Blood trickled from it as her eyes stared out into the room, comprehending nothing.

She was dead. He remembered his heart beat notch up so high it felt as if it were going to burst through his chest. His head felt like it was evaporating, his skin began to crawl, and his fingertips numbed a little. At that moment, he felt as if the entire house were closing in on him. He felt confused, like a caged animal. All he wanted to do was run, get away. His breathing became erratic. He found it difficult to control. Then he forced himself to look at Hassenback. That brought him focus. And purpose. He took three quick steps across a floor that somehow had turned spongy, and then nearly ripped the man's head off.

He'd thought about what to do with the DVD while ripping the house apart. He didn't want to make a copy, really didn't know if he could stomach seeing the little girl again, what she had to endure. But he knew he had to. There could be information there he could use, and the potential of good information outweighed his instinctual need to protect the fortitude of both his stomach and mind. So he copied it to a secure thumb drive, returned it to the player, advanced it to the track that showed the murder, and pressed pause. He'd given some consideration about whether he should even leave the DVD but decided law enforcement needed to know Hassenback had not rehabilitated. He was not the victim of a senseless crime. He knew they still had to do their jobs, try to find him and prosecute him, but perhaps the DVD would make them a little less careful.

There was no other possible end to the operation, no way he could have watched that image and not react the way he did. Yes, it may have cost him valuable information he could've collected through persuasive conversation, but trying to control his reaction would have been as unproductive as trying to reverse the tides. He shook the memory from his mind, tried to focus on the task at hand. But he failed. He looked at his watch. It was 6:45 PM. He put the taco down. He'd lost his appetite. What he needed was an ice-cold beer. He locked his computer, grabbed the keys to his truck, and walked out the door of the weekly. He had no idea where he was heading, but he figured just about any place would do.

"Hi, Papa!" exclaimed the little girl over the phone. "You see the new dolly I got? She's pretty. She says she lubs you." The little girl giggled, always happy to speak to her grandfather.

The sound of her voice was good medicine. It seemed that whatever bothered Tobias, Sarah and her wonderful, imaginative conversations always impacted him in a profoundly good way, notwithstanding their sometimes truncated nature. She was, after all, only four. She didn't have to say anything special; all she had to do was talk. As Sarah gabbed on about her dog, her parents, what she wanted for Christmas, and how much she wished it would snow, Tobias' horrific memories of the DVD faded, if only temporarily, into the fog in the back of his mind. He knew they'd lurk there, like a big cat in a black jungle, just waiting to pounce. Later in the evening, when he closed his eyes for the night, he knew the cat would slink to the foreground, bringing with it the awful image of that little girl. Now, however, he gave silent thanks for the reprieve, complements of the wonderful color and simplicity of Sarah's world.

"I do see your dolly! What's her name?"

"Wendy. And she has a potty mouth! She said something bad." She whispered, inflecting the last word to accentuate the gravity of doll's offense. "So I put her time-out. Hi wiwey!"

Evidently, the family dog had interjected itself into the conversation. Tobias got in an "I love you, Sarah," just as she passed the phone back to her mother.

"Well, I guess that's all she wants to do with you!" Said Anne, laughing.

"Yeah. But it was nice. I really needed to hear her voice. Yours too," Tobias replied, choking a little on the last words.

"How bad was it, daddy?"

"Bad. Worst I've ever seen. And, as usual, I can't talk about it. Thanks for asking though. Just wanted to hear your voices. You know how I get. I'm a big baby."

"Oh shush!" She admonished.

The line went quiet. Slowly, Sarah's little island of peace was being replaced with the jungle cat, the images slipping into his mind, stalking his thoughts.

"Tell Sarah her papa loves her. I love you too, honey," he said, preparing to hang up.

"Hey, how about a BBQ at your place Tomorrow? Brandon doesn't have to work, and it'd be good to catch up some. We haven't seen you two in about a month."

Tobias had a strong character; people in his position have to; otherwise, they turn into alcoholics and become social disasters. Given the intermittent peaks of stress in their lives, it's no wonder cops have one of the highest divorce rates in the nation. Many just can't handle the stress of the job and a social life at the same time. But even well-adjusted cops sometimes run into cases that bring them to the brink. And Anne understood that. She always had. And her self-invitation couldn't have come at a better time: he couldn't think of anything better than a day full of family, food, drink, and big doses of his granddaughter.

"Sounds like a great plan, sweetie. Give your mom a call tomorrow so we can set up a time; and don't bring anything. We've got it covered. Love you." Tobias paused for just a moment, then said, "Thanks." Then he cradled the phone.

"Did that help?" Carol asked.

He knew it was a rhetorical question. Whenever he had particularly bad days, he would give his daughter a call, try to lose himself in conversation with her and his granddaughter. It was medicine to him, gave him a psychological boost to help him climb out of the hole some of his cases put him into. He made the calls mostly during investigations of crimes against women and children. And when a child got hurt, he especially wanted to talk to Sarah.

Tobias regarded Carol as much more than a wife. She completely balanced him with the warm, comfortable companionship that most can look for their whole lives and never find. She was his best friend, his soul mate. She filled his life with artistic and intellectual diversions that carried away most of the occupational hazards associated with his profession. She was his rudder when he had difficulty steering his sometimes-troubled waters; she was his life-vest when those waters became ominous and threatened to drown him.

They'd met in college. Carol was a petite, brainy history major who had the sensuous librarian look down to an art. She was just under five-ten, blonde hair pulled into a tight pony tail, blue eyes, and an athletic body she constantly hid with loose-fitting clothes and over-sized sweaters. Tobias had always thought she looked like a blonde Audrey Hepburn adorned with tortoise shell glasses. The first time he saw her, she was playing Cressida in a small campus Shakespearean production. To the amusement of his friends, Tobias had an unwavering passion for Elizabethan literature and Metaphysical poetry. While he'd read the play, he knew it was rarely produced, so he skipped his night class in Biology and the late-night poker game with his buddies to attend the production.

While the presentation of the play was a little rough, Tobias was captivated by the girl playing the part of Cressida. She executed her lines flawlessly and did a great job bringing her character to life. At times, he thought she was looking at just him, performing for his entertainment only. Of course, this was not the case; she didn't even know he existed at the time. But his perception spoke to her ability as a first-rate thespian. As the scenes slipped by, he noticed he'd been watching less of the play and more of her.

When the performance was over, he felt an overwhelming need to talk to her. He wasn't looking for a date, but he felt a connection. It wasn't in his nature to just walk up to a beautiful and talented girl and strike up a conversation: he had two left feet, a rented tongue that frequently broke down, and a conversation pattern that was choppier than a wind-whipped sea. His need to speak with her, though, outweighed all of that. He was petrified when he initially approached her. Close up, she was striking, had eyes that radiated intelligence. And a little fire. To his astonishment, though, she'd accepted his invitation for coffee and a discussion about the play. That night, they began a conversation that would last for the rest of their lives.

"You okay, sweetie?" Asked Carol.

Tobias didn't answer. He walked over to her, gave her a kiss, and held her. As they embraced, she freed one of her hands to wipe a tiny tear streaming down the side of his face, a tear he knew told her in no uncertain terms that this had indeed been a terrible last couple of days. And it was going to be a long night. She said nothing, just continued holding him for a moment, then took him by the hand and lead him to the bedroom.
