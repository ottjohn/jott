By the time Gerkowitcz heard about the gate drop, it was too late. What the fuck was Frank thinking? Of all the vehicles to come down his road, it had to be a fucking Crown Victoria. Old people and cops. That's who drive those cars. And there wouldn't be any old people wanting to drive off road to get to his spread in the middle of the fucking desert, now would there? Out here, a Crown Vic equals cops. Any fucking idiot should know to fall on the side of caution there. But Frank didn't. So he engaged the release on the cattle guard, and the cops drove right in. They could have pushed everyone into the shelter, left Manheim up top to rake the fucking rock over the God Damned hatch, just like they had fucking planned. Manheim would have been perfect. He's smarter than a hundred cops. He could have played fucking stupid until the cops searched the place up and down, got bored, and left. But it hadn't worked that way. How the hell was he surrounded by so many stupid fucking people on a daily basis? Fuck!

And the reassurances by Frank hadn't helped, either. They're alone. That means no warrant. That means they don't have shit or they're here for another reason entirely. And that means we can play this whole thing off like an accident, a glitch in the security system. Right. A wrecked cop car and a security system on a ranch out in the middle of fucking nowhere. That's not going to raise any suspicions. Fucking moron.

But maybe they could play it off. Maybe an apology and restitution would be good enough. But he wasn't taking any chances, so he told Frank to arm himself, take his radio, and start scouting the back side of the property.

About five minutes after that all hell broke loose.

The morning light had begun to illuminate the property, so he had taken out his binoculars to scan the road. He found the cop car immediately. The cottonwoods obscured a lot of his view, but he could make out the important stuff and piece together in his mind what he couldn't see. The cruiser's front end was deep in the pit; the back end was sticking up, wheels off the ground. He saw Rick standing there, between the van and the car. The cops were out. They were dressed in plain clothes, had their hands up. It looked like Rick was pointing a handgun at the guy nearest him.

Then Frank radioed, said he'd spotted an intruder, about 300 yards to the east of the property. Then the radio went dead. About ten seconds later, he heard the unmistakable sound of a rifle shot, that crackling noise the air makes when a bullet moves through it at supersonic speeds. Frank had shot and killed the guy, no questions asked.

Then Rick killed the cop.

Then Rick got his brains blown out by some other unknown intruder. The guy had just come up out of the desert like a God damned phantom, leveled some kind of automatic at the back of Rick's head, and then blew it off. And he wasn't a cop, either. Because the very next thing he did was kill the other cop.

 

Guns. He needed lots of guns. He hadn't brought them up earlier because he thought there still might be a chance to play nice with the officers. But now there were no officers. Just a fucking maniac with an automatic. Gerkowitcz  dropped his binoculars and headed for the shelter. That's where the guns were. They had lots of them, but besides the rifle Frank took, they were all fucking pea-shooters, .22 calibers made up to look deadly, like Chihuahuas done up with plastic fangs. He walked down the steps into the basement, shuffled through the coarse gravel covering its floor, then climbed down the hatch and into the Shelter. As he walked across the floor of the great room, he found Manheim, gave him an update, and then told him to lock the customers in the rooms with the girls. It wasn't for their protection. He just didn't want a panic on his hands. That would make things more chaotic than they already were, might make things completely unmanageable. So the less they knew, the better. As Manheim walked away, Gerkowitcz hurried to the armory, which was in the storage room, directly across from the boardroom.

He retrieved a large set of keys from his right pants pocket, fumbled with them until he found the one that opened the heavy metal cabinet used for weapons storage. He unlocked it, turned a metal latch to open one of the doors, then reached in and grabbed a scope-mounted rifle. As he lifted it out of the cabinet, he noticed his hands were shaking. Four dead in barely a minute. What a fucking mess. He thought briefly of his family, his daughter, mostly, and admitted to himself his fat, lazy wife was infinitely more appealing than what he was facing now.

He chuckled to himself, then shook his head at no one.

What was he supposed to do? Lay down like a sniffling little pussy? No. He'd fight. Because that's who he was: a fighter. A survivor. If Frank was right, no one knew where the fucking cops were, anyway. They'd just clean the damn mess, kill the guy with the automatic, then dump the whole lot of them, car and all, into one of the water-laden pits out to the east. And to get all that done, he'd need Frank back in the building. He found himself once again seething at the man's stupidity, figured it would take a miracle to keep Frank out of the pit too when all this was done. Gerkowitcz picked up his two-way radio, held it up to his mouth, and keyed to speak.

"Frank, I need you back here."

"You sure? I haven't completely searched the area. There could be more." His voice was labored and jumpy, as if he were running and out of breath.

"Look, I'm not really sure what the hell's going on, but I do know one thing: if we're some kind of target here and there were more men out there, then we'd have been inundated by now. But we haven't been. That just leaves the guy with the automatic. And until we hear otherwise, we treat him as an unfriendly: shoot first, ask questions later. And the last place he was seen was out front, so that's where I need you."

"Roger that. On my way."

"And Frank?"

"Yeah?"

"Double time it. I need you here now."

"Yes, sir!"

Gerkowitcz stood next to the open cabinet for a long moment, set his mind into motion. There had to be an angle, something he could use to regain control quickly, put everything back to the way it was. He thought about his resources, but they were mostly defensive. Or non-functional. Like the fucking security system he'd hemorrhaged money for over the past several weeks. The video system could've given him a distinct advantage over the guy with the automatic, but of course it wasn't hooked up yet. Then he thought about his customers. Maybe locking them up was the wrong approach.

"Manheim!" He almost screamed the name. The sound of his voice took him by surprise as it boomed through the room and then echoed off its tall, concrete walls. It sounded strong, authoritative. It made him feel better about things, made him feel more in control.

Manheim rushed around the corner, then stopped suddenly, eyes open wide in surprise, mouth agape at seeing a rifle barrel pointing right at his face.

"You're not going to shoot me, are you?" His voice was halting, his words spoken slowly and carefully.

Gerkowitcz regarded the man. He was pale, thin, about as greasy as a mop dipped in peanut oil, and about as weak as a doped-up anorexic. But he was smart, and he had the power of persuasion. He lowered the rifle and smiled.

"How many customers do we have right now?"

"Full house."

Gerkowitcz shook his head. These days, a full house meant five customers. Would've been six if not for the stupid fucker who killed one of his girls earlier in the week. Just another stupid person in a whole sea swimming with morons.

"You take a good look at any of them?"

"Yeah, sure."

"How many look like they can handle a gun, operate under duress?"

Manheim cast his eyes up at the ceiling, ruminated on the question for just a moment, then said,

"Four of them are what I would consider to be less than ideal candidates for security augmentation, if that's what you're getting at."

"It is." Jesus. He was working with fucking Radar O'Reilly. "Go find the lamest duck of the bunch, leave the guy you think's capable alone for now. We might actually need him later."

"What are we going to do with a guy with no skills?"

"We're going to send him out the front door and use him as bait. If we can flush this guy out, maybe I can get a bead on him and blow his brains out.

For a few moments it was almost quiet, the only sounds coming from the subdued rumble of the Vic's V8 and the muted whine of the rear tires as they spun around in the air, grabbing at nothing. It was a pleasant change from the cacophony of gunfire that had just erupted, made him almost feel as if normalcy had descended into the desert around him, promising everything was going to turn out all right. But he knew that wasn't true. Not by a long shot. He sat on the ground, gun in hand, doing his best to leverage the ambient calmness as way to focus his vigilance. It appeared to be working until his short-lived peace was disrupted by the sound of a man's voice, the voice of the man he'd been hunting for the past couple of weeks. It was the voice of Jack Clark.

"Throw your weapon out. Come out where I can see you. Do it slowly." The voice was calm, the words spoken carefully.

Tobias remained where he was, in a seated position, adjacent to the left front quarter panel of his cruiser. He was alert, aware of everything around him, maybe even hyper-aware. But like a patient who claims to see and hear everything when under the sleepy spell of anesthesia, he was locked out of action, shackled by indecision, overwhelmed by sensory input. He managed to glance to his right, around the front of his car. He could see Sanchez lying on the ground no more than fifteen feet away, face half blown off. The remaining eye, his left one, was looking directly at him, unfocused, dead.

A whole bevy of emotions welled up within him, the most prominent of which was anger.

Tobias could feel it moving through him, mixing with the adrenaline still coursing through his system. The adrenaline focused the anger, removed its ambivalence, dissolved its impurities until there was nothing left but rage. Suddenly, his indecision was gone, vaporized by the toxicity of the raw emotion burning within him. He wanted to do something. He wanted to come out firing. But he knew he couldn't. Not because he was scared. He was beyond that. And not because he was at a tactical disadvantage, either. He'd been in this situation before, knew how to handle it. He couldn't come out firing because there was nobody left to shoot, nobody to bear the brunt of his rage. He wasn't going to kill Clark. That's because Clark hadn't tried to kill him. He could have, but he chose to fire off a burst into his car instead. And that was his last shackle, the only remaining circumstance that was holding him back. And it wasn't coming off. At least not yet. So he just sat where he was, trying to push his mind into a lucid contemplation about what to do next.

"What's your name?" The voice was still calm, almost peaceful.

"Tobias." He could hear himself answer the question, but it felt as if someone else had offered up the response.

"Clark."

"I know who you are."

"Then you know why I'm here."

"Yes, I do."

"Then you know I have no quarrel with you."

Tobias said nothing.

"Look, I have no intention of harming you. I just want to talk. Please toss your weapon toward the front of your vehicle and come out so I can see you."

Tobias shifted in the dirt until his back was pressed firmly against the cruiser. Clark sounded sincere, but he couldn't take that risk, couldn't just give up his gun and walk right into a situation that had a high probability of being an ambush. It wasn't that he didn't believe Clark. He was inclined to. But a guy doesn't throw away years of training and a mountain of stats that overwhelmingly indicate that if you give up your firearm to an opponent, more times than not you're going to wind up dead. Better to err on the side of caution, give the nod to the worst-case scenario. So he maintained his position, decided his best route was to build up a conversation, engineer a dialog that might knock down Clark's defenses.

"Can't do it, Clark. Department policy. And besides, I don't have any practical reassurance you aren't going to just blow my head off once you see me."

"But you do."

"How's that?"

"I shot your radio, Tobias. If had wanted to kill you, we wouldn't be having this conversation. You'd be dead."

Tobias didn't reply, just sat motionless, thinking.

"Look, you need to understand something: I made a promise to my little girl. At her grave. I'm going to keep it, Tobias. Please don't make me do something I really don't want to do."

Tobias shifted his position, kept his gun close to his side.

"Okay, I understand that. I even sympathize with it. But it's not going to bring them back, Clark. All you're doing is adding to a body count already way too high. Think about it. What will this bring? What good can come of this? You have them now. You have them cornered. You know where they are. Let the legal system take over. We can forget about everything that's happened, and you can just go home."

"Home? What home? Everything that was home, everything that was the best part of me, was taken two years ago. I made a promise. I'm going to keep it. And you're right: I've got them, and now they're going to pay."

"You're making a mistake, son. Let me call for backup. I can get a hundred cops out here in no time. No one else has to die."

"I've already given the system a chance. It failed. Now it's my turn. I don't want your help; I don't need your help."

"Then what do you want from me?"

"Just to turn your head for a little bit. That's all I ask. If you can do that, if you can give me a few hours, I'll come back and turn myself in. You have my word on that." Clark hesitated, then continued. "But if you can't, Tobias, if you're going to try to stand in my way ..."

Tobias cut him off. "Yeah, I know. I'm a dead man. Doesn't look like I have much of a choice now, do I? Automatic versus a department-issue pea shooter? Story of my life. But you have to know I can't make promises like that, Clark. I've sworn to uphold the law, not facilitate its decimation."

Clark said nothing.

Tobias thought about what he'd said, concluded it sounded hollow in the face of what had just happened. Sanchez was dead. He'd lost more than a partner. He'd lost a good friend. A son. And he felt responsible. But there was something else: the rage he'd felt earlier was being polluted by confusion and uncertainty, the deadly focus it had given him was eroding, giving way to a fundamental question posed by overwhelming circumstance: why the hell shouldn't Clark head down the road and shoot up the place? Every son of a bitch in there deserved a bullet in the head as far as he was concerned. Every last one. But he knew that wouldn't bring Sanchez back. Nothing would bring the kid back. He was a corpse now. And that's all he'd ever be until the end of time. Why? No reason. Because he was there. The son of a bitch killed Sanchez because he was there. He asked himself the question again. Why not? He couldn't think of any good reason. None. So he'd just turn his head. Let Clark do his thing. Then clean up.

Tobias emptied the chamber of his handgun, ejected the cartridge, then threw it toward the front of his cruiser. It landed toward the left side of the road, about ten feet in front of the gaping hole left by the phony cattle guard.

"Clark ... I'm coming out. I'm unarmed."

He waited for a reply but got none. He slowly turned until he was facing his cruiser, then began lifting himself upward, at a glacial pace, just until  his eyes could see over and around the crumpled hood of his wrecked vehicle. He saw nothing.

"Clark?" His voice was barely above a whisper.

No response.

"I'm going to do what you want. I'm okay with it. I'll look the other way. I'm coming out now, okay?"

No response.

"Are you going to answer?"

No response.

He was already partially exposed. He knew Clark could be anywhere, have him sighted in, head right in the crosshairs of his scope, ready for the kill shot. But he knew that wasn't the case. If Clark had wanted to kill him, he'd have been dead already. Simple as that. He didn't know why Clark wasn't answering, but he was certain he was going to find out in the next few seconds. Maybe it was some kind of psychological ploy, a dominance thing. He took a deep breath, then exhaled slowly as he stood. As his head cleared the cruiser just enough to give him an unimpeded view of the area directly in front of him, he discovered why Clark wasn't talking to him. He'd already left.

The shot he'd heard, the one that set off the deadly exchange on the road, wasn't from one of the Mossbergs. Nor was it from any kind of handgun, either. It was the distinctive crack of a high caliber rifle, which had most likely fired a long range shot that ended Tiny's life. He had to be dead. There was no other conclusion. No three clicks. Tiny wasn't distressed. Tiny wasn't anything. Not anymore.

As he crawled along the tree line, Clark made a mental checklist of what he had. It wasn't that he didn't already know. It was more about keeping his mind busy, maintaining his mental acuity as he slid along the desert floor like a snake dressed up in cotton outerwear. He focused on the small of his back, could feel the presence of the Steyr strapped there, secured firmly to his mid-section by several orbits of duct tape. The handgun had one round in the chamber and another sixteen in the clip. It was unused and ready for action. He spent four of the Vinny's bullets: one into the back of some guy's head, then another three rounds into the cruiser. That left six rounds in the clip with another ten in his pockets. Should be enough. Unless the place had a sizeable security team. But he seriously doubted that. If they had, they would've pressed and flushed. But they didn't, which meant there'd be no more than five guys in his way, probably four or less. Plus some customers and kids.

He fell into a pattern as he made his way toward the buildings in front of him: he'd crawl on his belly for ten feet, stop for two minutes, then repeat. When he stopped, he'd lay motionless, then silently scan his immediate area for threats. It was an exercise requiring stamina and patience, two things he was beginning to run short of. He figured it was a pretty good system, though: it had allowed him to progress more than a hundred yards without getting fired at or shot. He'd been assessing his surroundings during one of his stops when he heard a sound. It was a cracking noise, like a twig makes when fractured by the weight of a heavy, moving object. The crack was followed immediately by the clumsy thumping of footfalls, dampened by the desert sand. It came from his left, on the other side of the road, emanating from a location just about perpendicular to where he was dug in.

He pushed his face into the dirt, waited for several seconds, then looked up and scanned the area around him. Save for his rear, he could see no obvious threats. He rolled up into a sitting position, placed his back against the trunk of a large cottonwood, then slowly raised the barrel of the Vinny, used it as a pointer to scan the other side of the road. He had to shift the barrel a full twenty five degrees to his left before he saw the figure of a man moving quickly toward the vehicles. He peered through the scope, placing the crosshairs on the back of the man's bobbing head.  He had a handgun in his right hand, pointing in no particular direction. He was wearing jeans and a long-sleeved cotton shirt. And he was thin. As he walked, he shifted his head from side to side, almost frantically, looking for threats. When he turned his head to the left, Clark could see he was old. He had wrinkles, a stubbly grey beard, and a long, bulbous nose that seemed to burst out of an otherwise flat face.

Thin, old, and scared. Definitely not the guy who ran the place. Not security, either. A disappearing cattle guard and an employee who almost took down two cops single-handedly told him the person who ran the operation spared little expense for security and qualified personnel. The man he was looking at didn't fit the profile. He looked like he wanted to get away, not defend. He lowered the Vinny, concluded the guy was a customer, probably conscripted into service under a this-is-the-only-way-you're-going-to-save-your-ass argument. Instead of trying to help the cause, the guy looked as though he took the gun and decided to make a run for it, take his chances with the heat and the dryness of the desert. Good decision for an old guy. Too bad for him a pissed off cop was just down the road, waiting to take his anger out on somebody. So Clark let him go. No sense in giving up his location for that.

 

He continued to move on his belly like a slug, undulating along the ground, becoming one with it. He worked his way along the side of the road, using the cottonwoods, an occasional shrub, and dirt as camouflage. He felt as though he'd been crawling for hours, but the position of the sun told him otherwise: it was high in the sky, just short of its zenith. He'd been crawling for perhaps just under an hour, give or take a few minutes. He was sweaty. He was hot. He was running out of water. But with each crawl forward, he could see the buildings in front of him loom larger.

There were two main structures: one looked more like a typical residence, sporting a small yard and curtained windows. A half-consumed beer balanced precariously on the railing of its modest front porch. The other building, on the other hand, displayed none of the accoutrements of family quarters. It was much larger and much older than the smaller structure. It had a large front door and an even larger window that gave an unimpeded view of a gravel clearing in front of it. The gravel had a multitude of depressions leading to cement steps, indentations most likely made by frequent foot traffic. He concluded the older house was more than likely the center for business activity, so he decided to split his attention between the structures so he'd be watching the main building eighty percent of the time.

He got comfortable, dug himself in for a long wait. But almost immediately, something happened. Something he'd been looking for. He saw two people at the large window: one rail thin with greasy hair, the other fat and older. The fat guy appeared to barking at the thin man. Clark figured him for the boss, or at least some kind of supervisor. That made the smaller guy expendable. Clark rolled over onto his back, lifted his head, and checked his rear. Nobody behind him. Nothing to his sides, either. He returned his attention to the window, placed the crosshairs of his scope on the little man, centering them directly on his forehead.

Then he did a quick calculation regarding his next move, the one following the one he'd already queued up, the one that would end the life of the thin man. He thought about it for  several moments, concluded it was risky but acceptable. Besides, it was the only thing he could think of doing, the only course of action he could take if he were to push his little war to a quick conclusion. The little man maintained his position, appeared to absorb the tongue lashing from the fat man the way a paper towel might absorb a rancid, liquid mess. Clark kept the crosshairs on the man's forehead and used his left thumb to move the shot selector on the Vinny to single. He took a deep breath, then exhaled to empty his lungs. Then he squeezed the trigger and fired.

He figured it was a reasonable plan. Their decoy, some old guy named Charlie, had been tasked with a simple objective: just walk the road. Then he'd wait, hidden behind the couch, rifle barrel leveled over its back. He'd follow Charlie through his scope and look for an opportunity. He hoped Charlie would spook the guy with the automatic, cause him to take a shot. And that's all he needed. If Charlie drew fire, he could get a bead on the guy and take him out from the safety of the house, lay down a series of well-placed shots, right through the front window. But it didn't work out that way. Charlie did what he'd asked him to do, but he just kept on walking. Nothing happened.

The mistake had been in standing. The guy hadn't been flushed out, so he just wasn't out there, right? It was a reasonable conclusion. So they stood. Right in front of the fucking window. He'd been in the middle of giving Manheim an earful about his uncustomary lack of invention, the untimely logjam of genius he needed from him badly to solve the crisis unfolding around them. Manheim had just been standing there listening, giving him his full attention, both eyes focused, absorbing everything in that sick fucking intellectual way that made Gerkowitcz want to rip his head off and piss down his throat. Then his face exploded.

Besides the sound of the window shattering, the only thing he'd heard was a muffled pop, like when fireworks are going off in the distance. But this wasn't a shot taken from a distance. It was close. Through the corner of his left eye, he saw it: a faint flash, from low on the ground, less than fifty yards away. The next few seconds were a blur. He'd dropped to the floor, then crawled on his hands and knees to the back of the room to take cover behind an antique desk. It was made of thick, polished wood, solid craftsmanship, the kind he hoped would stop whatever fucking bullets the guy outside was spitting out of that God damned automatic. A fucking silenced automatic at that. Who the fuck was this guy? Whoever it was, he'd definitely established one thing for sure: he wasn't just after the cops; he was after him too. He was attacking his business. The cops just happened to be in the way.

He waited behind the desk for another shot, a frontal assault. He half expected the guy to come charging through the front door, lay down devastating bursts of automatic fire, riddle the entire building with bullet holes. But the assault didn't come. After about five minutes, he made a tentative grab for his rifle, laid the muzzle over the surface of the desk. Then he slowly raised up, giving himself just enough room to peer out of the shattered window. Nothing. He inched up further, just enough so his left eye was even with the scope on the rifle. He looked through it, dead ahead. Nothing. He inched up a little more, just enough to allow him to swing the rifle to the left and right while looking through the scope. Nothing. The fucker just disappeared. Right back into the fucking desert.

He continued scanning the property for another ten minutes, finally concluded it was safe to move about. So he crawled on his hands and knees to where he'd been standing when Manheim had been blown away, right to the spot where he'd dropped his radio. He picked it up, then keyed to speak.

"Frank! Where the fuck are you?"

The radio crackled, then Frank's voice came back. It was clear, as if he were standing right in the next room.

"Approaching the back of the house. Be with you in less than five."

"Keep your fucking eyes peeled! Manheim's dead. That fucker dropped him right through the front window. I certain it's just the one guy, the same one who killed Rick. He's either still out front pressed into the dirt, or he's pulled back and may be heading in your direction, so watch yourself."

"Want me to check it out?"

Gerkowitcz dropped his head for a moment, briefly wondered how low Frank's IQ was. The guy had just killed three men. Systematically. Like he did it for a living. He had a fucking silenced automatic. And he was moving about like he was a part of the desert. Like he was the desert. Frank was good, but he wasn't that good. Not against someone like that. And he didn't have the fucking sense to know it. Part of him wanted to tell him to go ahead and search the area. Just to rid himself of the moron. But he knew if Frank got wasted, his chances for survival diminished to just about nil. He needed Frank, if just for a little longer.

"Negative. Just get your ass back here. We need to talk about this, then do something. Fast."

"Roger that. Just about there. Sit tight."

Gerkowitcz put the radio down, then glanced to where Manheim had been standing. He was sprawled on the floor. He had a hole the size of a silver dollar in the back of his head. An effusion of blood and brain matter had poured through it, soaking the floor around him in a crimson pool about three feet in diameter. He felt his stomach heave just a little. He turned away, crawled back behind the desk, then sat and waited for Frank.

 

Frank arrived less than two minutes later. Gerkowitcz spent the following five minutes detailing what they'd done and how Manheim had died.

Frank said, "Sounds like a good strategy, but after what you've told me about this guy, a passive attack is definitely not going to work. There's something else, too. Something I was thinking about on the way back in. He's not a cop, so who is he?"

"How the fuck should I know, Frank? I'm on the business side of this operation, remember? My job's to make this run, not to understand who could be behind potential threats to it! That's your God damned job!"

"Right." Frank paused, sucked in his breath. "I think there's really only one plausible conclusion, Dean."

"You want to tell me what you're thinking, Frank? We're wasting time here while that asshole is out there planning his next move."

"He's a parent of one of the girls, Dean. And somehow, he's found us. The police caught on to what he was doing, and that's why they were here too. It's the only thing that makes sense."

Gerkowitcz nodded his head in agreement. A fucking parent. Not a maniac. A highly-skilled and very pissed parent. Then he had an idea.

"Then let's march all the girls out front, line them up, and threaten to kill them one-by-one until he surrenders. We can take up position inside the house on either side of the window and just wait. If his kid's in here, can you think of any better leverage?"

Frank hesitated for a moment. "Not gonna work, Dean."

"Why not?"

"Because it keeps us locked into a single position at the front of the house. He'll see that and attack from the rear. He's got the advantage there. If one of us leaves to watch the back of the house, he'll know that too, and he'll still attack from the rear. Except now, we've been split up, and that would be exactly what he wants. So again he'll have the advantage. In any event, staying in the house is a bad idea. If he gets in, there's too much cover for him."

"So what do you propose we do?"

"The guy you had in mind to help out, he any good?"

"No idea."

"Well, we need to find out. Another gun would definitely shift the odds into our favor. Overwhelmingly. Because what we're going to have to do, Dean, is press him. We need to be outside, in a three-man front, sweep the area in a circular fashion around the house until we find him. Once we do, it's three against one. He won't stand a chance.

"What if he gets into the house?"

"He won't. We'll be spaced apart sufficiently early in the sweep so that can't happen."

Gerkowitcz rubbed his hands over his face and thought. He liked the idea, but he'd already seen four people get blown away, and he didn't particularly like the idea of being the fifth. For the first time since this bullshit started, we was beginning to wonder if he was going to come out of this alive and intact. He lowered his hands, gripped his rifle, then looked at Frank dead in the eyes. He wanted to gauge his response, wanted to ascertain his confidence level with enough precision to leave no doubt about his answer.

"What if the guy's just too good, Frank?" He said it in a low voice, carefully, enunciating each syllable.

"Nobody's that good." Frank shifted his gaze for a moment, then shifted it back to meet Gerkowitcz's stare. "I'm not going to lie to you, Dean: odds are, one of us will go down if we're not extremely careful. We need to find him before he finds us. And that's going to be a tall order. Because if he's still out there, then he's waiting and watching. But I really doubt he's doing that right now. I'm betting you're right and he's doubled back. He's going to come around and approach from the rear. And if he's going to do that without getting his head blown off, then he's going to give this place a wide berth, probably come around the way his buddy did. Gerkowitcz kept his mouth shut. After what had happened, just the sight of Frank made him sick. Trust him? Right. But he also knew Frank was his best hope of getting through this mess alive. Hell, if Frank got him through this, he might even let him live.

"Let's get downstairs, make sure this guy's gonna play. Given the circumstances, I really don't see how he can refuse. Once we get him onboard, we'll come back upstairs and plan out what we need to do and how we're gonna do it."

Gerkowitcz didn't want to plan. He wanted action. He wanted this whole mess to be over with. Now. He didn't see how planning was going to help much. Whether they survived could hinge on how quickly they got their asses in gear and pushed the assault onto the intruder. It was three against one, and that wasn't bad. Put his odds of survival right at two-thirds, all things being equal. But not all things were equal. He'd be careful, he'd keep his head down, and he'd survive. Tobias scanned the desert in front of him, then looked up and down the tree line bordering the access road, looking for Clark. As he passed the body of the man who'd killed Sanchez, he noticed his handgun. It had been kicked away, was half-buried in the gravel road about ten feet from where he lay. They were falling at a cadence that suggested a hurried walk. He took three quick steps to the rear of the vehicle, then crouched down and waited.

"Drop your gun!" He said it quietly, just loud enough for the man to hear him, but quiet enough not to give his position away to anyone else who might be nearby.

"Police officer. Drop your weapon, get down on your knees, and clasp your hands behind the back of your head. Do it now."

He walked up behind him, took his hands one-by-one, moved them down to the small of his back, and cuffed him. Then he said something that was simply a result of years of training, something that didn't make sense given the circumstances. But he couldn't help himself. Some things just happen automatically.

"Cuffs too tight?"

"No. They're fine."

Tobias grabbed the cuffs, then yanked on them lightly until the man slowly raised himself to his feet. Then he turned him around so they were facing.

"Who are you, and what are you doing here?"

The man shrugged, gave him a casual look that masked the stress he was undoubtedly feeling. "Just a guy out for a nature walk, officer."

"Don't lie to me, asshole. I know what's going on here. And so do a lot of other people. In fact, in about ten minutes, there's going to be an army of cops crawling around here, shooting first and taking names later. So it'd be in your best interest to cooperate and answer the damn question. If you don't, I'll just turn you loose and let them find you. Then you can see how far you get answering questions with a bullet in your head. Now, what's your name?" There was a condescending tone to Tobias's voice, an edge that got the man's full attention.

"Charlie."

"Okay, Charlie, what are you doing here?"

"I'm a customer," he stuttered.

Tobias pointed at the gravel road behind the man. "With a gun?"

"Yeah. They sent me out here to flush out some guy their scared of, said he'd already killed one of them."

"So you just took the gun and marched right on down the road, no fear you might be killed by the guy they're after?"

"Hey, man, they said they'd kill me if I didn't do it. It's not like I had a choice." He said it as if he'd experienced a whole lifetime of having to do things he didn't want to, as if he were a victim of actions he'd taken at the behest of others.

Tobias took a step back, considered what Charlie said at face value, in spite of the plastic delivery. He was old and thin. His approach was sloppy. He'd given up too easily. Not security. So he was probably telling the truth. But what to do with him? He couldn't put him in the car. He couldn't leave him outside.

But he could put him in the van.

Tobias grabbed Charlie's cuffs, started leading him toward the side door.

"Where're you taking me?"

"I'm going to lock you in the van so I don't have to worry about your sleazy old ass." He half-heartedly reprimanded himself for his response, but then realized he didn't really care about prisoner protocol at that moment. He was way beyond that.

As they walked, Tobias holstered his weapon, felt he didn't need it. Not with Charlie, anyway. When they got to the side door, Tobias stopped the old man, moved him to his left, then checked the handle on the door. It was unlocked. He pulled the it all the way out, releasing the latch, then slid it open.

Then he found himself looking at something he should've expected but was altogether unprepared for. A girl. On a mattress. Dead. That was clear. She'd asphyxiated on her own vomit. It had clogged all of her airways, pushed its way out of her mouth, and dribbled down the sides of her face. Another victim. A potential sex slave that had succumbed on the ride out. He shifted his gaze over the body, figured her to be  no more than eight years old, probably in the third grade. She most likely played with dolls, most likely had an unquestioning trust in adults. As with most children her age, she was missing a vigilance she shouldn't have been required to possess in the first place: she lacked a fundamental cynicism that resulted in her death.

He felt his face flush, felt pure rage once again course through his veins. He tried to calm himself by taking several deep breaths, but it didn't work. He could feel his heart thumping, beating against his ribcage like a basketball bouncing against a hardwood floor. The girl didn't have to die. Just as Sanchez didn't. But they both did. Why? Because of the sleaze-bag who was standing next to him. So his perverted sexual needs could met for a profit. That's why they died.

He felt himself turn to face Charlie. Somewhere deep in his mind, he knew the man wasn't the direct cause of the death that lay around him. But he was certainly a major factor. He felt his right hand ball up into a fist. Then he felt his shoulder lurch and his hand swing around to deliver a haymaker blow that landed flush against the diminutive man's face. The old man stumbled, fell to the ground, then coughed and spit out blood and a single tooth. His breathing was labored for about a minute, then he got it under control, looked up at Tobias and smiled. It was full of defiance, full of malice.

"Bad move, officer. Now I got you on police brutality. Only thing a jury's gonna believe now is you're trying to railroad me. I'm just an old man. A guy who got beat up by an over-aggressive cop. They're gonna have sympathy for me. They're gonna let me go. And you know what? I just lost a tooth and a little blood for it. You? You're gonna lose your job."

Suddenly, Tobias felt the world slow down. His heart rate seemed to return to normal. The rage was still there, but it felt comfortable, like a favorite garment. He looked at the old man. He was still talking, but he sounded far off, delivering his words in slow motion. Tobias wanted to tell him something, but his mouth wasn't cooperating, was moving too slowly for his thoughts. After what seemed like ages, he heard the words come out.

"You know what old man?"

Charlie's smile got wider, dripped with arrogance."No. But tell me. I'm just dying to know what you're gonna say."

Tobias returned the smile. "Yes. Yes, you are."

Between a thought and a corresponding action lies a layer of will, the thickness of which is governed by an uneasy alliance between rational thinking and un-tethered emotion. Tobias was beyond reason. All he could see was red. All he could feel was hate. All he wanted was revenge. His layer of will had diminished to a point that it only took a twitch, an inclination of an action he was thinking, to make it happen. It started with a subtle motion of his right hand, reaching toward his beltline. Toward the gun. It ended with a bullet in Charlie's head.

 

Charlie's body lay crumpled on the ground at his feet, a single, red dot marking the center of his forehead. He didn't know why he did it. But he didn't feel bad about it, either. Just like Sanchez had said, they weren't even human. They didn't even deserve to breath the same air. He knelt beside the man, grabbed his shirt, and tore off a piece. He wiped the handle and the trigger guard of the gun with it, walked it over to the guy who'd killed Sanchez,  knelt down, and wrapped the dead man's hands around the handle.

Then he stopped, suddenly aware he'd discharged a weapon, made a very loud noise that would most likely attract attention. He quickly made his way behind the van for cover, took out his handgun, and waited for the reaction. But it never came. No commotion. No shots. Nothing. He figured that could mean one of two things: either Clark had finished his business, one way or the other, or nobody had heard the shot. Or no one was interested. He got up, slowly walked around to the side of the vehicle, arms outstretched in front of him, gun leading the way. But there was nobody around. And no one was coming.

He lowered his weapon, looked into the open side door of the van. There was a blanket there. It was folded up and placed right next to the dead girl. He removed it, walked over to Sanchez, and used it to cover his body. He looked at his cell phone one more time. Still no signal, so he threw it into the passenger side of the cruiser. He looked toward the buildings in the distance, toward where all the action lay. It was his new destination. It was the place he had to go. It was where the kids were. And he had to do what he could to save them. That and make the bastards pay. And there was only one way to get there, too. The same way Clark was probably moving about. He had to take the tree line. He had to press himself into the ground. He had to get dirty and crawl on his belly. So he walked to the nearest cottonwood, then dropped to the earth. He pushed his body into the dirt, bathed himself in it until he could taste it, until he smelled like the desert. Then he started crawling.

He'd wanted to take two shots: one to eliminate the thin guy with the greasy hair, the other to keep the fat guy, the one he'd hoped was the boss, on the floor. That's how he figured he'd escape. No eyes looking his way, so he'd have the luxury of sprinting across an open field, back to his truck to resupply. But the Vinny had jammed. No second shot. He'd learned in the military that there were no sunny days in battle, that a healthy pessimism provided a good umbrella to protect against the rain of enemy fire. So what to do in that case? Drive in. Not around front. Through the field around the back. The way Tiny had gone. It was slow going, and it was rough for the big truck. After about an hour of creeping along the uneven desert terrain, Clark found he could go no further. He'd looped around the east side of every hill he ran into, only turning west when he felt his position was approximately in line with the buildings on the property. If he were to find himself confronted with an all-out frontal assault, he figured his best bet would be a non-linear dash to his truck, behind which he'd find some protection.

It's an effective strategy for operating against a larger force: he would strike and retreat, then repeat until the only guy left was the boss. But now he couldn't do that. His best guess was they'd brought Tobias to throw him off balance. They were banking on a reaction from him, a full retreat at the sight of their hostage.
