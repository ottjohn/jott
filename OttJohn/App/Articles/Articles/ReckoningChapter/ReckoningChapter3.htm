Chapter 3

 

 

Clark studied himself in the bathroom mirror of his cheap rental, one of the many he'd occupied over the past couple of years. Without exception, they were all motels, with weekly rates, anonymity virtually ensured. Walk in, pay cash, no questions asked. No credit checks, no id required, no hassle. But even when making temporary living arrangements, he had to be somewhat discriminating: he couldn't patronize any of the large, expensive chains that required identification, but neither could he stay at the very low-end establishments that attracted prostitutes and drug dealers. The former violated his need for anonymity by creating a paper trail; the latter by attracting the police. Somewhere in the middle, he'd found a class of weeklies that met his needs, and when he was not on the move and sleeping in his truck, these were the types of places he would stay to rest and plan.

You're a murderer now, he said to himself. But there was no feeling knit into the words, none of the self-loathing he'd feared might inflict him after the kill. And he was glad for it. Because while it was his first, it certainly wouldn't be his last. He continued regarding himself, looking for some external change in his demeanor, something that would scream out he'd ended the life of another human. Some barely-perceptible physical manifestation that would betray him. But there was nothing, only his reflection: a figure of a man, eyes obscured by shadow, facial features buried and blurred by darkness. He felt suddenly detached, as if viewing a stranger. The light filtering into the bathroom from the tiny living area illuminated him from behind. It made him look like a sinister angel, unsure about which direction to go, turning his back on the light and contemplating the darkness. The eyes that stared back at him betrayed an emptiness that burst within him, a hollowness that filled him completely. He looked away.

He'd been operating under what he'd considered a successful catch-and-release strategy: target, track, interrogate, move on. In each case, he'd learned enough about his targets to put together a face-to-face encounter. Most had been house calls, a few had been abductions. Then he'd press them for information. Some had no fight in them, gave up everything upon asking. Some had been more difficult. Some had to be tied up; some had to be terrorized; still others had to be beaten into talking. In the end, he got what he wanted: electronic, personal, and extra-personal information that filled in names, places, dates, and methods of operation. Upon completion of the interrogation, he would have in his possession information that might be of some use to him, information that might point him in a positive direction in his search. While that information could help him, he knew it was damning evidence against those he targeted.

That's because the information he collected created a neat package of evidence against every one of them, evidence that could put each of them back behind bars for a very long time if it somehow got into the hands of the authorities. And that's how Clark had left them, with a warning: Say a word about this, even hint it to the police, and the information I have collected here today is the first thing they'll see. They'll view me as a tragic figure: a father and husband who lost everything to an animal just like you. I'll get a slap on the hand. You, on the other hand, you'll be on your way back to prison. What will become of you then? Will you choose the protection of a very lonely existence in solitary confinement or the passionate devotion of a protective and horny lifer?

All of his targets had understood the message. Except the one in Atlanta. He'd decided to call the police, and Clark had to make a very fast exit from that city. He'd been very lucky to have his scanner on, hear the APB, and get out of town. If he'd been apprehended, his search would have been slowed for some time, if not stopped altogether. And that was unacceptable. He needed information. He pined for it. Every waking moment and all his dreams were filled with just one monomaniacal objective: to add to the growing collection of information he now had. Although he knew his odds were very long, he was looking for a vector: one piece of information that would put him on the right path toward the apprehension and elimination of the animal who took his family away from him. Finding that one man was the only thing on Clark's mind. It was his great white whale.

He looked again into the mirror for another few minutes, then turned away. It was a waste of time. He had a job to do: he had to process the electronic data in his possession, and he had to cross reference it with information he already had. With each new target processed, that job became more difficult. But with each new target, he also felt his path become more clear, began to point in a definite direction. All he needed now was a break. He filled a Styrofoam cup with the sludge from a distressed coffee maker that had seen better days, sat at the lone table in his single-room weekly, and got to work.

Tobias and Sanchez returned to the Hassenback house, found the driveway empty, and pulled into it. The scene appeared to have been mostly processed. Tobias figured the vic's presence in the system as a convicted sex offender had pushed the work along unusually quickly. Funny how that worked. One tech was outside finishing up. He was checking the seal on an evidence bag, finalizing its disposition for what might become a key piece in an evidentiary trail that could one day lead to the prosecution of whoever had disrupted the day-to-day existence of an otherwise calm California neighborhood. The cop who had been placed at the end of the driveway to control access to the scene had departed, the interest shown by curious neighbors had waned, and the bustle of investigators had diminished to almost nothing. All that was left was the bright yellow tape flapping in the Santa Ana wind and the tech, who was just leaving. The house beckoned, and because of the status of the vic, promised oversights Tobias knew he'd probably find. And he'd have to find them. Not because he cared at all for the late Mr. Hassenback, but because it was his job. He loved it, and he took pride in doing it right.

Tobias retrieved his PDA and stepped through the front door, followed closely by Sanchez. As he expected, he found the living room stripped down: no body, no techs, less evidence. The couch on which Hassenback expired bore the injuries of newly inflicted marks caused by swabbing, plucking, and wiping. It had been pushed against a set of closed, heavy curtains but was still facing the entertainment center, which no longer held the assortment of CD's and DVD's that had occupied its shelves only hours before. There was only a 27 inch television and a DVD player parked on pause. Tobias made a note in his PDA to check it later. He then moved directly behind the center of the couch.

"Okay, so the intruder came up from behind, right?"

"Would be the most probable route, yeah. Only the hallway could've provided a surreptitious approach," replied Sanchez.

"Nice word, kid! 'Sec. Let me write that one down." Tobias pantomimed writing the word on an invisible notepad in his hand as Sanchez smiled. Then he turned from the couch and walked toward the hallway behind it.

"Which room?"

"First one. Looks more like a study than a bedroom, probably a more secure place to lay in wait. Plus, one of the techs found a slight disturbance in the carpet just inside the room, a compression consistent with a heavy person standing in one spot for some time."

Tobias walked into the room, looked around, walked out. He walked down the rest of the hallway and into both bedrooms. Sanchez had been accurate in his description of the rear of the house: it had been turned. There could be evidence there, but he doubted it. The intruder was obviously gloved and had some kind of protective gear on. The initial forensics had borne that out. He decided to focus on places the intruder had meaningful contact: the kitchen, the study, and the spot directly behind the couch. He returned to the study, looked at the tag that was supposed to mark the slight depression in the carpet.

"Techs must have eyes like hawks." Tobias studied the wall in front of him, found the first oversight. "Hey, this wall's not marked. We need to make sure that's not an oversight. He could've been leaning on it for support and transferred something in the process. Chalk it. Chalk the wall around the closet too. He couldn't have just stood in the doorway the entire time."

As Sanchez made a note, Tobias keyed its redundancy into his PDA. He then exited the study and moved to the kitchen. He could see right away the tech there had been pretty busy: a square piece of linoleum had been cut away.

"What was all the excitement here?"

"Tech found a slight discoloration in the floor, maybe caused by a caustic solution of some kind. It looked new, so they cut out a piece."

Tobias continued to look around. For the most part, the techs had done a good job. Better than he'd expected. As he looked over the kitchen, he noted in his PDA that it, too, had been searched: plates were stacked on the counters, shelves emptied, even the refrigerator looked rummaged. He followed a line from the countertops to the fridge to the wall, and up to the ceiling. Even the covers for the vents had been removed. The thoroughness made him wonder what the guy was so desperate to find, but he figured he already had a good idea.

"Whoever this guy is, if nothing else, he's a meticulous bastard." Tobias commented, mostly to himself. Satisfied the kitchen and dining area had been processed sufficiently, Tobias turned and made his way back to the living area. He moved behind the couch and stood there. He thought about the pains the intruder took to search the house. Then he thought about the apparent lack of struggle with Hassenback. It was like the intruder had snuck up behind him and simply broke his neck. First thing. If his hunch was right about why the intruder had been there in the first place, though, why would he do that?

"This doesn't make sense."

"Unless it really was a robbery," offered Sanchez.

"Yeah, but nobody's going to kill for porn. They might kill because of it, though. Why do you think the intruder searched the house?"

"'Cause he wanted something?"

"What?"

"He took computer equipment, but it doesn't look like he took much else. We really don't know for sure, but maybe he was just after the hard drive."

"Right. So perhaps we can assume the intruder turned the house looking for more computer equipment, like spare hard drives, thumb drives, CD's, DVD's, etc. And why would he do that?"

"Because he was after what was on the media."

"And what do you suppose Hassenback kept on his storage devices?"

"But I thought we already decided the intruder wasn't after porn."

"Begins with an I."

Sanchez slumped his shoulders, let out an audible sigh, and rolled his eyes slightly. "Internet?" He asked, sharply.

"No. I-N. What do computers do, Sanchez?"

"Information."

"Yeah. Intriguing."

Sanchez looked puzzled. "What?"

"He took information. Which means Hassenback might not have any connection with the guy who killed him."

"Why's that?"

"He took information, Sanchez. That seems pretty clear. I think he's trying to track someone down. I don't think he's there yet."

Sanchez shook his head. "Sorry boss, but I don't follow."

Tobias spread his arms out at his sides as he looked at Sanchez. "If Hassenback had committed a sexual crime against one of the intruder's children, then killing him would have completed the guy's business. But that's not what happened. He searched the house after the murder. Why search the house after you killed the guy who hurt one of your kids?"

Sanchez thought about it for a moment. "Perhaps he was after porn after all, then? Perhaps he wanted to make certain Hassenback had no photos or videos of his child."

"Stop it, Sanchez! You're ruining my moment!"

"I do my best. But think about it: if his kid's picture's in there and he doesn't grab it, then we can work on identifying him through the images."

"Your point is well taken, kid, but I don't think it's correct. Could be, but I doubt it. The guy was rummaging through the house like he had all the time in the world. I mean, look at this place. If he didn't feel pressed at all, then why not just destroy the equipment here? He could've simply stuck what he found in the sink, broken it, set it on fire, whatever. So why take it? It appears he's been pretty smart about everything else he's done, so why would he make the mistake of absconding with equipment if all he's after is its destruction? Doesn't make sense." Tobias paused for a moment, then continued. "This guy's after information. Maybe on one of Hassenback's buddies. There's just one thing that's a little confusing to me, though."

Sanchez nodded. "No evidence of interrogation. Because if there had been an interrogation, then there would have been some kind of struggle. And there's nothing to indicate that. So if he was after information, why didn't he question Hassenback?"

"That's the sixty-four dollar question, kid. He should've. I can't believe he didn't want to. So something must've caused him to change his plan."

"Like what?"

"Don't know. Not sure if that it matters."

Tobias walked to the front of the hallway, from where the intruder must have been watching Hassenback. He looked at the back of the couch; he looked at the television. Then he went through the mental gymnastics one more time.

"If the intruder was able to sneak up and snap Hassenback's neck, he was certainly capable of sneaking up and subduing him for questioning."

"But something changed his mind," said Sanchez.

"Right." Tobias looked again at the television. He could see most of the screen from where he was standing. Then he remembered the player was still on pause.

"Hassenback was watching something."

"How do you know that?"

"Just a guess. The player's on pause. You got some gloves?"

"Yeah."

"Slap some on and press play."

Sanchez put on his rubber gloves, knelt next to the DVD-player and pressed play. As the images played out on the television, both officers recoiled, and for the first time in 15 years, Detective Paul Tobias got sick at a crime scene.
