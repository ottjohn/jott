Chapter 14

 

 

Tobias took out his PDA and opened the document that contained a copy of the marketing piece from the fictional San Andreas Bank. He smiled to himself as he saw four numbers that could clearly be put together to create an IP address. Massaging the digits into the text had been clever. But not clever enough. He looked over at Sanchez, who was focused on traffic. He had an uncharacteristically broad grin on his face.

"What?" Asked Tobias.

"You did it again." The grin grew into a twisted smile that curled up the left side of his face.

"Did what?"

"That just-one-more-thing-ma'am thing you do. I know you get it by watching Columbo. I bet you have a stash at your house and you take notes late at night. Am I right?"

"No."

"Yeah. I'm right. Admit it."

"No, I get it all from Carol. She's the one who taught me all about pregnant pauses and terminating key sentences with a sledge hammer. Without her, I wouldn't be nearly as effective as I am." Tobias laughed, thought to himself that he'd honestly found the Columbo shtick to be quite effective.

Sanchez drove past the zoo, then used his blinker to signify to other drivers he was about to merge onto the Santa Ana Freeway. Upon successfully inserting the unmarked into the sea of aluminum that flowed southwest in the general direction of L.A., he looked over at Tobias, hands gripping the wheel in a 10-2 position.

"So what brought about that last line of questioning? To my caveman mind, it appeared unrelated."

"It was. Honestly, I just wanted to know how she was recording suspicious activity. I figured she was putting it on the internet because of her use of the word post. I think what she's doing is great, and I hope she at least gets some recognition for it. If law enforcement agencies are going to adopt something like that, though, it's going to have to be reworked so the data can be centrally organized and accessed from anywhere. I'm sure she's aware of that, though."

"So that's what made you take off in a hurry?"

"Nope. It was the address to her website. It has no domain name, which means you can only get to it using an IP address. The address referenced in the bogus investment literature is the same way. No domain name, just an IP address."

Sanchez gripped the wheel a little harder, his knuckles starting to whiten slightly

"I didn't think there was a URL referenced in that document. Is there?"

"There is." Tobias said, a little smugness in his voice. He checked the intonation, then continued, "but it's not presented as one. It's wrapped up in the document, part of a code, like we talked about on the way out. Jana's URL begins with a 209, and I think the URL for the marketing site does too."

"Okay. That's it. I need to get back to school and take some general computer courses."

"Good idea, kid. Crime is moving more and more high tech, anyway. I heard they have a pretty good program in computer forensics at the community college in Glendale."

"Yeah, maybe I'll look that up." Sanchez paused, then continued, "so I'm assuming this is what you're going to be working on tonight, figuring out the IP address and the web site it's attached to?"

"Yep."

"Carol's going to be pissed." Sanchez elongated the vowel in the terminating word, shook his head in mock disapproval.

"So I'll compensate by letting her get some of those damned porcelain nymphos."

Sanchez chuckled. "I think you mean nymphs!"

"Yeah, that too."

"What do you want me to concentrate on?"

Tobias thought for a moment, noticed Sanchez looked haggard. The kid was tired, had been working his butt off.

"I want you to concentrate on going home, getting rest, and just being young."

"No can do, boss," He replied, relaxing a bit as he pulled into the parking lot of the station. "You work; I work. How ‘bout I follow up with Agent Rathman and see what he's turned up?"

"If you can catch him, that'd be helpful."

Sanchez parked and they both exited the vehicle. As Tobias started walking across the lot,  he realized he was missing something important. His car.

"Hey, kid!"

"Did we forget something?"

"Yeah." Tobias chuckled. "You picked me up at home, remember?"

"Shit." Sanchez laughed. "Just hop in my car, and I'll drop you. It's on my way, anyway. I keep forgetting I live right up the street from you."

"Hey, you should really consider that!" Tobias ducked into the passenger seat of Sanchez's late model Ford Mustang and buckled himself in.

"Yeah, right, boss. I'll do that right after I have my first million in the bank."

 

Tobias arrived home a little past four in the afternoon. As he approached the concrete path to his front door, he had the peculiar feeling he was being watched. He took a single step onto the path and then saw the reflection of two shimmering eyes glaring at him from below. He stopped, looked, and all at once saw a small troupe of six porcelain nymphs, all strategically placed around freshly-planted Agave and Baby's Breath, staring up at him. In spite of his negative banter about their cost earlier in the day, he had to admit he was impressed with the arrangement. Carol had a real gift, could probably even have her own cable show. He smiled to himself and continued to the top of the walkway, made a mental note to drag out his good camera and take some pictures to capture her work it its pristine, uncrushed, and un-pilfered state. Tobias reached the front door and stepped inside.

"Hello, Handsome!" Carol greeted him with a glass of Petit Syrah. "My husband's going to be away for the rest of the night; want to stick around and keep me company?" She handed the wine to Tobias, batting her eyes, giving him her most sultry look.

Tobias took the glass, raised it to his lips. The earthy, berry-tinged flavor bathed his tongue, interacting on a cultural-chemical level with his taste buds. He swallowed the silky liquid and it splashed happily into his waiting stomach.

As he drank in his wife's image, he couldn't help but think that after all these years, she still had it, could still fire him up with same sort of ease one might light an electric grill. She had gracefully moved past mid-life; in all honesty, much more gracefully than he had. She was the same weight as when they'd first met; still wore her hair in a cute, pulled-back ponytail, which showed only a little more grey now. Where she once had smooth, flawless skin, she now had crow's feet around her eyes and a hint of smile lines around her mouth. They were definitely not frown lines. Tobias considered her proposition. A little role playing sounded like a fabulous idea. It was something they'd engaged in regularly to maintain a sense of adventure in their relationship. But he knew he couldn't. The main mystery of the phony marketing letter having been resolved, he'd be able to think of nothing else. He knew she'd understand, but it took everything he had to decline her invitation anyway.

"Must be something really important," she said, wrapping her arms around his neck.

"Two hours. I promise. No more. Electronically speaking, I'm in hot pursuit. I need to look at some evidence, and then set up a strategy for tomorrow. Can you wait that long?" He wrapped his arms around her waist, gave her a peck on the lips.

"Sure. You bet! I'll just go back to the store and buy some more of those pretty little porcelain toys!"

Tobias moaned, gave her a mock frown, then gave her another kiss, this one lingering a little longer than the last. "I won't be long. I promise."

Tobias sat at the little nook in his kitchen facing his computer, PDA in hand, notes from the last several days scattered about haphazardly in front of him. He looked at a printed copy of the phony marketing literature recovered from Hassenback's thumb drive, decided his first order of business would be to figure out the correct way to string together the numbers he suspected formed an IP address. There were four numbers in all, which meant a total of 24 combinations, so he figured his first half hour would consist of nothing more than punching numbers into the location field of his browser and waiting for responses until he either found what he was looking for or simply came up empty-handed.

He entered his first guess, which was simply a concatenation of the numbers in the order in which they were presented: 209, 158, 85, 109. To his surprise, a page immediately started to load. He felt a slight rush of satisfaction, but it was tempered by the apprehension of what might show up on his screen. When the page finally loaded, though, he found it looked nothing like what he'd expected: instead of a page full of pre-adolescent pornography, it appeared professional looking, something one might expect to find when surfing to the corporate homepage of a small bank. It surprised him, but, then again, it really didn't.

He looked over the content of the main page, noticed it contained marketing script essentially the same as that in the literature on Hassenback's thumb drive. Its suggestive exposition about the age of the bank's funds and their associated in-house management required little interpretive work for those who knew the piece for what it was. Still, he was certain that unintended visitors to the site would have no idea about its underlying connotation, would have no idea the site was an electronic storefront for an organization specializing in pedaling the flesh of young children. He shook his head, then reached for his glass of wine and took a sip. The flavor bathed his tongue with an unaccustomed dullness, its essence losing much of its appeal against the stark, incomprehensible reality of the document glowing on his computer screen.

He had to admit the operation was smart, though: it steered clear of presenting grotesque imagery of children in various states of undress and sexual situations. Every single illicit site he had the displeasure of viewing in the past didn't do that, instead choosing to bathe their pages in an assortment of shocking filth that could only bring pleasure to those with severely aberrant psychologies. If they were as careful with the rest of their operation, he figured it would be difficult if not impossible to determine ownership in a timely fashion. He figured, too, if time was something he could put in a gas tank, he'd be running on fumes.

He spent another hour on the website, re-reading it several times to ensure he didn't miss anything important. Besides a very clever recruiting method that required new members to use the computer of an existing member to register, he found nothing. Almost every single feature of the site was non-functional, perhaps as a security measure against those who might beat the odds and accidentally stumble onto it, odds he put at roughly one in four billion. The only other aspect of the site that had caught his attention was a bulleted list at the bottom of the main page: the bullets themselves were thumbnails of children's toys. There were bears, pacifiers, blankets, dolls, monkeys, and baseball gloves. He understood the images were placed there to appeal to potential and existing clients, a marketing effort by the organization to communicate the age group in which it specialized. He looked closely at a particular thumbnail in the lower left-hand corner of the list. It was a pink, fluffy blanket that looked a lot like one he'd purchased for Sarah when she was about three years old.

 He felt his face flush, and his heart began to race. The thought of Sarah as a victim had occurred to him often enough. His job kept such thoughts in the foreground more than in his subconscious. But most of those thoughts had been associated with preventative measures and protective behavior. What if that had been her blanket? He wondered whose blanket it was, wondered if any or all of the images on the screen were trophies. He reached again for his wine, gulping this time instead of sipping. As he placed his glass back on the table, he wondered if Sanchez was right. Maybe there was no point to solving the Hassenback case. Maybe the bastard simply got what was coming to him.

He shook the offending thought from his mind, quickly concluded the website had nothing more to offer in the way of content. He then pulled up another page built and maintained by a company called Network Solutions. It contained search utilities he'd used in the past to gather information regarding website ownership and management. He found the site's tools were very useful for delivering two key pieces of information: the name of the company or person who registered the domain name for a website, and the ISP that managed the website's IP address. Because of the nature of the business, and because the site had no domain name, he held out little hope of finding anything useful.

Tobias clicked the whois link in the upper left-hand corner of the page, entered the IP address for the site, and clicked search. After a few seconds, the browser returned a record that listed a business called Ripened as the ISP for the site, which itself had a public-facing storefront at castlebound.cz. As he suspected, though, ownership information for the site itself was missing: no contact information, no phone numbers, no technical support. Too bad. The little hope he had for finding some kind of registrant information evaporated like a thin film of moisture on a hot slab of concrete. He shrugged, unfazed, decided it was a long shot anyway.

He clicked his browser's back button, returned to the whois search utility, then typed in the domain name for the ISP. This time he got what he was looking for: an address, phone numbers, and an email for technical and general information. He took a closer look at the address and noticed the organization simply listed a P.O. Box. That was the good news. The bad news was that the address was in the Czech Republic. He shook his head and cursed under his breath. Determining ownership just got a lot harder, if not impossible. Certainly, he could call the numbers listed, as he was confident they were legitimate. But he doubted the people on the other end of the line would be very forthcoming when confronted with questions about their involvement in hosting illicit material. And he could forget about the court system: no doubt, a subpoena from the Unites States was way out of its jurisdiction there. Tobias sat back, frustrated. What he had didn't amount to much, but he guessed it was better than nothing.

He spent the next hour thinking about three things: Hassenback's killer, Eduardo, and the website. For most of his adult life, he'd prepared for the worst and hoped for the best. In this particular case, however, he had no idea what was bad and what was good. At least with respect to Eduardo. He ruminated on the concrete, mixing in a little hypothetical: he was now in possession of a file that implicitly detailed the presence of a well-run organization that catered to the sexual desires of pedophiles. He had possession because Hassenback did, and Hassenback received the file from a guy named Eduardo. But Hassenback got himself killed by an unknown assailant, who also turned his house looking for information.

So would it be a good or a bad thing if the assailant also had the file? He figured that all depended on timing: if the assailant was indeed in possession of the file, then he also knew it originated from Eduardo. The assailant, though, had an advantage: he had Hassenback's hard drive, which probably contained email addresses, Eduardo's being one of them. If the assailant were in possession of the file, and he were actively looking for Eduardo, that could be a very good thing. If Tobias could find Eduardo first, that is. In that case, he could offer Eduardo protection in return for his assistance in laying a trap for the assailant. If he didn't find the guy in time, though, Eduardo would most likely be in a great deal of danger. Or worse. And that would be a bad thing.

But if he could find Eduardo first, he could provide an alternative and probably more promising method for finding the organization that owned and operated the website: a money trail. Certainly this was not something he could take on by himself, nor could the LAPD for that matter.  Working through international banking transactions was much more suited the talents of Agent Rathman and the FBI. In spite of the resources he tapped, there was no doubt Eduardo was the key person of interest in the investigation, and he needed to be located sooner rather than later.

Tobias leaned back in his chair, grabbed for the lonely glass of Pinot his wife had poured for him a little more than two hours before. He lifted it to his lips, and took the last sip. As he looked at the empty glass, he thought about that certain point in the evening, for him at least, when wine becomes counter-productive to almost every endeavor except meaningless conversation, such as religious and political discussions. Given his current rate of consumption, he figured his point of no return was right around 8 PM. He looked at the clock. It was seven. He had another hour, so he got up and poured himself another glass.

As he returned to his kitchen table, he again tossed around ideas about how to find Eduardo. He only had a first name, and that wasn't very useful. He figured if he had the time, he could interrogate the sexual offender community, but he knew that would probably amount to a lot of nothing. They were typically very tight-lipped, as dropping names on an offender who was steering well south of the law was essentially admitting to guilt by association. He could only think of one person who could give him the information he needed, and he was dead.

 Or was he? He might be dead, but he still had records, and those live on for quite some time after a person dies. Hassenback obviously had an internet connection; therefore, he had to have an ISP to whom he owed some kind of monthly payment. Tobias flipped open his PDA and typed in a note to remind himself to return to the Hassenback residence to look for an ISP bill or a bank statement that would show a record of payment to an ISP. Once he determined the ISP, he could subpoena its records, which might show him some level of detail about Hanssenback's internet activity. He could then leverage that to determine the mail service he was using, subpoena that company for its records, which might just yield Eduardo's email address. Once he had that, he could use it to find Eduardo.

Tobias got up from his chair and stretched, the soft white light in his kitchen bathing him in its hazy, happy glow. He'd worked a little longer than he'd promised, but he had some basic information uncovered, and he even had a strategy for finding Eduardo. The real leg-work could wait until morning as most of that would require judicial approval. He shut down his computer, cleaned his paperwork from the kitchen table, and grabbed his wine. Exhausted, he trudged robotically toward his bedroom, hoping along the way Carol wasn't too disappointed in him for once again allowing his professional life to disrupt their loving home.

Clark woke at two in the morning. He'd gotten about three hours of sleep, figured too that he must have been out pretty good. Because for a moment he was lost, thought he was back in his old house, in his own bed, next to his wife, his daughter asleep in the adjacent room. Then he remembered. It wasn't an unfamiliar sensation. And most of his nighttime memories were good. So he'd learned to appreciate them, to hold them close and take them for what they were: his only remaining connection to a demolished family. He got out of bed, stood and  stretched until his hands palmed the low ceiling above him. He held the position until he could feel his muscles loosen and the blood begin to flow to the oxygen-starved regions of his body. He lowered his arms, rolled his neck from side-to-side for a minute, then moved on to his first order of business: coffee. Strong Coffee. He prepared a batch using the brew Carri had purchased for him, thought again about the compassion she'd shown him, the support. Then he turned away from the contemplation to focus on the task at hand.

As the diminutive coffee maker chortled its way toward another pot of filmy sludge,

he ripped the sheets off the bed, then retrieved his briefcase and the bag of articles he'd purchased for the morning's engagement. He then sat on the mattress and slipped into his first layer of gear: a tight-fitting, full-body athletic ensemble designed for running protracted distances in cool, humid regions. It was tight and it was hot, but it was perfect for separating most of his body from the surrounding environment. After putting on a cheap pair of cotton socks, he retrieved duct tape from his briefcase, tearing off six eighteen-inch strips. He carefully employed the adhesive to seal off the openings in his first layer of clothing. He spent the next few minutes flexing and rotating his neck, wrists, and ankles, adjusting the tape with each movement until he was satisfied his body was sufficiently guarded against shedding contaminants. Satisfied he wasn't leaking, he tugged on his slacks, put on his shirt, then fashioned a passable Windsor knot for his blue, second-hand tie, which he decided to wear loosely for effect.

After putting on his boots and jacket, he hopped off the bed and moved to the coffee-maker, which had finally quieted. A tantalizing aroma filled the room, stood in stark contrast with the reality of the grainy, unpleasant flavor he knew would accompany the first sip. And every sip thereafter. The little appliance sat atop the dresser in steamy silence, seemingly exhausted from its work. He'd grown fond of it for some reason. Perhaps because it seemed to speak to him as it hacked and coughed out its brown phlegm. He'd stayed at a lot of weeklies, and each had been equipped with similar appliances. But this one truly seemed to put an effort into making really bad coffee. But he'd tolerated it. And along the way, he'd figured perhaps it wasn't the coffee that was so important. Perhaps it was the camaraderie. The commonality. They were both appliances, so to speak. And they were both focused on a single objective: to do their best to deliver their very worst.

He filled same Styrofoam cup he'd been using for several days, its customary white color long obliterated by splotchy brown stains. He took a sip, frowned, retuned the cup to the dresser, then moved back to the bed to retrieve a small bottle of coconut-scented body lotion from his open briefcase. He squeezed a copious amount into his right hand, rubbed it in to his hair and the exposed skin on his body. While he liked the smell of the lotion and understood it was good for protecting and even healing damaged skin, he didn't use it for that purpose. Lotion can be used as a micro-adhesive, so it can also be employed to prevent hair and dead skin from shedding and ultimately contaminating the places he visited. It wasn't perfect, but he figured it gave the crime scene techs a lot less to work with. As he finished rubbing the lotion into his hands, he took a look in the mirror to ensure nothing was obviously out of place. He then grabbed the rest of the gear he'd purchased the day before, picked up his coffee and his briefcase, and headed out the door.

Twenty minutes later, he was driving through the parking lot of the apartment complex. He continued past Eduardo's pimped-out Vega, took a quick glance at his unit. There appeared to be a dim light shining through the curtains of the window that faced the landing of the stairwell leading up to his door. A light in a window could mean a lot of things. It could mean Eduardo was awake. Or it could mean he had stepped out and left the light on as a security measure. Maybe the guy slept with the light on. As the window slipped out of his field of vision, he noticed it flickered and changed color. It was a television. And that meant Eduardo was probably up.

He drove to the south side of the complex, parked near an exit. He figured there was no sense worrying about whether Eduardo was home, asleep, or awake. It didn't really matter. The man had information he needed, and he was impatient for it. But he remained in his truck. Staring out at the sporadic traffic moving about in front of him. As he focused on the headlights moving from left to right, he began to wonder if he hadn't made a mistake, wondered if he shouldn't have conducted surveillance as he had with all of his former targets. Perhaps the one observation wasn't enough. Perhaps it was an invitation to trouble. Then he thought about Hassenback.

He suddenly felt a need to leave, to return to his weekly and re-think his strategy. He put the truck into reverse, then stepped on the brake. Things were different this time. Had the police been involved in any of his first operations, they would've been investigating mostly home invasion and assault complaints. Not something they'd get too worked up about. Especially given the victims. But this time was different. He'd killed Hassenback, and the police had more than likely made his murder a priority. And he had no idea where the police were in the Hassenback investigation. They could be right on his ass as far as he knew.

In a perfect world, he knew he should simply take the time to do the surveillance right. But the world's far from perfect, and he was excruciatingly close to his goal. And that's what mattered. He put the truck in park, convincing himself he had to deal with Eduardo immediately. He took a deep breath, opened his door, and stepped out in the warm morning air. He reached back into the passenger seat, grabbed his briefcase, opened it, retrieved his portable police scanner, turned it on, and diminished its volume slightly. As he locked his truck, he looked at his watch. It was 2:45 in the morning. He turned and faced Eduardo's apartment. The light continued to flicker from the curtained window. Eduardo was there. He could feel it. He took another deep breath, let it out slowly, shook off any remaining doubts, and headed for the stairs.

 

As he neared the stairs, Clark paused to glance at the names on the mail boxes at ground level. After reading five labels, he found E. Gutierrez, 233.

Eduardo Gutierrez.

He quietly climbed to the second floor, stopping just short of Eduardo's door, an old, peeling green thing that probably hadn't seen a paint brush in decades. Toward its top, and just about dead center, was an after-market spy glass he figured might give a fish-eye view of the landing. He took a half-step back to ensure he was out of its field of vision, then took in the front of the unit. There wasn't much to it. Besides the door, the apartment had a single exterior window that was mostly shuttered by drapes thin enough to betray the dim, flickering light he'd seen from his truck. He slowed his breathing, then spent the next couple of minutes listening for sound. Besides the faint mechanical laughter coming from a television, he heard nothing. Perhaps Eduardo wasn't home after all. Only one way to find out. As he stepped toward the door and prepared to knock, he heard the distinctive sound of a cough. It came from directly behind the exterior window. Eduardo was home.

And he was likely awake. Which meant he'd lost his early morning tactical advantage. He reassured himself it was still the best time to move, though, as most of Eduardo's neighbors would be fast asleep. That was good for one main reason: sleep-induced sluggishness muffled the sounds of struggle, which meant the likelihood of witnesses would be diminished significantly. And there would probably be some kind of struggle. Because if he lost his tactical advantage by not catching Eduardo in a deep sleep, he'd have to leverage fear. If it came to that. Clark twisted the knob on his police scanner to adjust the volume up and then forcefully rapped on the door three times, causing its old, brass knob to protest the unwelcome racket with a loose metallic vibration. His scanner squawked loudly about a possible 211 in Glendale. He figured it was too much noise, but he needed the radio racket to complete the illusion.

No response. He knew only seconds had passed, but it felt like minutes. As he raised his hand to rap on the door again, Eduardo answered, a partially-illuminated silhouette, caught up in the flickering light of a distant television. He was wearing oversized jeans, flip-flops, a red, loose-fitting tank top, and a string of Puca shells wrapped tightly around his neck. His hair was black, shoulder-length, very curly, and still swept back in a tight  pony tail. He had a lit cigarette in his right hand, his other positioned above his head, pressed against the inside of the door. He looked at Clark with brown, beady eyes, set close within a very narrow head.

"Yes, may I help you?" The first word was inflected, making it sound more like jes. As Eduardo waited for an answer, he let out an involuntary yawn, then took a hit of his cigarette.

"Mr. Gutierrez?"

"Yes." He returned his cigarette to his lips, took another quick puff. Clark's scanner squawked loudly in his left hand, catching Eduardo's attention. For the briefest of moments, Eduardo's eyes went from beady to wide then back to beady.

"I'm Detective Randolph with the LAPD. I need to ask you a few questions, sir." Clark flashed his three-dollar badge. Eduardo held his ground, but his eyes got a little wider. He took another drag of his cigarette, looked over Clark's shoulder and asked,

"What's this about?"

"Just a few routine questions regarding compliance with the terms of your parole. I promise this won't take long."

"I don't understand. You're not my parole officer. And why are you here at this hour?" He said it with his brows furrowed, his tone hushed so as not to disturb his sleeping neighbors.

"No, sir, I'm not your parole officer. I'm part of a taskforce working with parole enforcement. I apologize for the late hour, but we've had some incidents in this area over the past couple of weeks, and we've been working 24/7 checking things out. Now if you'll let me in the door, sir, I'll ask you the questions I have, and then I can be on my way."

Clark looked over Eduardo's shoulder into his apartment. As he suspected, the front of the unit contained a small, cramped living space. Toward the middle of the room, he could make out the left edge of an ugly orange couch set behind an even uglier plastic coffee table. Behind the couch was a small eating area built into a half wall that separated the kitchen from the living room. In front of the wall, pushed up against the eating area, were two plastic patio chairs. Dirty laundry was strewn about an olive-colored shag carpet. In the background, he could hear the television broadcasting Hispanic programming. The smell of cigarette smoke and stale beer wafted out of the door.

"I can answer your questions here, can't I?" Eduardo asked, apparently hoping to keep Clark out of his apartment.

"That suits me, Mr. Gutierrez." Then, more quietly, "If you really want to bother your neighbors in the middle of the night with a line of questioning that'll reveal your past activities and recent incarceration, I'd be more than happy to proceed right here."

As Eduardo made a show of contemplating his options, Clark took a moment to assess his own situation: he'd been at the door for just about two minutes, and no one had joined them. Further, he was certain the only sounds were coming from his scanner, his conversation with Eduardo, and the television. Which meant Eduardo was more than likely alone.

"Can I meet you somewhere? Like your office or something?"

Eduardo's eyes shifted; he released his words haltingly. It was obvious to Clark the man had something to hide. Which was good. Maybe it was something he could leverage.

"Well, sir, I'm here now and you're here now, so it really doesn't make much sense to reschedule, does it?" Clark's radio cackled loudly, this time squelching about a possible homicide in Santa Ana.

Eduardo's eyebrows bridged slightly, betraying panic. He seemed to be reaching for something, anything. He thought a little more, then said, "Dude. I wanna help you but I gotta go to work, man. My shift starts in like thirty minutes. If I take time for this now, I'll be late. You understand? I can't afford to lose my job."

It was a lie, broadcast by body language. Eduardo was facing away from him, almost at a ninety degree angle. He had to admit, though, it could have been a defensive posture. But eyes never lie. And Eduardo's  shifted up and to the right as he spoke, which told him the man was definitely inventing most of what was coming out of his mouth. He decided to take a calculated risk, confident he was right:

 "I can call your employer. If you filled out your application appropriately, he knows you're a felon, and he'll understand you'll have to do things like this from time-to-time."

Eduardo frowned, grasped at one last straw. "Why isn't Danny doing this?"

 "You're not his only parolee, sir, and this isn't his job anyway. The sooner we get inside, the sooner I can ask my questions. Let me in, and we can talk. We can get this over with, you can go back to what you were doing, and I can be on my way, okay?"

Clark had been standing at Eduardo's door for too long, perhaps a little more than four minutes. In spite of the hour, he figured the odds of someone seeing him were starting to grow, moving from doubtful to likely. And he couldn't have witnesses. He'd have to be in the apartment interrogating Eduardo in the next few seconds or abandon his current strategy in favor of abduction, which would delay his progress for at least another couple of days, something he couldn't afford to do. He took a small step toward the entry, so his left foot was planted a few inches from the bottom of the door. Eduardo noticed the subtle move, took another drag of his cigarette, and said,

"Wait here. I wanna call Danny, see if he ..."

Before Eduardo could finish his sentence, Clark secured the open door with his foot, covered Eduardo's mouth with his right hand, and placed his left hand on Eduardo's right shoulder, jamming his thumb into the man's armpit for leverage.
